Even though most ADH enzymes exist as tetramers, also dimers, like the ADH ﬁom Rhodococcus rubber DSM 44541 (Kosjek et al. 2004), T hermomicrobium roseum (Yoon et al. 2002), hexamers, e.g., ADH ﬁom Pyrococcus furiosus (Ma and Adams 1999), and recently even a dodecameric ADH were identiﬁed (Hess and Antranikian 2008). The overall fold does not seem to inﬂuence the general stability toward denaturat- ing conditions, e.g., temperature or organic solvents, because this parameter varied throughout the different quaternary structures without a clear trend.

TADH showed a broad substrate spectrum. The highest conversion rates were observed with primary alcohols and aldehydes. Besides those substrates, cyclohexane deriva- tives and especially 3-methylcyclohexanone were excellent substrates for TADH. Interestingly, this substrate speciﬁcity could be explained using the cubic-space section model developed by Jones et al., for horse liver ADH (Jones and Jakovac 1982). Overall, the substrate spectrum was similar to the speciﬁcity of TbADH, allowing the conversion of much bigger substrates than yeast ADH (Faber 2004). However, in contrast to TbADH, which has an inverted enantioselectivity for small substrates (Keinan et al. 1986), TADH was following exclusively Prelog’s rule (Prelog 1964), yielding the (S)-enantiomer in high enantiomeric excess. This selectivity, together with the broad substrate spectrum, makes TADH an interesting biocatalyst to be used in organic synthesis of chiral alcohols. In addition, TADH is very stable in the presence of detergents and denaturating agents. TADH activity doubled under mildly denaturating conditions in 10% (v/v) Triton-X100. Similar results were achieved with ﬁreﬂy luciferase, even though the reason for this is still unknown (Kricka and Deluca 1982; Savelli et al. 2000).

TADH was stable and retained its activity in organic solvents over a broad range of log PO/W values. Especially, hexane and octane were very well suited to be used as a second phase serving as a substrate reservoir and product sink for low-water-soluble substrates and products. However, in contrast to whole-cell biocatalysts, there is no general correlation between log PO/W of the second solvent and the activity (Filho et al. 2003). The water-miscible solvents 2- propanol and acetone can be applied in concentrations of up to 10% (v/v), reducing TADH activity down to 25% or 50%, respectively, but allowing applications of TADH in processes using substrate-coupled cofactor regeneration.

Another interesting feature of TADH is its high aldehyde dismutation activity (about 8 U mg_1), forming the corresponding carboxylic acids and alcohols. This reaction was found in several ADH, like horse liver ADH (Shearer

et al. 1993), ADH from yeast (Trivic et al. 1999), and in the ADH from Helicobacter pylori (Mee et al. 2005), but a synthetic application was not considered yet. The intercon- version of alcohols and aldehydes is reversible, whereas the oxidation of the aldehyde to the carboxylic acid is irreversible. Furthermore, the reduction of aldehydes and oxidation of alcohols is dependent on pH, because TADH exhibits different activity optima for these reactions. NADH accumulation occurs if no more aldehyde can be oxidized. The reaction cannot be completed without cofactor regen- eration unless stoichiometric amounts of NAD+ are present (Velonia and Smonou 2000). If acetone was added, the ratio of butyric acid to n-butanol favors butyric acid formation. At higher pH, an excess of butyric acid was formed during the same time period. Using this approach, the complete conversion to the acid is possible, which makes TADH the ﬁrst ADH applied for the production of carboxylic acids. Up to now, the biotechnical production of carboxylic acids is only possible using aldehyde dehydrogenases and supplies the respective cofactor. In an approach using the TADH dismutase activity, it is possible to produce the acid by dismutation, which is in itself a highly interesting reaction. It has to be noted that the initial dismutation until the consumption of the aldehyde without net change of the redox state occurs relatively fast. This is visible in Fig. 5, where at a pH of 9, the accumulation of NADH occurs almost immediately. However, to achieve complete conver- sion to the carboxylic acid, longer incubation and the application of a cofactor regeneration system were necessary.

In such a reaction, acetone could be used as sacriﬁcial substrate for substrate-coupled cofactor regeneration, which is an advantage over known aldehyde dehydrogenases that require an external cofactor regeneration system.

In conclusion, TADH was identiﬁed as a new biocatalytic tool for organic synthesis of chiral alcohols as well as carboxylic acids. The enzyme is NADH dependent, and the substrate spectrum complements the scope of previously described ADHs.
