Enzyme activity assays. The EgADH enzymatic activity was monitored in a spectrophotometer (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA) fol- lowing the NAD+ reduction at 340 nm. All buffers for determi- nation of activity were previously degassed with N2 for 10min immediately before the enzymatic assay. The assay reactions were carried out at 25 °C in buffer mix (50 mM imidazole, 10 mM each of acetic acid, MES, and Tris adjusted at pH 7.0 with HCl), 5 mM MgCl2, 1 mM NAD+, 20-40 mM cysteine and 1.3 :|: 0.2 ug puri- ﬁed EgADH; the reaction was started by adding 30 mM ethanol. Cysteine was included in the kinetic assay to trap the aldehyde product of ethanol oxidation; in its absence, the initial rate lasted only few seconds, making the calculation of the kinetic rates less reliable. In turn, the cysteine solution was prepared in the presence of DTT (which reached a ﬁnal concentration of 0.4 mM in the ki- netic assay) to preserve cysteine in its reduced state. For pH pro- ﬁle determination, buffer mix (pH 7.3) was adjusted with NaOH or HCl at pH values from 4.0-9.0 and the reaction was started by adding enzyme to avoid the effect of pH on protein stability dur- ing the duration of the kinetic experiment. Other buffers (50 mM bis-Tris propane-HCl pH 7.0—9.0; 100 mM sodium pyrophos- phate—phosphoric acid pH 8.8; 50 mM MOPS-NaOH pH 7.0; and 40 mM HEPES—NaOH pH 7.4) were also used, instead of buffer mix, to discard effects of buffer components. To determine the Km values, the NAD+ was varied from 0.05—5 mM (at 30 mM constant ethanol) and ethanol from l—40mM (at lmM constant NAD+) in buffer mix at pH 7.0. Isopropanol and isopentanol were used at 30 and 40 mM maximal concentrations.

RESULTS

Gene cloning. By screening a cDNA expression library of E. gracilis by enzyme activity using different oxidizable substrates, a clone was identiﬁed with a 1,000-bp cDNA insert containing the 754-bp 3’-end (nt 5 l0—l,264; Fig. 1) of a gene encoding a putative zinc-containing ADH. This fragment was cloned in an over-ex- pression vector and the recombinant protein puriﬁed; however, after exhaustive experimental procedures of puriﬁcation, the pep- tide did not display any ADH activity. partial cDNA sequence for a hypothetical E. gracilis ADH was also found in GenBank (accession number DQ384320; Watkins and Gray 2006). This sequence showed high similarity to the amino terminus of several ADHs and corresponded to nucleotides 126-861 of the sequence reported in this paper (Fig. 1). Appar- ently, the ADH activity exhibited by the E. coli colony isolated from the expression library screened by dehydrogenase activities was probable endogenous bacterial ADH.

Using the DNA of this clone as template and speciﬁc oligonu- cleotides, the partial coding region (663 nt) was ampliﬁed by PCR and the resulting fragment was used as a probe to re-screen the library by DNA hybridization. Using this strategy, a clone con- taining a 1,567-bp insert was identiﬁed, which contained a 1,038- bp ORF (nt 130—1,170; Fig. 1) coding for a putative E. gracilis Zn-containing ADH of 346 amino acids.

The clone also contained 129-bp of a 5’-untranslated region (UTR) sequence with putative translation initiation sites (i.e. ATG codons at nt positions 31, 43, and 76; Fig. 1); however, BLASTX analysis of this sequence showed no similarities to other ADHs or other proteins. Moreover, searches for a peptide transit signal in this region showed low probability (0.66). The cDNA insert also contained 397 bp of a 3’-UTR sequence (nt 1,171—1,567; Fig. 1) with no sequence similarities to other proteins.

Our sequence showed 100% identity in the overlapping trans- lated amino acid sequence with the sequence reported by Watkins and Gray (2006). Moreover, sequence alignments with the Taxo- nomically Broad Expressed Sequence Tags Database (TBestDB) from E. gracilis (http://tbestdb.bcm.umontreal.ca) provided by the Protist EST Program developed in several Universities of Canada and released on July 2006 displayed high percentage of identity ( >95%) at the nucleotide sequence level with clusters numbers ELL0000 4, 40, 229, 2125, 2324, 2474, 2489, 2494, 2604, and 6228.

Amino acid sequence analysis. NAD(P)+-dependent ADHs can be classiﬁed into three major superfamilies (Riveros-Rosas et al. 2003): ( 1) the Fe-dependent ADHs; (2) the short-chain (ap- proximately 250 amino acid residues) dehydrogenase/reductase family (SDR) and (3) the Zn-dependent or medium-chain ADHs (350-375 residues dehydrogenases/reductases; MDR). Based on this classiﬁcation, the 346-amino acid EgADH can be placed into the MDR superfamily.

The EgADH sequence showed low identities (30—36%) with MDRs from members of the Archaea, plants, and Metazoa whereas highest identities were with bacteria (54%) and fungi (46%). There was also low identity (31%) and similarity (48%) with the MDR from the kinetoplastids Leishmania and Trypano- soma. Alignment of the EgADH amino acid sequence with well- characterized Zn-containing ADHs from P. aeruginosa, E. coli, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, horse, and human (Fig. 2) showed 51%, 42%, 41%, 30%, and 27% identities and 67%, 60%, 57%, 46%, and 42% similarities, respectively.

The amino acid residues coordinating the two Zn atoms were totally conserved in the EgADH sequence: the residues binding the catalytic Zn as well as modulating its electrostatic environ- ment were (E. gracilis ADH numbering) Cys 38, His 61, and Cysl48, whereas Cys 92, 95, 98, and 106 were responsible for binding the structural Zn atom (Casadio et al. 2002; J omvall 1994; Sun and Plapp 1992). On the other hand, the Gly residues at po- sitions 60, 71, and 80 having structural roles in the protein were also conserved (Branden et al. 1975). EgADH also showed an Asp residue at position 196, which determines speciﬁcity for NAD+ in other ADHs (a Gly or Ala residue at this position is present in enzymes speciﬁc for NADP+; Sun and Plapp 1992). The Thr res- idue at position 40 (or Ser in other ADHs), which establishes a hydrogen bond with the substrate, was also conserved in the EgADH.
