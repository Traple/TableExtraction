Influence 0f the substrate structure 0n the kinetic behaviour ofZr/MIULADH

Different approaches have been used to correlate the sub- strate structure and the kinetic behaviour of LADH, resulting in different predictions concerning the suitability of an alcohol, aldehyde or ketone as a substrate [8-13, 18, 39, 40]. These models (diamond lattice, cubic space, computer- modelling studies) may be useful for predicting whether a sub- stance is a substrate of LADH or not, and may give some indication of the expected relative turnover. All these ap- proaches, however, remain qualitative and give no quantitat- ive details on the substrate-dependent differences in reactivity. Furthermore, they are not able to discriminate between bind- ing and activation contributions to substrate-speciﬁc reac- tivity. Detailed studies of the kinetic behaviour of LADH [2, 5, 41], YADH [5, 41, 42] and human LADH [7, 41] exist for alcohols, aldehydes and ketones, based on systematic vari- ation of the substrate structure, but in most cases these studies have been restricted to steady-state kinetics.

One consequence of the restriction on the steady-state kinetic approach is that until now the hydride-transfer step of

secondary-alcohol oxidations has been generally regarded as the rate-limiting step. It is apparent from our data that this applies at best to isopropanol. For all other secondary alcohols investigated so far, the rate constant of hydride trans- fer was found to be several times higher than kca, obtained from steady-state kinetics. The generalization of Dickinson and Dalziel [5], who ﬁrst published detailed kinetic data for these substrates, was based on the observation that k0,, for different secondary alcohols is lower than the dissociation rate constant of NADH. However, it is not possible to obtain all rate constants for an ordered bi-bi mechanism from steady- state kinetics alone. If the ternary-complex interconversion has to be taken into account, steady-state kinetics do not allow discrimination between the hydride-transfer step and the substrate-binding steps [5]. The selective determination of the hydride-transfer rate constants in combination with steady- state kinetics enables the discrimination of both steps. We could show that the rate-limiting step of secondary-alcohol oxidation by Zn(II)LADH is not the hydride-transfer step, but the dissociation of the resulting ketone in combination with NADH dissociation (Table 3). The explanation of these findings results from the unusually low dissociation rate con- stants of the ketones. Acyclic aliphatic ketones are very poor substrates of LADH, having very high Km values and, since the hydride-transfer step is rate limiting, very high Kd values (Tables 1 and 3).
