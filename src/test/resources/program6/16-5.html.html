Active site

The active site of the enzyme is centred around the catalytic zinc ion, and is situated in the cleft between the catalytic and cofactor—binding domains of each monomer. The A. pernix ADH was crystallised in the presence of the cofactor NADH, which is present at full occupancy in the current structure. The NADH moiety is bound by the protein predominantly through hydrogen bonding. A total of 14 hydrogen bonds occur between the cofactor and amino acid residues, as described in Table 2. In particular, Asp218 forms hydrogen bonds to the two hydroxyl groups of the adenosine ribose. This residue is equivalent to Asp223 of horse liver ADH, and thought to be a determinant of cofactor specificity. It is highly con- served in NAD(H)-dependent type I ADHs, but is replaced by glycine in NADP(H)—dependent enzymes of this class?“ One ionic bond is formed, between the NADH and Arg354. The adenosine ring is held between the side-chains of Val262 and Val219. The adenosine moiety of the cofactor also forms hydrogen bonds with eight water molecules.

No water molecules are bound to the nicotinamide component of the cofactor, which is situated within the hydrophobic region that surrounds the active site of the enzyme. Binding of the cofactor is very similar in the A. pernix enzyme to that of the horse liver ADH, except the residue His55, which inter- acts with atom AO1 of the cofactor and is replaced by an arginine residue in the horse liver enzyme.

On solution of the structure, additional elongated electron density was observed in the active site of the enzyme, showing the presence of a small molecule bound directly to the active-site zinc ion at full occupancy. The best description of this electron density was achieved by fitting a model of octanoic acid, coordinated to the zinc ion by its carbonyl oxygen atom (Figure 6). We have subsequently shown that octanoic acid is a good inhibitor of the A. pernix ADH (our unpublished results). Organisms are known to metabolise alcohols to aldehydes using ADHs, with the aldehyde subsequently metabolised to an acid. If this were to occur in A. pernix, fatty acids such as octanoic acid could be involved in feedback inhibition. Octanoic acid was not added at any point during purification or crystallisation of the enzyme; however, it is known to be naturally occurring in a range of biological systems. In Escherichia coli, the eight-carbon unit in lipoic acid is derived from octanoic acid.” In one of the two monomers of the asymmetric unit, the density extends beyond the eight-carbon chain of octanoic acid, although becoming weaker at this point, indicating the bound inhibitor to be a mixture of octanoic acid and longer chain fatty acids.

The inhibitor interacts with the protein through both hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic inter- actions. At the carbonyl end of the molecule, both oxygen atoms interact with the zinc and with neighbouring residues (OE1 to Thr56 and His79, OEZ to Asp168). The distance between OEZ and Asp168 is very short, at 2.1 A. The atom OE1 is positioned less than 2 A from the catalytic zinc, and interacts with both C4 and C5 of the nicotin- amide ring of NADH.
