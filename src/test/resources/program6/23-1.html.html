However, the side chain allows additional activation mechanisms, in particular the hydroxylation of the benzylic carbon atom and subsequent formation of a reactive sulphuric acid ester (Watabe et al., 1982; Surh and Miller, 1994; Glatt et al., 2003).

1-Methylpyrene is a compound that meets this concept. It can- not be activated to a vicinal dihydrodiol-epoxide due to the lack of a terminal benzo ring. Nevertheless, it is hepatocarcinogenic in newborn mice (Rice et al., 1987). The parent PAH, pyrene, is considered to be non-carcinogenic. We have demonstrated that 1-hydroxymethylpyrene (1-HMP) is a major metabolite of 1-methylpyrene in rat and human hepatic microsomal systems as well as in cell lines engineered for the expression of indi- vidual rat and human cytochromes P450 (CYPs) (Engst et al., 1999). 1-HMP is a potent mutagen in Salmonella typhimarium strains engineered for expression of various mammalian sulpho- transferases (SULTs), but is inactive in conventional strains (Glatt, 2000; Glatt et al., 2002). The same pattern of DNA adduct spots, determined by 32P-postlabelling, were detected in tissues of rats treated with 1-methylpyrene, 1-HMP or 1- sulphooxymethylpyrene (Glatt et al., 2003) indicating that this pathway is indeed important in vivo. Likewise benzylic adducts were detected in animals treated with 1,8-dimethylpyrene (C. Donath, H. Frank, A. Seidel, H.R. Glatt, manuscript in prepara- tion).

1-HMP administered to rats was primarily excreted in urine and faeces as free and conjugated carboxylic acids (Ma et al., 2002). This biotransforrnation eliminates the benzylic hydroxyl group, the prerequisite for the formation of a reactive ester, and therefore implies detoxiﬁcation. Since we suspected that the ﬁrst step of the oxidation of the benzylic alcohol may be mediated by alcohol dehydrogenases (ADHs) (Fig. 1), we administered a competing substrate, ethanol, or an inhibitor, 4-methylpyrazole (fomepizole) (Li and Theorell, 1969) together with 1-HMP to rats (Ma et al., 2002). Both treatments led to dramatic (up to 200- fold) increases in the levels of DNA adducts formed by 1-HMP. This interaction is particularly interesting, as a strong synergism has been observed between alcohol and tobacco smoking for cer- tain malignant tumours in humans, e. g. those of the oesophagus (Tuyns, 1983) and the larynidpharynx (Tuyns et al., 1988).

Genetic polymorphisms leading to amino acid exchanges have been reported for ADHlB, ADHlC, ADH2 and ADH4 (Duester et al., 1999; Buervenich et al., 2000; Stromberg et al., 2002). For the speciﬁcation of susceptibility factors, we are striving to identify the human ADH forms involved in the detox- iﬁcation of benzylic alcohols of PAHs. We succeeded expressing six forms in bacteria (Kollock et al., 2008); the remaining form, ADH5, was expressed in mammalian (Chinese hamster V79) cells, but failed to metabolise methylpyrene derivatives at signif- icant rates (R. Kollock and H.R. Glatt, unpublished result). The usage of both expression systems was complicated by the expres- sion of endogenous ADH activity towards substrates of interest. Thus, it is necessary for kinetic analyses to physically separate the cDNA-expressed human ADH from the endogenous activ- ity. In a preceding study we had partially puriﬁed human ADH2 expressed in S. typhimurium and demonstrated that this enzyme efﬁciently metabolises 1-HMP, 2-HMP and 4-HMP (Kollock et al., 2008). However, we also had found enhanced catalysis of the oxidation of 2-HMP by cytosolic fractions of bacteria expressing ADH3 and ADH4 bacteria compared to untransformed bacte- ria. Likewise, expression of ADH1C enhanced the conjugation of 1-HMP in the presence of bacterial cytosolic preparations. To follow up these ﬁndings, we have now separated ADHlC, ADH3 and ADH4 from the endogenous ADH enzymes of recombi- nant bacteria to study their catalytic activities towards benzylic alcohols of methylpyrenes and dimethylpyrenes. In addition, we studied the catalysis of the reverse reactions as well as interac- tions with ethanol and 4-methylpyrazole.
