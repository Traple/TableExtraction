A NAD-dependent, oxygen-labile alcohol dehydrogenase was puriﬁed from Desulfovibrio gigas. It was decameric, with subunits of M, 43,000. The best substrates were ethanol (Km, 0.15 mM) and 1-propanol (Km, 0.28 mM). N-terminal amino acid sequence analysis showed that the enzyme belongs to the same family of alcohol dehydrogenases as Zymomonas mobilis ADH2 and Bacillus methanolicus MDH.

Bacteria of the sulfate-reducing genus Desulfovibrio can use ethanol and a few longer primary alcohols as substrates (29). The alcohols are oxidized to the corresponding acids. Some Desulfovibrio strains grow slowly on methanol, which is oxidized to CO2 (15, 20). Alcohols are not only substrates for Desulfovibrio strains; a few strains were demonstrated to produce ethanol or butanol even during growth on lactate in the presence of sulfate (23, 24) or by ferrnentative break- down of fructose (7) and storage polyglucose (21).

Ethanol-grown cells of ﬁve Desulfovibrio strains including Desulfovibrio gigas contained high activities of NAD-depen- dent alcohol dehydrogenase and dye-linked aldehyde dehy- drogenase (11). A molybdenum-iron-sulfur protein from D. gigas exhibited aldehyde-oxidizing activity (25); data on a puriﬁed alcohol dehydrogenase from a Desulfovibrio strain are still lacking. Here, we describe the puriﬁcation and characterization of a NAD-linked alcohol dehydrogenase from D. gigas (DgADH) and show that it is an oxygen-labile, decameric enzyme.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Organism, cultivation, and preparation of cell extract. D. gigas NCIB 9332 was grown at 30°C in 20-liter cultures under N2 in a bicarbonate-buffered medium (19) that was supplemented with trace elements as given in reference 18, 100 nM sodium selenite, 100 nM sodium tungstate, and 0.02% (wt/vol) yeast extract (Difco Laboratories). After heat sterilization, a ﬁlter-sterilized vitamin solution (21), ethanol (to a concentration of 40 mM), and Na2S (to a concentration of 2 mM) were added and the pH was adjusted to 7.4. After 48 to 36 h, the cells were harvested by Pellicon cassette ﬁltration (HVLP 0.5-p.m Durapore ﬁlter; Millipore Corp.) and centrifugation (20 min at 8,000 X g and 4°C), washed three times with 50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0), suspended in 50 mM Tris (pH 8.0) containing 2 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), and stored under N2 at —20°C. Enzyme assays. Assay systems in cuvettes were made anaerobic by bubbling with N2 for at least 3 min and were then closed with grey butyl rubber stoppers. Enzyme-con- taining solutions were injected with a syringe (Hamilton). Activities were measured spectrophotometrically at 30°C by recording the formation or disappearance of NADH. The dehydrogenation assay mixture contained 50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 9.0), 10 mM ethanol, and 5 mM NAD; the assay mixture for the reverse reaction contained 50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 2 mM acetaldehyde, and 0.2 mM NADH. One unit of activity is the amount of enzyme that reduces or oxidizes 1 umol of NAD or NADH per min, respectively. Protein was determined by the method of Bradford (2), with bovine serum albumin as a standard.

Puriﬁcation of DgADH. It was reported that in cell extracts the alcohol dehydrogenase activity was not sensitive to oxygen (11). During initial attempts to purify DgADH under aerobic conditions, however, all or most of the activity was lost after a single chromatography step. The use of anaerobic techniques, including a glove box, throughout the puriﬁca- tion largely prevented a rapid loss of activity. DTT was also required for good activities. Active fractions were cooled on ice. Cell extract (50 ml; at 1.5 ml min“) was loaded onto a Q-Sepharose Fast Flow (Phannacia) column (27 by 2.6 cm; cooled to 10°C) and equilibrated with 20 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0) containing 2 mM DTT. The alcohol dehydrogenase was eluted after 73 ml of buffer. Fractions containing alcohol dehydrogenase activity were pooled (84 ml); NaCl was added to 80 ml of the pool to a concentration of 1 M.
