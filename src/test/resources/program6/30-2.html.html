Computational Details and Parameters

The deﬁnitions of the possible microscopic states that the titratable and polar groups can occupy are described elsewhere.12 The parameters used in the calculations are listed in Table I. The hydrogen atoms were built by means of the HBUILD routine of CHARMM.28 The positions of these hydrogens were taken as reference states of the titratable and polar groups. For carboxyl groups, the deprotonated forms were chosen as the reference states. The protonated forms of each of the carboxyl oxygens were created independently by using the same routine. Because the ionization behavior of the active site of DADH is of special interest, some additional changes of the standard

parameter set were made. Serine residues were considered as titratable groups with one deprotonated state and three protonated states, which correspond to the hydroxyl group rotamers that are stereochemically preferred. Similarly, three rotamer states of the NAD+ ribose hydroxyl group O2’ were considered. In the ﬁrst rotamer, which is also the reference state, the hydroxyl group is oriented so that the hydrogen points to Lys155N§. In the second state, the hydrogen lies on the line connecting the hydroxyl oxygens of the NAD+ ribose and Tyr151. In the third orientation, the hydrogen is in a position distant from both Lys155 e-amino group and Tyr151 hydroxyl group. In the crystal structure of the binary complex E.NAD+ of DADH, a water molecule (W144) was found hydrogen bonding to Ser138 and Tyr151. Four possible orientations were chosen for this water molecule, which was also treated as a titratable site forming a hydroxyl ion upon deprotonation. In all these orientations, the water molecule forms hydrogen bonds with Tyr151 and/or Ser138 hydroxyl groups. In the reference state orientation, the water molecule acts as proton acceptor in both hydrogen bonds. In the remaining states, the water molecule is either a proton donor to both or to just one of the above groups. The reference states and the possible positions of the hydrogens of the titratable and polar groups are given in Figure 1 (A).
