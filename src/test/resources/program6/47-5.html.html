Km and Vmax values of ADH I and also of ADH II for ethanol and acetaldehyde (Table 2), clearly show both enzymes favor the reduction of acetaldehyde to ethanol, but not the oxidation of ethanol to acetalde- hyde. However, since both ADH I and ADH II are induced 12 and 20 times, respectively, when the mutant strain was grown in the medium containing ethanol, where the ethanol concentration was reduced from 4% to 2% without producing acetic acid (unpublished data), they seem to work for ethanol assimilation. As for the ethanol oxidation, ADH I is more favorable than ADH II because ADH I has a lower Km value and higher Vmax for ethanol than ADH II. However, since the Km value of ADH II for acetaldehyde is very high, 24 mM, compared with that of ADH I, the reverse reaction is relatively unlikely to occur at low levels of acetaldehyde. Thus, ADH II may work for ethanol oxidation to acetalde- hyde even when acetaldehyde is accumulated at some level. This notion may be supported by the ﬁnding that Km values for NAD and NADH of ADH II, especially for NAD, are extremely low compared with those of ADH I. Thus, ADH II seems to be mainly involved in ethanol assimilation under the conditions that the NAD level is low and acetalde- hyde is relatively high, which may occur at the second stage when A. pasteurianus CN6-2 is grown on ethanol medium. In contrast, ADH I seem to work for ethanol assimilation at the initial stage of the cell growth in the presence of ethanol, where ethanol concentration of the cell is high enough but that of acetaldehyde is marginal.
