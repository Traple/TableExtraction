The substrate cleft

The inner part of the substrate cleft is formed by the zinc atom, its ligands, Phe-93, Ser-48 and the nicotinamide ring of NAD. The substrate cleft is, however, about 20 A long and the main part of the rest of the cleft is completely hydro- phobic with the side-chains of one Val, one lle, four Leu, two Phe and one Met lining the cleft. The outer frame of the cleft, close to the surface, contains polar residues. The cleft is much larger than necessary for a small substrate like ethanol or benzyl alcohol. The size of the cleft is approximately right for a retinol molecule which is a substrate for the enzyme (see Pocker et al., 1986). Leu-1 16 is in the middle of this cleft and different conformations of its side-chain have been observed. It changes the size of the cleft to adapt to different sizes of the substrate.

Human LADH

There is a divergent group of isoenzymes found in human livers (Strydom & Vallee, 1982). The isoenzymes are gener- ally divided into three classes, where the three isoenzymes, a, )6 and y, of the first class have high homology (about 90°/») to each other and to the crystallographically studied horse enzyme (Jiirnvall et al., 1987). The isoenzymes, at and x, of the two other classes are less similar to the first class and to each other. The sequence homology between the class 1 iso- enzymes and the isoenzymes of the other classes is about 65% (Kaiser et al., 1988).

The LADH isoenzymes have approximately the same degree of sequence homology as the serine proteinases in the pancreas. The latter group of enzymes differs in substrate specificity. Similarly, the different forms of alcohol de- hydrogenase found in human liver constitute a group with signiﬁcant differences in substrate specificity. l have model- built the homologous human LADH isoenzyme based on the horse LADH structure to locate the basis of these differ- ences.

y-and B ADH

The predominant isoenzymes in human livers are formed from the B- and y-subunits. The y-subunit is very similar to horse LADH from which the crystallographic investigations have been made. No residue closer than 8 A from the active- site zinc atom is different. There is one interesting substitu- tion to a smaller residue in the substrate pocket. Val instead of Leu in position 141 is probably the reason why this iso- enzyme form is active on some steroids.

The B-isoenzyme has a smaller inner part of the substrate pocket due to a Thr instead of Ser-48 in horse LADH and the y-isoenzyme. This results in a lower activity for second- ary alcohols such as cyclohexanol. The gene for the B- isoenzyme has been cloned and used as a base for mutagenesis experiments (J.-O. Hoéig, H. von Bahr-Lind- striim & H. Jiirnvall, personal communication). We have crystallized the B-isoenzyme for further studies along this line.

a -ADH

The a-isoenzyme is of fetal expression and has a few resi- dues close to the active site which are different from other isoenzymes. Primarily Phe-93, which forms the inner part of the substrate pocket, is Ala in the a-isoenzyme. There is, because of this, a quite different shape of the inner parts of the substrate pocket, and there is an additional cleft which could accommodate a molecule as large as a tyrosine ring. As a consequence, the substrate specificity for this isoenzyme is different. 1t should have a much higher affinity for secondary alcohols than the other isoenzymes.

Jr-ADH

There are a few gaps in the alignment of the Jt-ADH sequence with the horse LADH sequence, both located in one outer region of the substrate cleft. The structure of 2r- ADH is compatible with a structure similar to the horse enzyme. There are however several interesting differences at the active site. ar-ADH has a few of the hydrophobic residues at the active site substituted to Phe. The concentration of aromatic side-chains is comparable to some large hydro- phobic cores found internally in many protein structures. The substrate cleft is consequently smaller and more hydro- phobic than in the horse enzyme. One difference also occurs close to the internal part of the nicotinamide binding site. A Tyr occurs instead of Phe-93 which forms one wall of the inner part of the substrate cleft. The hydroxyl group of the Tyr side-chain naturally forms an additional hydrogen bond to the carboxyamide group of NAD. His-51 has been sug- gested to be important in the back-and-forth proton transfer between the active site and the surrounding solvent (Eklund et al., 1976). Mutagenesis of yeast ADH supports this (Plapp et aL, 1987). In ar-ADH the corresponding residue is a Ser which probably has effects on the activity of the enzyme.

x -ADH

x-ADH has several features similar to zr-ADH. It also has a Tyr at the position corresponding to Phe-93. A His is also absent in position 51. For x-ADH the corresponding resi- due is a Tyr. The substrate cleft is different from the other isoenzymes. It is more narrow in its inner parts, but wider at the outer parts of the molecule.
