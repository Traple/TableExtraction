Effect of various inhibitors and metal ions Various compounds were investigated for their effects on enzyme activity (Table 5). The enzyme was completely inhibited by 1 mM of CuClz (0%), NiCl2 (7%), BaCl2 (5%) and HgClz (0%). Other inorganic compounds, such as MgCl2, CaClz, MnCl2, and ZnClz had no inﬂuence on the activity. While the enzyme was completely inhibited by p-chloromercuriben- zoate, other thiol reagents, such as iodoacetate and N-ethyl- maleimide were not inhibitory toward the enzyme. Metal chelating reagents, such as 8-quinolinol (98%) and EDTA (99%), carbonyl reagent such as NaN3 (94%), serine inhibi- tor, e. g., phenylsufonyl ﬂuoride (85%) had no significant ef- fect on the enzyme.

DISCUSSION

We isolated PEDH from 2-phenylethanol-assimilating soil bacteria Brevibacterium sp. which was grown on the me- dium containing 2-phenylethanol as a sole source of carbon. PEDH oxidized 2-phenylethanol to phenylacetaldehyde, and utilized NAD+ but not NADP+ as the cofactor. Previously, we detected 2-phenylethanol oxidase activity in wall-mem- brane fraction (1). The present results sug- gest that the oxidative degradation of 2-phenylethanol via phenylacetaldehyde to phenylacetate is one of the metabolic pathways of 2-phenylethanol by Brevibacterium sp. PEDH plays a role, in part, in oxidation of 2-phenylethanol into phenylacetaldehyde in this strain (Fig. 1).

The substrate speciﬁcity of PEDH is different from that of NAD+-dependent aromatic alcohol dehydrogenase. Aryl alcohol dehydrogenase (EC 1.1.1.90) from Pseudomonas putida could catalyze the oxidation of benzyl alcohol but not 2-phenylethanol and alkanol (7). Benzyl alcohol dehy- drogenase from benzyl alcohol-assimilating T hauera sp. also could not oxidize alkanol (8). Although phenylacetaldehyde reductase from styrene-assimilating Corynebacterium strain, ST-10 reduced various aldehydes, it did not catalyze the re- verse reaction (9). The dehydrogenase from P. putida ap- pears as a homodimer composed of subunit of 42 kDa. ADHs from T hauera sp. and Corynebacterium sp. display a similar subunit size (40-42 kDa) and were found to be ho- motetramers. The active form of PEDH is a monomer, while the size (39 kDa) of PEDH is similar to those of ADHs listed above. The alcohol dehydrogenase superfamily can be divided into three groups: group I is zinc-dependent long chain ADHs (approximately 350 residues per subunit); group II is short chain zinc-independent ADHs (approximately 250 residues per subunit); group III is iron-activated ADHs (ap- proximately 385 residues per subunit) (10). From the results of inhibition studies, PEDH is zinc-independent alcohol de- hydrogenase containing approximately 350 residues per sub- unit. Thus PEDH dose not come into any groups. Further- more, the N-terminal amino acid sequence of PEDH showed no similarity to other reported dehydrogenase. Based on these results, it can be said that PEDH is a novel enzyme from the view point of these characteristic properties as well as its broad substrate speciﬁcity.
