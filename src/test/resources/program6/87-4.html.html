Within the class, variation in functionally important resi- dues is very small. Ten of the eleven residues critical for substrate binding are strictly conserved between alligator and chicken, but residues at the substrate binding site differ somewhat among the vertebrate enzymes of major class I type (Table 5). Especially, the piscine enzyme differs, which is compatible with its early origin, reflecting enzymogenesis from the original class III [1, 8]. Conservation of the coen- zyme-binding site is even stricter, and critical positions are identical between alligator, chicken, and human (Table 5). This contrasts sharply with an overall variability in the verte~ brate enzymes affecting close to half of all residues and an exchange of one out of five residues in the alligator enzyme with respect to even the closest relative (the avian form, Ta- ble 4). Hence, this particular conservation illustrates the im- portance of the functional residues and identifies the major alligator enzyme as a class I form.

The vertebrate enzymes of the other classes differ dis- tinctly (Table 5). This is compatible with their separate sub- strate specificities and active-site geometries [16]. Hence, the class I enzymes, with the exception of the cod form, with its partly hybrid properties [8], are distinct from those of the other classes, and these relationships have persisted subse- quent to the divergence of the vertebrate forms. This suggests that the class I enzyme has served a separate metabolic need.
