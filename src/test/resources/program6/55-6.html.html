Effect of organic solvents on TADH activity

A main limitation of ADH catalyzed reactions is the enzymatic availability of low-water soluble substrates in aqueous media (Orlich et al. 2000). Water-miscible solvents might act as solubilizers for substrates, increasing their availability, whereas nonmiscible solvents can be used as substrate reservoir and product sink to avoid substrate or product inhibition. Some water-soluble solvents also serve as substrates for TADH, which allows their use as sacrificial substrates for substrate-coupled cofactor regeneration. Thus, the efficiency of TADH as biocatalyst in organic/aqueous reaction media was elucidated by investigation of enzyme activity and its stability in organic solvents (Fig. 3). Solvents were categorized according to their partition coefficient (log PO/W), which represents their partitioning between octanol and water (Leo et al. 1971).

The exception was acetone, where 50% of the original conver- sion rate was determined, characterizing it as an interesting sacrificial substrate in a substrate-coupled cofactor regen- eration approach for TADH-catalyzed oxidation reactions. Regarding reductions catalyzed by TADH, 2-propanol could be used for substrate-coupled cofactor regeneration, as the activity in the presence of this solvent was still 20%.

The activity of TADH in the presence of water- immiscible solvents was higher with increasing log PO/W. For toluene and octanol, the remaining activity was around 40%. However, in hexane (log PO/W=3.90) and octane (log PO/W=5.l8), almost 80% of the conversion rate of TADH without solvent was measured. The addition of hexadecane (log PO/W of 8.25) reduced the rate to 35%.

Investigation of the organic solvents showed that water- miscible solvents like 2-propanol and acetone could be applied to TADH as sacrificial substrates in substrate- coupled cofactor regeneration approaches. For two-liquid phase setups, octane or hexane was identiﬁed as solvents of choice, as TADH activity was only slightly affected.
