The general applicability 0f the whole-cell system con- taining ADH “A” for preparative scale redox-reactions was recently proven (Stampfer et al., 2002, 2003a). The best substrates are (oa-l)-alcohols 0r (oo-1)-ketones. Non- terminal multiple bonds adjacent t0 the alcohol moiety enhanced the activity (Stampfer et al., 2003b).

The kinetic data plotted according to Lineweaver-Burk resulted in linear graphs intersecting on the left side of the ordinate and below the abscissa indicating that the enzyme follows a sequentially ordered mechanism such as ordered BiBi or the closely related Theorell-Chance mechanism (Bisswanger, 2000) which was described for a carbonyl re- ductase from Rhodococcus erythropolis (Zelinski and Kula, 1994) and an ADH from Thermoanaerobium brockii (Oestreicher et al., 1996; Al-Kassim and Tsai, 1990).

The enzyme contains a tightly bound zinc as described for medium-chain dehydrogenases (Li et al., 1999; Yang and Zhou, 2001; Tsigos et al., 1998; Jornvall and Hoog, 1995). The metal plays an important role in stabilizing the tertiary structure of the enzyme toward heat, extreme pH, and denaturation (Magonet et al., 1992).

From the molecular characteristics observed, we propose that the isolated ADH can be classified in the medium- chain family (J omvall et al., 1987; Danielsson et al., 1994).

By GC-analysis of product and co-product formation we have proven the existence of a so-called “coupled sub- strate’ ’ process, i.e., pure NAD(H)-dependent alcohol dehy- drogenase is able to transform the desired substrate and to regenerate the cofactor simultaneously. Additionally, the recycling of NADH during the reduction of 6-methyl-5- hepten-2-one was monitored spectroscopically in absence and presence of 2-propanol (10% and 25% v/v). Elevated cosubstrate concentration led to higher NADH concentration toward the end of the experiment (Fig. 6).

CONCLUSION

The chemotolerance of the isolated alcohol dehydrogenase toward elevated organic solvent/cosubstrate concentrations (up to 80% v/v 2-propanol, 50% v/v acetone) allows its use for redox reactions in both directions by simple choice of the appropriate cosubstrate.

To the best of our knowledge, no alcohol dehydrogen- ase from any other source tolerates such high acetone or 2-propanol concentrations and allows recycling of the co- factor in parallel to perform the desired preparative reaction.
