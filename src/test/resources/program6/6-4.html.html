Correlation between aggregation and secondary structure

Sometimes the induction of the non-native ﬁ-sheet seg- ments in proteins is known to cause aggregation [46]. To evaluate and understand the influence of secondary- structure changes on the aggregation of ADH, we performed far-UV CD experiments at various times during the incub- ation of ADH (0.3l mg/ml) at 50°C. The backbone con- formation study in Figure 4 reveals neither changes nor induction of the non-native ﬁ-sheet in the CD spectra. Hence, the results obtained herein clearly show that aggregation is not induced by the conformational changes in the backbone under the influence of temperature, which cannot be a key factor in the ADH aggregation.

Correlation between the charge effects and aggregation rate

Charge-charge repulsion can be effective in preventing the aggregation caused by heat stress in some proteins [47]. The binding of the electrolyte ions to the protein solvent- exposed charged functional groups reduces the charge effects on the protein surface and thus decreases the coulombic repulsion forces. For this reason, the aggregation of proteins in solution can be induced by the addition of electrolyte, which screens these repulsion interactions with the increase in the solution ionic strength. lt is worthwhile mentioning that hydrophobic interactions can be enhanced upon the increasing of the ionic strength, but they are negligible when compared with the effect on the decrease in the coulombic interactions. The electrolytes demonstrate a dominant effect on the protein ionic stabilizing forces as mentioned previously [48]. For the ADH aggregation process, no changes are observed upon NaCl addition in the concentration range 0-400 mM (results not shown). Therefore it seems that the effect of the net charge is not the main factor in the ADH aggregation process under the experimental conditions (50 mM phosphate buffer, pH 7.5). Since the net charge is zero at the pl, the predicted elec- trostatic contribution to the protein stability can be negligible at that pH value [49]. Here, because the pl ofADH is 6.8 [50], its net charge at pH 7.5 is nearly zero and the charge effects have little influence under these experimental conditions. lt seems that, in the native state at this pH, the polar side-chain residues are the major factor in this solution. These ﬁndings agree with the results of a previous study, which was conducted by the DSC (differential scanning calorimetry) technique [40]. In this technique, no effect of the ionic strength on the thermostability of ADH was observed (i.e. the electrostatic interactions were not a dominant factor in the ADH thermostability).

Correlation between hydrophobic effects and aggregation rate

The intermolecular interactions between denatured or partially denatured species are believed to be responsible for the aggregation and deactivation of many proteins in vitro [I2]. Consequently, we have investigated the signiﬁcant role of hydrophobic interactions on the ADH aggregation. The oz-CyD molecule has an internal hydrophobic cavity and is hydrophilic on the external surface [I5]. Primarily, we evalu- ated the effect of oe-CyD on the extent of ADH aggregation at 50°C. As evidenced from Figures 5 and 6, oe-CyD reduces the ADH aggregation by almost 60%. This is probably due to the dynamic hydrophobic interactions between the exposed hydrophobic regions of the conformationally changed subunits under higher temperatures and within the hydrophobic cavity of oe-CyD, precluding inter-subunit interactions of the enzyme.
