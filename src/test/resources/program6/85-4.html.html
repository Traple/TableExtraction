Yet in contrast to similar spectral changes occurring after coenzyme binding, a difference of at least 18 nm (Table 2) is observed between the modiﬁed human B161 and the horse liver EE enzyme ternary complexes with NADH and DACA. It is not possible to decide on the basis of our data whether the different changes are due to changes of the protein conformation or to different binding of DACA, but a different kinetic behaviour with this substrate must be expected. Differences in the binding of substrates or inhibitors in ternary complexes with coenzyme and liver alcohol dehydrogenases having threonine or serine at position 48 have recently been found by computer-graphics studies [24].

Besides the cobalt(II) d-d bands the spectral properties of the chromophoric substrate DACA are sensitive to changes in the microenvironment of the active site in different alcohol dehydrogenases (Table 3). This is reﬂected by the absorption maxima of DACA bound to zinc [or cobalt(II)] of the 51,61 isozyme, which appear at 458 (469) nm compared to 464 (478) nm for DACA bound to zinc [or cobalt(II)] of the horse liver enzyme. Interestingly the absorption maximum of DACA bound to native ylyl isozyme - (NADH); is 464 nm like that of DACA in the horse liver complex (Table 3).

It is well known that the position of the absorption maximum of DACA is dependent on the polarity of the sol- vent [25]. In enzyme-bound DACA the magnitude of the red shift can be taken as a measure of the strength of the net electrostatic ﬁeld in the active site [26]. A more polar environ- ment causes a larger red shift. The B1 subunit of the human enzyme differs from the y, subunit and from horse liver E subunit in having threonine instead of serine at position 48 [8]. This amino acid is located close to the active-site metal

atom and its methyl group may affect the polarity of the active site. The red shift of 11 nm (Table 3) induced in the cobalt(II)- bound DACA as compared to DACA bound to the native B131 isozyme may be due to a stronger polarization of the carbonyl group in DACA by cobalt(II) than by zinc. It indi- cates that this substrate is directly coordinated to the metal atom as in horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase.

In the case of horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase the high degree of structural similarity between the zinc and the active site cobalt(II)-substituted derivative is shown by X-ray crys- tallography [4]. This is important for the relevance of the kinetic data obtained with the cobalt(II) enzyme. The spectro- scopic properties of Co(c)2Zn(n)2 B151 human alcohol de- hydrogenase allow the conclusion that this enzyme derivative fulﬁlls the criterium of high structural similarity too; it prom- ises to be a valuable system for the elucidation of the catalytic mechanism of human liver alcohol dehydrogenase. On the other hand, the differences observed for different isozymes in the reactivity of their metal-binding sites as well as in the binding of substrates will certainly become a valuable source of information for revealing structure-function relationships of mammalian alcohol dehydrogenases.
