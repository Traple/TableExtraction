3. Results

3.1. Partial puriﬁcation of human ADHs expressed in bacteria

All human ADH forms studied were eluted from the anion- exchange column by the low-salt eluent close to the ﬂow-through front. No ADH activity was detected in these fractions when cytosol from untransformed bacteria was used. Elution of the endogenous ADH activity required high salt concentration. Thus all separations were straightforward, as previously reported in detail for ADH2 (Kollock et al., 2008). However, the width of the human ADH peak differed somewhat between the individual forms, which — together with the expression level — affected the purity of the preparations obtained (Table 2). The purities of the preparations used varied from 20% to >90%. The activities are expressed in nmol product per mg ADH protein per min.

3.2. Eﬂect of DMS O on catalytic activities of human ADHs

Since alcohols and aldehydes of alkylpyrenes are very poorly soluble in aqueous media, they have to be delivered in a water-miscible solvent such as DMSO. Moreover, when the con- centration of these substrates in the delivering solvent was too high, they precipitated at the addition site in the incubation mix- ture. Therefore, relatively high concentrations of DMSO were required, unless the substrate concentration was kept low. For this reason, we studied the inﬂuence of DMSO on the activities of human ADHs using substrates that pose no solubility problems (ethanol, l-octanol and acetaldehyde) or only require a low level of delivering solvent (octanal). Data for ADH2 were reported in our preceding study (Kollock et al., 2008). DMSO inhib- ited ADH2-catalysed oxidation in an uncompetitive mode and reduction in a mixed mode. The same pattern of effects was seen with ADH1C (Fig. 2, upper row). However, this enzyme was more sensitive to DMSO than ADH2 in particular for the reverse reaction (reduction of the aldehyde) as reﬂected in the inhibi- tion constants (Table 3). ADH4-catalysed oxidation reactions (Fig. 2, left plot in middle row) were also inhibited uncompeti- tively by DMSO with a K value (1.0%) similar to those observed with ADH1C (1.3%) and ADH2 (2.5%). However, reduction

reactions with ADH4 were inhibited in a non-competitive mode (Fig. 2, right plot in middle row), as opposed to a mixed mode found with ADH1C and ADH2. ADH3, unlike the other forms, tolerated very high concentrations of DMSO (Fig. 2, lower row).

From these results it is obvious that DMSO is not an ideal substrate-delivering solvent for ADH-catalysed reactions. However, many other water-miscible solvents are alcohols or carbonyl compounds or are also known to inhibit ADHs [e.g. dimethylformamide (Sharkawi, 1979)]. Despite these problems, we decided for DMSO for the following reasons: (a) in general, lipophilic polycyclic aromatic compounds are better soluble in DMSO than in other possible delivering solvents; (b) we were primarily interested in ADH-mediated oxidation reactions. Since these reactions were inhibited by DMSO in an uncom- petitive mode, VmaX/Km is unaffected by the inhibitor. This kinetic parameter, also termed catalytic efﬁciency, is decisive for describing reactions at low substrate concentrations, i.e. in exposure situations realistic for humans.

3.3. Oxidation and redaction of alkylpyrene derivatives by human ADHs

All ten alkylpyrene alcohols and aldehydes studied were substrates for each ADH form investigated. Reaction rates of the dimethylpyrene derivatives as a function of the substrate concentrations are exemplarily presented in Fig. 3. Catalytic constants for all substrates and enzymes are compiled in Table 4. Km values for the alkylpyrene derivatives (measured in the presence of 2—10% DMSO) ranged over a wide range, from 0.035 ptM (1-F-8-MP with ADH4) to 106 uM (2-HMP with ADH3). The same was true for Vmax [1.48 nmol/(min mg) for 1- F-8-MP and 3500 nmol/(min mg) for 2-HMP, both with ADH4] as well as VmaX/Km [1.3 ml/(min mg) for 1-F-6-MP with ADH3 and 1042 ml/(min mg) for 1-F-8-MP with ADH2].

The catalytic efﬁciencies for the reduction of alkylpyrene aldehydes were usually somewhat higher than for the oxidation of the corresponding alcohol by the same ADH form. In most cases the differences were small (within a factor of 2). The most pronounced exceptions were the reduction of 1-F-8-MP medi- ated by ADH2 and of l-FP mediated by ADH4, which were kinetically favoured over the corresponding oxidation reactions by factors of 12 and 22, respectively. The true differences might even be larger due to the different types of inhibition effected by the solvent DMSO (Section 3.2).
