The best characterised catalytic-metal-ion-substituted species of LADH is Co(II)LADH. Besides its spectroscopic relevance, the catalytic metal ion substitution can also be seen as a chemical mutation of the enzyme which offers further insight into the catalytic mechanism of LADH. It could be shown that metal ions of different charge densities maintain their different polarizing effects when bound at the catalytic center of the enzyme. The absorption maximum of the chromophoric 4- trans-(N ,N-dimethylamino)cinnamaldehyde, complexed with LADH and NADH, was shown to be an indicator of the Lewis-acid strength of a particular catalytic metal ion [48, 49]. The metal-ion-dependent shift of the ab- sorption maximum correlates with the kinetic properties of the LADH derivatives, which are also influenced by the polarising effect of the metal ion. Comparison of Tables 3 and 4 shows systematic differences between Zn(II)LADH and Co(II)LADH concerning substrate binding, which can be summarized in two generally valid statements.

(a) For all alcohols, Co(II)LADH has a lower equilibrium dissociation constant, and for all ketones a higher equilibrium dissociation constant, compared to Zn(II)LADH.
