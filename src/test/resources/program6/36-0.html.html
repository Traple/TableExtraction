Abstract: The purification and characterization of an organic solvent tolerant, NADH-dependent medium-chain secon- dary alcohol dehydrogenase (denoted sec-ADH “A”) from Rhodococcus ruber DSM 44541 is reported. The enzyme can withstand elevated concentrations of organic solvents, such as acetone (up to 50% v/v) and 2-propanol (up to 80% v/v). Thus, it is ideally suited for the preparative-scale enantioselective oxidation of sec-alcohol and the asymmet- ric reduction of ketones, using acetone and 2-propanol, respectively, as cosubstrates for cofactor-regeneration via a coupled-substrate approach. The homodimeric protein was found to bear tightly bound zinc and displayed a molecular mass of 38 kDa per subunit as determined by SDS gel electrophoresis. The optimal temperature ranged from 30—50°C and the half-life at 50°C was 35 h. In addition, excellent storage stability was found. The pH optimum for reduction is pH 6.5; pH 9.0 is preferred for oxidation. The enzyme followed a sequential reaction mechanism. The substrates are medium chain sec-alcohols or (to-H-ketones; primary alcohols or aldehydes are not accepted. © 2004 Wiley Periodicals, Inc.

INTRODUCTION

The majority of asymmetric bioreductions of ketones using isolated enzymes is based on the use of two independent en- zymes, i.e., an alcohol dehydrogenase showing broad sub- strate tolerance for the conversion of the actual substrate, and a substrate-specific dehydrogenase for cofactor-regeneration (usually NADH or NADPH) (Hummel, 1997; Faber, 2000; Nakamura and Matsuda, 2002; Devaux-Basseguy et al., 1997). Only recently, a sec-alcohol dehydrogenase from Rhodococcus erythropolis has been overexpressed which can be used efficiently in combination with formate dehydrogen- ase for cofactor regeneration in a ‘ ‘two-enzyme approach’ ’

For practical reasons, however, any system based on a single dehydrogenase—simultaneously catalyzing the desired substrate-conversion plus cofactor regeneration—would be preferable in view of facilitated handling and operational costs. The prerequisite for a successful coupled-substrate process is the availability of a dehydrogenase, which can withstand elevated concentra- tions of cosubstrate (a sacrificial alcohol or a carbonyl compound) which is required to shift the equilibrium toward reduction or oxidation-products, respectively.

Various attempts to reduce ketones using a coupled substrate-approach were reported (Hildebrandt et al., 2001; Itoh et al., 2002; Matsuyama et al., 2001; Schubert et al., 2002; Wolberg et al., 2000); however, limited concentra- tions of cosubstrate (maximum of 25% 2-propanol) and substrate (10-50 mM) prevented successful applications on a preparative-scale. Reports on the reverse reaction, namely, the oxidation of sec-alcohols employing isolated enzymes, are rare. For instance, horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase was studied in the presence of aldehydes or cyclohexanone at rather low (co)substrate concentrations (Grunwald et al., 1986, Gorrebeeck et al., 1991, Weenen et al., 1995). The applicability of alcohol dehydrogenases from Thermoanae- robium brockii (Korkhin et al., 1998; Bogin et al., 1997), T. ethanolicus (Holt et al., 2000), or Lactobacillus brevis (Hummel, 1997; Schubert et al., 2002; Wolberg et al., 2000) is limited due to their requirement for NADPH (instead of NADH) as cofactor. In order to design a simple and ﬂexible redox-system applicable to the preparative-scale oxidation of alcohols and the reduction of ketones using a single enzyme (Scheme 1), we initiated a search for an NADH- dependent sec-alcohol dehydrogenase which is stable to elevated substrate (minimum 100 mM) and cosubstrate concentrations. High stability in the presence of elevated acetone concentration is of special interest since acetone is reported to form a dead-end complex with the ADH and NAD(H) (Winberg and McKinley-McKee, 1988).
