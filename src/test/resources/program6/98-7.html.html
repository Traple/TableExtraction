Recombinant Putative ADH-2. To validate the results ob- tained in this study, adh-2 (8800764) gene was cloned and overexpressed into E. coli, since both the preliminary results obtained from transcription and translational level studies suggested that this putative ADH enzyme played a major and consistent role in ethanol metabolism. The 1.1 kb gene was successfully amplified and inserted into several E. coli host cell types (TOP10) and overexpressed (in BL21 Star (DE3) strain). The His-tagged puriﬁed recombinant enzyme ADH-2 (8800764), consisting of 333 amino acid residues and resulted in a protein of approximately 36 kDa, as was deduced from the protein sequence. This protein band, visualized with 1D-SD8-PAGE (refer to Figure A in Supporting Information), was further veriﬁed to a high confidence level by peptide sequencing (with

An activity assay of the recombinant His-tagged ADH-2 enzyme using benzyl alcohol as the substrate resulted in a specific activity of 0.12 U/ mg, being 4 times higher than that of the crude extract (see Table 2). The enzyme was further purified via heat treatment and yielded a specific activity of 0.18 U/ mg. Moreover, using benzyl alcohol as substrate, this enzyme exhibited an activity that increased with temperature up to 95 °C. At 80 °C, the enzyme fraction gave a speciﬁc activity of 0.75 U/ mg (4 times higher than at 65 °C), whereas at 95 °C a specific activity of 1.05 U/ mg (6 times higher than at 65 °C) was obtained. All these enzymatic assays were carried out in the presence of 2 mM of zinc sulfate, since this enzyme was annotated as putative zinc-dependent alcohol dehydroge- nase.“ Moreover, studies reported that the presence of zinc ions were necessary to increase a NADJF-dependent ADH from S. solfataricus strain MT-4, and the same was also observed in ADH-10 purified from S. solfataricus P2.5'7»41

The Michaelis constants (KM) of the purified enzyme toward various types of alcohol (including ethanol, iso-propanol, and n-propanol) were determined here, since our previous work reported the ability of S. solfataricus to utilize these cosub- strates, in the presence of glucose.” At 65 °C, the KM value of ADH-2 activity toward iso-propanol was found to be the highest (at 2.40 mM :l: 0.45), followed by ethanol (1.33 mM :l: 0.25), then benzyl alcohol (0.44 mM :l: 0.02), and n-propanol (0.19 mM :l: 0.02). The KM values demonstrate that this enzyme has a higher affinity toward n-propanol compared to benzyl alco- hol, ethanol, or iso-propanol and a higher afﬁnity toward benzyl alcohol rather than ethanol, as was also reported for ADH-10.7

By comparing this heat treated and His-tag purified protein to the previously characterized ADH-10 (8802536), enzyme activity was found to be significantly lower, despite a 30% identity between the amino acid sequences (see Figure B in Supporting Information). Specifically, the activity of ADH-10 ranged from 3.4 to 10.1 U/ mg (depending on the number of purification steps employed), obtained through a series of purification steps using both heat treatment and gel filtration chromatography? The lower enzyme activity obtained in this study may be due to the presence of a variety of inhibitory factors. Thus, more purification steps will be the subject of future work, in order to increase the activity of this recombinant enzyme, since this scenario was reported in ADH-10 (8802536) purification.5*7»32 It may be also possible that the His-tag attached to this enzyme may interfere with its activity, although it was reported that His-tag had no inﬂuence on the activity of 4-(ﬁ-D-ribofuranosyl)aminobenzene 5’-phosphate (RFA-P) syn- thase recombinant enzyme of Methanococcus jannaschii, but this cannot be overlooked.” It would be prudent to further look into fully characterizing and to explore the enantioselectivity potential of this putative ADH for commercial applications.42—45

Conclusions

S. solfataricus was able to survive in ethanol either in the presence or absence of glucose. However, the presence of ethanol in both cases inhibited the growth. The highest ethanol consumption rate was found to be approximately 15.1 mg L-l hr-l from cultures grown solely on 0.79% w/v ethanol. The ethanol uptake and utilization was further proven by the identiﬁcation of duplex mass spectra from in vivo metabolically labeled cultures supplied with either 12C or 13C universally labeled ethanol.
