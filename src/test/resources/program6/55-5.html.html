TADH converted cyclic ketones very well; the highest reduction rate was measured for of 3-methylcylcohexanone (163% as compared to cyclohexanone), while activities decreased for cyclohexanone derivatives with a methyl- group in 2- or in 4-position. Even compounds with large substituents at the ring were accepted as substrates by TADH. Double bonds in the ring decreased activity (e.g., 3- methyl-2-cyclohexenone or (—)-carvone). A relatively high conversion rate was detected for the double-ring structure of 2-decalone (28% as compared to cyclohexanone).

TADH exhibits a broad substrate spectrum, which includes aldehydes, aliphatic ketones, cyclic ketones, and double-ring systems. Activities were highest for primary alcohols and aldehydes, but also for secondary alcohols signiﬁcant rates were achieved. Higher speciﬁc rates were observed for the conversion of cyclic ketones.

Enantioselectivity

Reductions of prochiral ketones were performed using a substrate-coupled cofactor regeneration approach with 10% 2-propanol as the sacriﬁcial substrate. The enzyme showed high preference for the (S)-enantiomer yielding the corresponding (S) alcohols in optical purities of greater than 99% enantiomeric excess (Table 4). From racemic 3- methylcyclohexanone, only the (S)-3 -methylcyclohexanone was converted by TADH in a stereoselective (S)-alcohol- forming reduction yielding (lS,3S)-3 -methylcyclohexanol (as shown by nuclear magnetic resonance analysis; Hollmann et al. 2005). The activity toward the (R)-enantiomer is roughly 80 times lower (Table 3). Thus, a kinetic resolution of the substrate as well as a chiral reduction of the other enantiomer led to the formation of two interesting products in one step. The diastereomeric excess of the reaction was 98.4%.

As exempliﬁed in Table 4, asymmetric reduction of ketones led to chiral alcohols in high enantiomeric excess, which makes TADH a useful biocatalyst in chiral organic synthesis.

Effect of metals, inhibitors, and other reagents on TADH activity

The effect of metals, inhibitors, reducing agents, detergents, and denaturants on TADH was investigated (Table 5). All tested metal ions did not inﬂuence the enzymatic activity. Whereas the iron-chelating compound 2,2’-bipyridyl did not have any impact on the biocatalyst, the chelator of divalent ions ethylenediamine tetraacetic acid (EDTA) reduced activity drastically (loss of more than 97% of its activity).
