The concentrations of inhibitor were 0 (I), 20 (I), 50 (A) and 200 (o) umol kg_1.

During the steady-state metabolism of ethanol, the concentrations of NAD+ and NADH are ~05 mM and 2 uM, respectively and these concentra- tions are fixed in the simulations. Acetaldehyde is rapidly metabolized and its concentration is main- tained at zero. Previous simulations of ethanol metabolism indicate that the activity of alcohol dehydrogenase is the major controlling factor in the disappearance of ethanol, whereas the activity of aldehyde dehydrogenase controls the steady- state level of acetaldehyde [51]. Since the rate of ethanol disappearance appears to increase at longer times, a step is included for the elimination of the inhibitor. The rate constant for elimination

numerical integration of the differential equations that describe the mechanism. Rate constants (in convenient units) are set according to values estimated for the ADH reaction mechanism, as determined by transient kinetics experiments or approximated according to steady-state kinetic constants [40,51,54]. As it turns out, alcohol dehydrogenase is typically saturated with NAD+ and ethanol under the usual conditions of alcohol metabolism and the only sensitive rate constant is the rate-limiting step in the mechanism, the release of the product NADH, k+4. Thus, this step can be set to the turnover number of the enzyme. Because the maximum rate of the reaction is the product of the enzyme concentration and the turnover num- ber (V=kCat[E]), the concentration of enzyme is then set to a value that will simulate the elimina- tion of ethanol as determined in the absence of inhibitor. Although most of the ADH is in the liver, the concentration is calculated according to the total body mass because the rate of elimination of ethanol is calculated as moles per kilogram animal per minute.
