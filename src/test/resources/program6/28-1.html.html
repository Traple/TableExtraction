Regions around the active site zinc ligands are highly conserved and all residues implicated in the function are conserved in E. coli ADH and yADH. Besides, high degree of amino acid homology (41%) was observed for yADH and tetrameric ADH I from Pseudomonas aeruginosa [20].

Bhattacharyya et al. [21] have identiﬁed a hydrophobic region in the yADH molecule that binds to the hydrophobic probe bis-ANS (4,4’- dianilino-1,1’-binaphtyl-5,5’-disulfonic acid) and is mostly present in a random coil conformation. This region includes -YSGVCHTDL- HAWHGDWPLPVK [40-60]- sequence. The peptide corresponding to residues 40-60 reveals chaperone-like activity by preventing dithio- threitol-induced aggregation of insulin at 27 °C, oxidation-induced aggregation of "y-crystallin at 37 °C and aggregation of thermally dena- tured yADH and BL-crystallin at 52 °C. In spite of the presence of the 40-60 region, aggregation of yADH has been observed when it is sub- jected to heat treatment at 52 °C [21]. However, analysis of the precipitate showed that only about 20-25% of the denaturing yADH form large aggregates and precipitate. After heat denaturation at 48 °C a signiﬁcant portion (70-75%) remained in solution. The data presented by Fu et al. [22] also indicate that there exists a small amount of tetrameric form of yADH able to retain that size when heated at 50 °C for up to 50 min.

It has been proposed that interactions involving residues 40-60 in one of the yADH subunits with other regions of the same subunit, or with another subunit, may be responsible for the formation of soluble aggregates [21]. Residues 40-60 in yADH may function as an intra- molecular chaperone and prevent complete unfolding of yADH during thermal stress. The B-sheet-forming residues 41-45 in yADH, which have a propensity to interact with the other B-sheet-forming sequences 68-71 and 369-374 in native protein, may be interacting with other unfolding regions of the denaturing protein, resulting in the suppression of aggregate formation. The sequence of the active ADH- peptide, -YSGVCHTDLHAWHGDWPLPVK-, is found only in yADH, and a BLAST search analysis showed that several amino acid residues in this region are also highly conserved within the family of dehydrogenases.

In the present work we have studied thermal aggregation of yADH by dynamic light scattering (DLS) at a ﬁxed temperature (56 °C) and under the regime wherein temperature was elevated at a constant rate. Unfolding of yADH, when being heated, was controlled by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC). The character of the depen- dence of the protein aggregate hydrodynamic radius on time appeared to be typical of systems containing a chaperone. The chaperone-like activity of yADH was demonstrated using a test system based on aggregation of UV-irradiated BL-crystallin at 37 °C.

2. Materials and methods 2.1. Yeast alcohol dehydrogenase I

Yeast alcohol dehydrogenase I from Saccharomyces cerevisiae was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. yADH concentration was determined spectrophotometrically at 280 nm, using the extinction coefﬁcient A“ of 12.6 [23]. Purity of the enzyme was analyzed by SDS-PAAG electrophoresis performed according to Laemmli [24]. Enzyme activity of yADH was determined in pyrophosphate buffer, pH 9.2, at 25 °C by the method of Hayes and Velick [25], whereby the rate of absorbance at 340 nm resulting from reduction of NAD was measured. One enzymatic activity unit reduces 1 mmol of NAD per min at 25 °C under the speciﬁed conditions and the activity was expressed as mol of NAD produced per s/mol of yADH.

All solutions for DSC and DLS experiments were prepared using deionized water obtained with Easy-Pure II RF system (Barnstead).

2.2. Puriﬁcation and UV-irradiation of BL-crystallin

Puriﬁcation of BL-crystallin from lenses of 2-year-old steers was performed according to the procedure described earlier [26]. Concentration of BL-crystallin was calculated using molar extinction coefﬁcient 2.3 cmz mg” [27].

UV-irradiation of BL-crystallin (1 mg/ ml) was carried out in 1.0 cm path quartz cell. The volume of the sample was 2 ml. This volume allowed us to reach the temperature of 8 i 2 °C for 10 min prior to beginning UV-irradiation. UV-irradiation was applied by means of a 1000 W high pressure Hg lamp (DRSH-1000, Russia) with 15 cm water ﬁlter and 282 nm interference ﬁlter with a half-width of 15 nm. The duration of irradiation was 60 min. The

UV output incident upon the sample cell under these conditions was 0.3 W/cmz as measured with a Spectra-Physics 407A Power Meter. The temperature of the samples in the cell was monitored with miniature thermometer and did not exceed 10 °C.

2.3. Calorimetric studies

Thermal denaturation of yADH was studied by DSC. The experiments were performed on a DASM-4M differential scanning microcalorimeter (Institute for Biological Instru- mentation, Pushchino, Russia) as described earlier [26,28]. yADH solutions were heated at various constant rates (0.5, 1.0 and 2.0 K/min) from 5 to 80 °C. The reversibility of the thermal transition of yADH was tested by checking the reproducibility of the calorimetric trace in a second heating of sample immediately after cooling. The temperature dependence of the excess heat capacity was further analyzed and plotted using Origin software (MicroCal Inc.).

The kinetics of thermal aggregation of yADH were studied by DLS at 56 °C in 50 mM sodium phosphate buffer, pH 7.4, 100 mM NaCl. The buffer was placed in a cylindrical cell with the diameter of 6.3 mm and preincubated for 10 min at 56 °C. Cells with stopper were used for incubation at high temperature to avoid evaporation. 2.5. Modelling of a three-dimensional structure of yADH

A three-dimensional structure of yADH subunit was constructed with automated program EasyPred (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/biotools/EasyPred/). ADH from P aeruginosa (1LLU) was used as a template. BLAST program (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool; http://www.bbc.co.uk/blast/; http://blast.wustl.edu/) shows 41% homology between yADH and ADH from P. aeruginosa.
