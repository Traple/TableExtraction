3. Results and discusion

In general, the activity and stability of enzymes depend on the nature of the solvent and the water content. Enzymes can show increased thermal sta- bility in organic media. This is an attractive bonus to their use, especially in relation to synthetic reactions.

The objective of the present investigation was to identify the inﬂuence of organic solvents of different physico-chemical characteristics such as hydrophobicity and denaturation capacity. In this connection, two related alcohol dehydrogenases, TBADH and YADH, were studied. The ﬁrst en- zyme is a well-characterised thermophilic protein with broad substrate speciﬁcity [15,16] and the second is a mesophilic enzyme with narrow sub- strate speciﬁcity [17]. The observed differences in

3.1. Thermal stability in organic solvents

As pointed out by Klibanov [7], water has a dual effect on an enzyme: it is essential for maintenance of native conformation [18] and is also involved in the mechanisms of inactivation processes [19]. In the present study, thermal sta- bility studies were performed by incubating the powder form of the enzymes obtained from lyophilization (see Section 2), in n-octane and n-dodecane, pyridine and toluene. As indicated in Fig. 1, substantial stabilization was achieved in three of the non-polar organic solvents in both cases. However, pyridine, a hydrophilic solvent (logP= 0.71), was found to have a detrimental effect on the stability of both proteins, especially on YADH. An explanation for this effect of pyridine may be that this solvent has the ability to interact with the enzyme by stripping water from these proteins and by competing with the non-co- valent interactions, especially at elevated tempera- tures.
