Here, we sought to understand the behavior of S. solfataricus P2 when exposed to ethanol as the sole carbon-source. This study employed iTRAQ facilitated proteomics, gas chromatog- raphy mass spectrometry (GC-MS), and in Uil/O metabolic labeling to assess microbial physiology, metabolite quantiﬁca- tion, and substrate uptake under various ethanol concentra- tions. Furthermore, transcription levels were determined using quantitative RT-PCR on selected adh genes to allow for comparison between mRNA and protein levels. One of these putative adh genes (adh-2) was then cloned and overexpressed in Escherichia coli to assess enzymatic functionality and activity.

Materials and Methods

Growth Conditions. S. solfataricus was aerobically grown in a pH 4.0 medium at 8O °C, in a horizontal gently shaking water bath, as detailed previously. 1323 Control cultures supplemented with (i) 0.40% w/v glucose (positive) or (ii) no carbon source (negative) were prepared, whereas experimental cultures were grown in various ethanol concentrations ranging from 0.08 to 3.97% w/v. To ensure ethanol adaptation was achieved, the experimental cultures were repeatedly inoculated (at least 12 doubling times) in (glucose-free) ethanol medium. All the cultures were also grown in volumetric ﬂasks, providing a natural reﬂux effect that minimized the ethanol evaporation. Both the control and experimental cultures were set up with identical inocula, having an initial optical density (OD530) of 0.20. To correlate OD530 with cell number, cell counting was also carried out using a hemeocytometer (Marienfield, Ger- many) under a microscope (Axiostar Plus, Zeiss, USA). The growth behavior of these cultures was monitored by measuring their optical density at 530 nm using an Ultraspec 2100 Pro (Biochrom, England) spectrophotometer. All chemicals were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Gillingham, Dorset, UK) unless otherwise stated.

Stable Isotope Metabolic Labeling. S. solfataricus was grown separately in 0.79% w/ v 12C and 13C universally labeled ethanol. For this particular study, we only focused on 0.79% w/v ethanol cultures, because the highest ethanol consumption rate was observed at this concentration (see Results and Discussion section). Both cultures were harvested at the mid-exponential phase by centrifugation at 5000>< g for 15 min at room temperature (22 °C). The cell pellet was then resuspended in B-PER Bacterial Protein Extraction Reagent (Pierce Biotech- nology, Rockford, USA) and soluble proteins extracted accord- ing to manufacturer’s protocol. Total protein concentration was subsequently measured using the RC DC Protein Quantification Assay (Bio-Rad, Hertfordshire, UK) according to the manufac- turer’s protocol. Protein extracted from both 12C and 13C ethanol cultures (10 [lg each) were then mixed in a 1:1 ratio prior to protein separation via 1D-SDS-PAGE (on 7 cm >< 7 cm >< 1 mm gels, with a 4% stacking gel on top of the 12% resolving gel). A broad range protein molecular weight marker (10 — 225 kDa) (Promega, Southampton, UK) was used as a molecular weight indicator. The gel band was then trypsin-digested for mass spectrometric based peptide identification, as detailed previ- ously.13

In the metabolic labeling experiment, the light version of the peptide (12C) can be identified by submitting the information dependent acquisition (IDA) tandem mass spectrometry data to the Mascot database search engine (version 1.6b10; Matrix Science, UK; http://www.matrixscience.com/home.html, ac- cessed in September 2006). The adjustable search parameters were set to a MS and MS/MS tolerance of 1.2 and 0.6 Da, respectively. Only a single miss-cleavage of trypsin was allowed. A carbamidomethyl modification of cysteine and methionine oxidation was set as the fixed modification and variable modification, respectively. The search was performed against the Mass Spectrometry protein sequence DataBase (MSDB, NCBI, NIH, Maryland; ftp: / / ftp.ncbi.nih.gov/ repository/ MSDB/ msdb.nam, downloaded in September 2006). Only proteins identified with molecular weight search (MOWSE) scores greater than 50 were considered significant.1°

For each peptide identiﬁcation, duplex spectra were observed in the mass spectrum, contributed by the light and heavy versions of the carbon isotope. The carbon composition of the peptide identified (based on 12C) was calculated using a composition calculator (http: / / db.systemsbiology.net:8080/ pro- teomicsToolkit/, accessed in September 2006). Manual inspec- tion was carried out to identify the chromatographic shifts between the heavy and light versions of the same peptide. The mass spectrum and peptide fragmentation patterns generated from the heavier 13C peptides were also inspected. Peptide areas were then calculated using the LC-MS reconstruction tool within the Analyst Qs software package (Service pack 8; Applied Biosystems).
