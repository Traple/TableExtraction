DISCUSSION

The major liver alligator enzyme: a class I alcohol dehydrogenase

The properties of the alligator enzyme are typical of class I as is apparent from the multiplicity of sub-forms (Fig. 1), relative activities toward ethanol and other substrates, the kinetic parameters particularly toward the latter (Table 2) and the structural relationships (Tables 4 and 5).

The alligator enzyme is the first reptilian alcohol dehy- drogenase to be characterized and turns out to be useful in the recognition of the structural properties of this whole en- zyme family. It establishes that class I alcohol dehydroge- nase, although characteristically more variable than several other dehydrogenases [17], has undergone a fairly constant rate of evolutionary changes in different branches of the ver- tebrate radiation. Pairwise residue differences between diver- gent lines are presently found at close to 10% of the posi- tions/100 million years. This fact, and the conservation of the functionally important residues (Table 5), suggests a constant metabolic function, common to most vertebrates, for the ma- jor liver form of alcohol dehydrogenase.

Segment architecture of class I alcohol dehydrogenase

The present data, with alcohol dehydrogenase class I structures known from different major vertebrate lines, allow an overall evaluation of the enzyme architecture (Fig. 4). Two features are noticeable and unique. They concern both the constancy and the variability of residue distributions in what will be referred to as ’constant’ (C) and ’variable’ (V) segments in different sections of the protein chain. Two seg-

ments, C1 and C2, are conspicuous owing to their extensive residue conservation. Three segments of high variability, la- belled V1, V2, and V3 (Fig. 4), also exist. It is remarkable that all these segments are equally prominent within the class I enzymes of vertebrates as between the classes of all alcohol dehydrogenases. This particular correlation gives new insight regarding the enzyme in general, and can be interpreted func- tionally.

Constant region C1 occupies a segment of the catalytic domain and highlights the importance of the N-terminal re- gion. Although no obvious relationship to the active site or other specific properties have been assigned specifically to this segment previously, it seems to constitute a special unit. Another constant pattern, segment C2, on the other hand, is directly involved in coenzyme binding and is at the centre of the coenzyme-binding fold. This portion is strictly conserved and rich in glycine residues in all dehydrogenases; therefore, it is the most compelling of the two specially conserved seg- ments in Fig. 4.

The variable segment V1 corresponds to parts of the backbone that contributes to the active-site relationships, be- tween Cys46 and His67, the two zinc ligands to the active- site zinc atom. Such variability in the proximity of an active site was first noticed for the classes (cf. [18]) and was con- sidered functionally significant in view of the different sub- strate-binding properties [16]. This segment now appears also to be variable in the entire vertebrate system, even within the same enzyme class (Fig. 4, top), but without varia- tion in the functional residues (Table 4) and with the adja- cent, preserved catalytic zinc site [19].
