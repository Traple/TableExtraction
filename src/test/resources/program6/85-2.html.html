Both the rate of metal extraction and the stability of the enzyme are pH-dependent (Table 1). At pH 7.9 the rate of metal extraction is very slow. At pH 7.0 the amount of de- natured enzyme is rather high. During denaturation zinc is delivered to the solution, which can bind to nondenatured enzyme. Therefore the range of pH for a successful metal extraction is very limited; pH 7.4—7.5 and 0—4°C are opti- mal conditions. The temperature dependence of Tes buffer must be taken into consideration. The observed decrease in speciﬁc activity (Table 1) is in accordance with the fact that the catalytic zinc ion has been extracted by the dialysis against dipicolinic acid.

If EE horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase is treated in the crystalline state by dipicolinic acid [7] an active-site metal- depleted species with a residual activity of approximately 1% is produced. The relatively high residual activity found in our preparations with human B 1 B 1 isozyme (Table 1) may be due to the reincorporation of zinc which is delivered from the noncatalytic site after the denaturation of the active-site metal-depleted species.

Preparation of Co( c )1Zn( n )2 B 1B1 human liver alcohol dehydrogenase

After the complete removal of dipicolinic acid, which is bound strongly to the active-site metal-depleted B1B 1 isozyme, coba1t(II) was inserted in the active site by dialysis against 20 mM coba1t(II) acetate, 33 mM Tes, pH 7.4. The reconsti- tuted enzyme was freed from excess of cobalt by dialysis. One atom cobalt and approximately one atom zinc per subunit were determined by atomic absorption and the speciﬁc activity of this enzyme derivative amounted to the value of the original zinc enzyme (Table 1).

The broad LMCT transition between 300-400 nm does not allow one to discriminate between coba1t(II) bound to the catalytic or noncatalytic center. However the circular dichroism of these bands is typical for either catalytically or noncatalytically bound cobalt. This is also found in the circu- lar dichroic spectra observed with coba1t(II) hybrid horse liver alcohol dehydrogenases [16, 17].
