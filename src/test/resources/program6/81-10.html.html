This means that part of the agarose-bound enzyme molecules is exposed to much larger strains than in the crystal lattice whereas part is perhaps better stabilized. During exchange against cobalt the noncatalytic zinc ions of the bound enzyme behave similarly to those of the soluble enzyme, so that evidently the strain is not as effective in the second metal site as it is in the catalytic site. Above all, the strain is not effective before the catalytic metal ions have been removed.

In other words, the catalytic zinc ions have an important stabilizing effect on the tertiary and quater- nary structure of the immobilised enzyme. As soon as they are removed (whenever possible) the multipoint attachment to the matrix causes a distortion of the chain folding which can only partially be reverted by addition of a metal template.

In the noncatalytic sites, which bind the rapidly exchanging metals, the exchange mechanism could very well operate in such a way that no metal-free inter- mediate occurs in the reaction pathway [1,2]. There- fore, these sites could be better protected from irre- versible distortion than the catalytic sites.

Thus, the properties of the metal binding sites, preferentially the catalytic ones, are perhaps among the most sensitive criteria for the state of the tertiary and quaternary structure of the immobilised enzyme and are suitable indicators of structural changes.
