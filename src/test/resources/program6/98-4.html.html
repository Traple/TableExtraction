Ethanol Consumption. S. solfataricus cultures grown in 0.79% w/v ethanol (without glucose) utilized approximately 4.7 g/ L ethanol within 21 days, giving an average rate of ethanol consumption of 15.0 mg/ L/ hr (Figure 2). This was found to be 2-fold higher when compared to an average ethanol consump- tion rate of 7.1 mg/ L/ hr, as was reported for co-metabolized cultures (ethanol and glucose) previously.” In contrast, cultures supplied with 2.38 and 0.40% w/v ethanol gave an average ethanol consumption rate of 12.5 and 10.6 mg/L/hr, respec- tively. The lowest ethanol uptake was observed in cultures growing on 0.08% w/ v ethanol, at the rate of approximately of 3.0 mg/L/hr (refer to Figure 2). In all experimental cultures, incomplete consumption of ethanol was observed. We specu- late that this may either be due to the accumulation of inhibitory intermediates during growth, depletion of other critical medium constituents (e.g., sulfur or trace elements), or a lack of cofactors for the continuation of enzymatic activity, as described elsewhere.1

Ethanol Uptake Veriﬁcation via 13C-Labeled Peptide Iden- tification. Peptides identified from in vivo cultures grown on 0.79% w/v 12C and 13C universally labeled ethanol further strengthens the claim of both ethanol uptake and metabolic utilization by S. solfataricus. Light and heavy versions of ethanol (12C and 13C) were used as they are assimilated into the cells in a similar way, since both isotopes have the same physicochem- ical properties.23 For example, Figure 3 shows the mass spectrum of both light and heavy versions of the peptide sequence YDSGDYVGLLR, derived from the hypothetical pro- tein cutA-4 (SSO2639). The peptide, derived from a culture grown on 12C ethanol, was given by a m/z at 629.3, whereas the heavy version peptide from a culture grown in 13C ethanol was at 657.4. The mass difference between these peaks corre- sponds to the number of carbon atoms in the peptide (in this case 56 carbon atoms), leading to a difference in the mass to charge ratio (Am/z) of 28 (the peptide charge state is +2). The peptide area ratio between these two cultures is 1.03 :|: 0.2, and thus deemed to be acceptable as the protein from these cultures were mixed in a 1:1 ratio. This same observation, where duplex peaks generated from both isotopes (12C and 13C), was noted throughout all the peptides identified, including those belonging to the proteins annotated along the ethanol degradation pathway such as acetyl CoA and ADH proteins (data not shown). These findings further suggest that ethanol is indeed incorporated into S. solfataricus and utilized as a carbon source.

Translational Responses. The global translational responses of S. solfataricus toward the change of carbon source (from glucose to ethanol) were also investigated via the iTRAQ approach. A total of 284 proteins with 22 MS/ MS spectra (see Table B, Supporting Information) were identified and quanti- fied from S. solfataricus cultures grown at various ethanol concentrations (0.08, 0.40, and 0.79% w/v, see Table 1) when compared to a glucose control. The majority of these proteins were either annotated as hypothetical (approximately 24%) or having some translational and energy metabolism related functions (19 and 17%, respectively) (refer to Table B, Sup- porting Information). The complete list of proteins identified can be obtained from Table C in the Supporting Information, or from our website (http:/ lwrightlabgroup.shef.ac.uk/projects/ index.htm).

A previous study had also predicted the rate of false positives in similar iTRAQ experiments as being less than 1%.“ The iTRAQ variation seen in both technical and biological replicates was relatively small, with <50% variation in protein expression (data not shown). By taking these variations into consideration, a change in protein expression obtained from the iTRAQ approach was regarded as significant when the log ratio expression was 20.18 and 5—0.18 (i.e., > :|: 50% variation)?‘ Thus, by using this data cutoff, approximately 21 and 31% (on average) of the proteins identified from S. solfataricus grown at various ethanol concentrations were either increased or decreased in expression (Table B, Supporting Information). An average of 48% of the proteins identified from these ethanol cultures remained unchanged in expression level, within the aforementioned statistical cutoff. For the ethanol co-metabo- lized cultures, reported previously, however, 85% of the proteins identified remained unchanged, with 5 and 10% of the proteins had increased or decreased expression compared to the glucose control.” Greater proteome changes were observed in ethanol cultures compared to the co-metabolized cultures, possibly due to the presence of glucose in the co-metabolized culture, minimizing the ethanol impact on the organism.
