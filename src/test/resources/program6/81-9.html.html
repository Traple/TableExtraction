The importance of the role of the catalytic metal ions for the maintenance of the protein structure is especially obvious from the behaviour of the immo- bilized enzyme, discussed below. In contrast to the obvious importance of the catalytic metal ions for the structural stability of the protein, the role of the noncatalytic metal ions, which often are termed the ‘structural metal ions’ remains unclear. We have therefore omitted this term from our discussion.

The fourth conclusion is that model compounds support the assignment of the two cobalt chromo- phores in substituted liver alcohol dehydrogenase. The coordination of both pairs of zinc ions in horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase has been described as distorted tetrahedral in the X-ray crystallographic investigation at 0.24-nm resolution [18]. Only recently, tetrahedral low-molecular-weight complexes of tran- sition metals with sulfur ligands have been synthesized and characterized [38,39,50]. In Table 3 various tetrahedral cobalt complexes with different numbers of sulfur and nitrogen ligands are compiled. Here, models of both chromophores of cobalt alcohol dehydrogenases can be found. The noncatalytic site is paralleled by tetrathiophenolatocobaltate(II) [39] and cobalt(lI)rubredoxin [51]. A model of the catalytic site in its binary complex with imidazole is represented by bis(2-mercapto-2,2-dimethylethylamino)cobaltate- (II) [38]. Most convincing is the close similarity be- tween the spectra of the ‘blue hybrid’, cobalt plasto- cyanin and cobalt azurin [52]. The latter four com- plexes with mixed sulfur and nitrogen ligands show their main absorption band in the region between 620 and 650 nm. Binding of HST to the catalytic cobalt atoms of the ‘blue hybrid’ (Fig.2) generates a new absorption band at 697 nm, and all the complexes formed entirely from sulfur ligands have an additional absorption band between 700 and 800 nm. This phenomenon is predicted in the spectrochemical series of Tsuchida. Accordingly, the 650-nm band of the ‘blue hybrid’ is shifted to 656 nm in the ‘green hybrid’. Generally, the entire spectrum of the d-d transitions of cobalt alcohol dehydrogenase is red-shifted in comparison with other cobalt proteins which lack sulfur ligands.

There remains one uncertainty which is evident in the literature: comparisons of the primary structures of yeast and horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase suggest that the noncatalytic ligands are also present in the former enzyme [55]; given a similar tertiary structure, they should be able to bind cobalt (or zinc) too. In accordance, the spectrum of cobalt yeast alcohol dehydrogenase shows an absorption band at 740 nm, though less well resolved than in the ‘green hybrid’ or the fully substituted enzyme from horse liver [16]. The cobalt content was determined as 4 mol metal/mol protein [16]. The properties of this preparation were interpreted as to ﬁt to the structure of yeast alcohol dehydrogenase Co(c)4-enzyme in our nomenclature. The ligands in the catalytic sites of yeast alcohol de- hydrogenase are probably the sa-me as in the liver enzyme [56]. From this it follows that the fourfold substituted cobalt yeast alcohol dehydrogenase de- scribed [16] seems to be a species with substoichio- metric amounts of cobalt distributed over all the eight possible binding sites, including the noncatalytic ones composed of sulfur ligands only.
