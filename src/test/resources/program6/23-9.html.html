Several human CYPs have shown a pronounced regio selec- tivity for the benzylic position in the biotransforrnation of alkylpyrenes, including CYP3A4, a dominating form in liver and small intestine, and CYP1A1 and CYP1B1-forms expressed primarily in many extrahepatic tissues after induction by PAHs or other arylhydrocarbon receptor agonists (Engst et al., 1999). Likewise, SULTs capable of activating 1-HMP have been detected in many human tissues (Glatt et al., 2003). It is probable that the efﬁciency of sequestering enzymes would beneﬁt from a localisation at the site of the formation or further activation of benzylic alcohols. ADHs differ in their tissue distribution. Expression of ADH2 is strongly concentrated to the liver (Li et al., 1977; Nishimura and Naito, 2006). ADH1C is also high in liver (Edenberg and Bosron, 1997; Nishimura and Naito, 2006), but additionally has been detected at substantial levels in intes- tine (Parés and Farrés, 1996). ADH4 is absent in liver, but highly expressed in the upper gastrointestinal tract (Moreno and Parés, 1991; Parés and Farrés, 1996). ADH3 was detected in all tissues investigated (Edenberg and Bosron, 1997).

The existence of backup systems may mitigate the possible inﬂuence of genetic polymorphisms of individual ADH forms as well as exposure to selective inhibitors of individual forms. However, as shown in the present study, some compounds, such as 4-methylpyrazole and ethanol, may affect all ADHs forms involved in the detoxiﬁcation of alcohols of alkylpyrenes. 4- Methylpyrazole is used in the treatment of acute methanol and ethylene glycol poisonings (Mycyk and Leikin, 2003); there- fore, human exposures to this ADH inhibitor are short. The situation is different for ethanol. Blood levels of ethanol are usu- ally expressed in mg/ml. The Ki values of ethanol for ADHlC, ADH4 and ADH2 (expressed in these units: 0.078, 0.15 and 1.7 mg/ml, respectively) are systemically attainable by drinking alcoholic beverages. For comparison, the legal limit for driv- ing a car is at 0.5 mg/ml in Germany. The Ki value for ADH3 was much higher, 68 mg/ml (equivalent to 8.5%, v/v). It is only attainable in the lumen of the upper gastro-intestinal tract, but not in blood. It should be emphasized that the inhibition of the

oxidation of alkylpyrene alcohols by ethanol was competitive. Levels of human exposure to PAHs are low; daily total PAH uptake from food and tobacco smoking is in the low pug range (Glatt et al., 2007). Under these conditions, competitive inhibi- tion is very penetrating, in particular if levels of the inhibitors can exceed their Ki values manifold, as is the case for ethanol with regard to ADH1C and ADH4.

ADHs did not only catalyse the oxidation of alkylpyrene alco- hols, but also enhanced the backward reaction, the reduction of the aldehyde. The observed catalytic efﬁciencies of the backward reaction usually were somewhat higher than those of the forward reaction. For ADH3, which is insensitive to DMSO, these values may reﬂect the real situation. For the other enzyme forms, the true values for the catalytic efﬁciencies must be shifted more in favour of the reduction of the aldehyde due to the type of inhibition by DMSO. The forward reaction was inhibited in an uncompetitive mode, not affecting the catalytic efﬁciency. The backward reaction was inhibited in a mixed or non-competitive mode, reducing the catalytic efﬁciency. In any case, the occur- rence of a relatively efﬁcient reverse reaction implies that the aldehydes have to be sequestered by other enzymes, such as alde- hyde dehydrogenases, for ﬁnal detoxiﬁcation. These enzymes must have efﬁciencies in the same order of, or better than, those of the reduction of the aldehydes by ADHs. Such enzymes have been found and will be presented in a follow-up study (K. Rost, R. Kollock, W. Meinl, H. Frank, A. Seidel and H.R. Glatt, manuscript in preparation).
