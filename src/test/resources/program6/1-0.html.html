Reaction engineering is an important tool in the case of cofactor depending enzyme-catalyzed reactions. lt allows the establishment ofconditions resulting in lower product specific cofactor costs as compared with product-specific enzyme costs. This is shown for the stereospecific reduc- tion of carbonyl compounds yielding chiral amino acids and alcohols. ln continuous processes, cofactor costs can be reduced ifthe cofactor can be retained within the biore- actor or recycled into it after separation of the product. ln case of readily water-soluble substrates it is even possi- ble to recycle the cofactor during a single pass through a continuously operated reactor more than 4000 times because normally very low cofactor concentrations are sufficient to saturate the enzymes involved. INTRODUCTION

Enzymes have been proven to be versatile catalysts for the synthesis of milligram amounts of valuable com- pounds up t0 industrial production of bulk chemicals in a thousands-of-ton scale. This has been documented in numerous reviews and textbooks (Davies et al., 1989; Faber, 1995; Gerhartz, 1990; Roberts et al., 1992; Shel- don, 1993; Wong and Whitesides, 1994). Whereas regio- and stereoselectivity is seldom a problem in biocatalysis, space—time yield and product yield are of similar impor- tance as in classical catalysis. These aspects of enzyme reaction engineer- ing have been reviewed as well (Biselli et al., 1995; Bommarius, 1993; Kragl et al., 1992, 1993a, 1993b; 1996; Prazeres and Cabral, 1994). Yet there is still a prejudice that cofactor costs are so high that cofactor-dependent reactions cannot be carried out economically when us- ing isolated enzymes (Ryabov, 1991). In biotransforma- tions with whole cells, cofactor regeneration is accom- plished by the complete enzyme apparatus of the cell (Brodelius and Vandamme, 1987; Faber, 1995; Tanaka et al., 1993).

In this study, we present results on the enzymatic reduction of carbonyl compounds yielding chiral amino acids and alcohols (Fig. 1). The reductive amination of the corresponding a-keto acids by means of leucine dehydrogenase (EC 1.4.1.9) yields L-rert-leucine 4 or L-leucine 11 (Ohshima and Soda, 1989; Wandrey and Bossow, 1986). This method is especially useful for ob- taining non-natural amino acids, which are not accessi- ble by fermentation processes such as L-rert-leucine 4. Syntheses and applications of L-tert-leucine have been reviewed recently (Bommarius et al., 1995). Peptides and pseudopeptides containing this compound have a bet- ter metabolic stability when used as pharmaceuticals. This may be due to its sterically demanding tert-butyl group which lowers enzymatic hydrolysis in vivo. L-tert- Leucine may also be used directly as a chiral synthon. Reduction of ketones using alcohol dehydrogenase (EC 1.1.1.1) from Rhodococcus erythropolis yields (S )- alcohols such as (S)-1-phenyl-2-propanol 9 (Hummel and Gottwald, 1991).

For all these processes, NADH is required as a cofac- tor, which is regenerated by the well-established formate dehydrogenase (E.C. 1.2.1.2) system using formate as substrate (Schiitte et al., 1976). This reaction is irrevers- ible thus the coupled reaction is well dislocated toward the product side. However, due to the high KM value of the formate dehydrogenase a surplus of formate is used to guarantee sufﬁciently high activity even at high conversion.
