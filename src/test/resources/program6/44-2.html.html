Acid phosphatase activity

The acid phosphatase activity was determined in a 96-well microtiter plate, based on the hydrolysis of p-nitrophenyl phosphate (Wang and Pallen, 1991) at pH 5.5 for 30 min. The p-nitrophenol released was detected in an ELISA reader (Labsystems, Helsinki, Finland) at 450 nm. The speciﬁc activity (nmol p-nitrophenol'min""mg“ protein) was calculated by using p-nitrophenol as standard.

Alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) activity

ADH activity was assayed by measuring the absorbance increase at 340 nm due to reduction of NADi to NADH. The reaction mixture (1 ml) contained: 20 mM glycine-NaOH buffer, pH 9.5, 10 uM E- 64, 50-200 p.g of sample protein, 760 mM ethanol, and 1.9 mM NADi. In some reactions, 1.9 mM NADPf and 760 mM of different alcohols, e.g., methanol, n-propanol, isopropanol, and n-butanol, were tested. Enzyme activity is reported as total activity (1 unit = p.mol NADH formed/min) and speciﬁc activity (1 U = p.mol NADH formed~min"'mg" protein) at room temperature incubations. Rep- resentative data from at least 3 experiments are shown.

Electrophoresis and immunoblot

Electrophoresis was performed according to Laemmli (1970) in 7.5% polyacrylamide-SDS gels. Samples were diluted with sample buffer and boiled for 3 min; thereafter, 5% 2-mercaptoethanol was added, and the samples were boiled again for 1 min. Proteins separated by electropho- resis were electroblotted to nitrocellulose paper (Towbin et al., 1979); the paper was blocked with 5% nonfat dry milk and successively in- cubated for 1 hr with the rabbit anti-L. lactis ADHE antibody and a peroxidase-labeled secondary antibody. After each antibody incubation, the paper was washed with PBS containing 0.05% Tween 20, and the antibody complex was detected with Luminol according to the manu- facturer’s instructions (Amersham/Pharmacia, Buckinghamshire, Eng- land).

Electron microscopy

Negative staining was performed according to standard protocol us- ing 1% uranyl acetate. For immunogold labeling, the samples were ﬁxed with a mixture of 2% paraformaldehyde and 1% glutaraldehyde, then washed 3 times with PBS, and postﬁxed in the dark with 2% uranyl acetate for 1 hr at 4 C. Samples were dehydrated at 4 C in the presence of 2% uranyl acetate and successively with 30, 50, and 70% methanol for 5 min for each methanol concentration. Finally, samples were de- hydrated with 90% methanol without uranyl acetate for 30 min at 4 C. Samples were successively inﬁltrated at 4 C with methanol 90%/Low- icryl K4M (1:1) for 1 hr, with methanol 90%/Lowicryl (1:2) for 1 hr; and with 100% Lowicryl overnight. Samples were embedded in Low- icryl and polymerized by ultraviolet light irradiation for 48 hr at 4 C. Thin sections were rinsed with distilled water, preincubated 1 hr with 10% normal BSA and 1% BSA in TBS (20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.2, 154 mM NaCl, and 20 mM NaN3), and incubated in droplet solutions at room temperature for 4 hr with or without (control) the antibody anti- L. lactis ADHE. Grids were washed 5 times with TBS containing 0.1% BSA, followed by 24 hr of incubation with an anti-rabbit secondary antibody conjugated to 15 nm colloidal gold. Samples were analyzed by electron microscopy using a Jeol 100S (Tokyo, Japan) electron mi- croscope at 60 kV.

To compare the ADH activities in soluble and particulate fractions, several alcohols were tested as substrates (data not shown). Both soluble and particulate NADi-dependent ADH activities used unbranched alcohols ethanol, n-propanol, and n- butanol, but they did not use methanol. Isopropanol was better used by the soluble enzyme(s), possibly because of the presence of the previously reported (Kumar et al., 1992) NADPt-depen- dent ADH] activity, which could have little activity in the pres- ence of NADi.

The NADi-dependent ADH activity was also measured in ameba fractions in experiments of plasma membrane puriﬁca- tion (Aley et al, 1980). The activity was found in the soluble fraction and, to a remarkable extent, in the internal membrane preparations (data not shown). The enzyme ADH2 was also detected with an antibody against L. lactis ADHE. As shown in Figure 1, a 96—kDa band, corresponding to the molecular mass of ameba ADH2, is well recognized by the antibody in the total extract (Fig. 1B, lane 1), the soluble fraction (lane 2), and in the internal membranes (lane 3). In plasma membrane preparation, the band recognized by the antibody was faint (lane 4). These results agree with the distribution of the 96—kDa pro- tein (T orian et al., 1990), which later was identiﬁed as the ame- ba ADH2 (Flores et al., 1996).

To determine if some ameba membrane vesicles carry ADH2, the internal membrane fractions were ﬁxed, and the ADH enzyme was detected by electron microscopy with the antibody against L. lactis ADHE and a gold-labeled secondary antibody. As shown in Figure 2B, the antibody did not bind to membrane vesicles, but it was bound (Fig. 2A, control) to a granular or amorphous material found in internal membrane preparations. In some cases, the gold particles were observed near the membranes, but in most cases, the antibody did not bind to the lipid bilayers; therefore, ADH2 does not seem to be a membrane enzyme.

To characterize the amebic granular or amorphous material to which particulated NADi-dependent ADH was associated, isopycnic separation and differential centrifugation of ameba subcellular components were performed.
