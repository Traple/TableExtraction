that the activity of class I ADH is signiﬁcantly higher (P < 0.001) in liver cancer than in healthy tissue in the total patients group and in specimens from both drinkers and non-drinkers. The other tested classes of isoenzymes had higher activities in cancer cells than in healthy tissue, but differences were not statistically signiﬁcant in all patient groups (P > 0.05).

The total activity of ADH was signiﬁcantly higher in liver cancer patients than in healthy patients (about 20%). The median activity of total ADH was 10.64 nmol/min/mg protein in cancer tissue and 8.75 nmol/n1in/mg protein in healthy tissue in the total patients group. The total activity of ALDH was also higher in cancer tissues than in healthy tissue.

The activity of ADH and ALDH in cancer tissues of drinkers did not differ from that of non-drinkers (P < 0.05) (Table 3). There were, in addition, no signiﬁcant differ- ences in ADH and ALDH activity between healthy tissues from drinkers and non-drinkers. Comparison of the activity of ADH isoenzymes between drinkers and non-drinkers revealed no signiﬁcant differences.

The analysis of ADH, ALDH and ADH isoenzymes activities did not indicate signiﬁcant differences between primary and metastatic tumors (Table 4).

Discussion

It is generally accepted that heavy alcohol consumption is associated with an increased risk of digestive tract cancer including also the liver [13]. After ethanol ingestion, the liver is the major site of its metabolism. The hepatocytes contains three main pathways (ADH, microsomal ethanol oxidizing system, and catalase) located in a different sub- cellular compartments. Chronic ethanol abuse leads to induction of hepatocyte microsomal cytochrome P450 2 E1, an enzyme that metabolizes ethanol to acetaldehyde and free radicals. Cytochrome P450 2 E1-dependent etha- nol metabolism is also associated with activation of procarcinogens, changes in cell cycle, and altered immune system responses [14].
