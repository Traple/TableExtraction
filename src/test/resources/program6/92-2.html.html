DgADH is barely active towards methanol (2%), whereas the methanol dehydrogenase of B. methano- licus C1 with a similar decameric structure functions as the key enzyme in methanol utilization by this organism, al- though its best substrate is ethanol. It remains to be estab- lished whether alcohol dehydrogenases of Desulfovibrio strains that were reported to grow on methanol, such as D. carbinolicus (15) and D. alcoholovorans (20), are more active towards methanol than DgADH. For the reverse reaction, aldehydes were used at 2 mM; the highest activities were with acetaldehyde (100%), propionaldehyde (250%), and butyraldehyde (37%), but the enzyme was not very active towards formaldehyde (8%). The reduction of acetal- dehyde was 4.9-fold faster than the oxidation of ethanol. NAD(H) was the electron acceptor (donor). NADP(H) as an electron acceptor (donor) with ethanol and acetaldehyde as the substrates yielded activities of 1.5 and 1.6%, respec- tively, of the values found with NAD(H).

The dehydrogenation reaction had a broad pH optimum around 9.5 and the reverse reaction around pH 7.5. The optimum temperature was approximately 40°C. At higher temperatures, rapid inactivation of the enzyme occurred. The apparent Km values were as follows: for ethanol, 0.15 mM; for propanol, 0.28 mM; for acetaldehyde, 1.8 mM; for formaldehyde, 50 mM; for NAD (with ethanol as a sub- strate), 74 uM; and for NADH (with acetaldehyde as a substrate), 20 uM. The D. gigas enzyme is an ethanol dehydrogenase par excellence: it has a far lower Km for ethanol than the classical Zn-containing fermentative en- zyme ADH1 from Saccharomyces cerevisiae (8) or the fermentative primary alcohol dehydrogenase from Thermo- anaerobacter ethanolicus (3). The Km determination, how- ever, was carried out at a higher pH than the normal cytoplasmic value. Because of its rather low Km and in view of its oxygen lability (see below), DgADH seems well adapted to its role in an organism that thrives in anaerobic sediments where ethanol concentrations are not likely to exceed the low-micromolar range.

Preincubation of the enzyme for 5 min with metal-chelat- ing compounds resulted in the following relative activities (control without addition is 100%): EDTA (10 mM), 81%; 2,2-bipyridyl (3 mM), 89% and (1.5 mM) 74%; and 1,1- phenanthroline (0.6 mM), 38%. Preincubation with sulfhy- dryl reagents inhibited the reaction to a greater extent; relative activities were as follows: N-ethylmaleimide (2 mM), 4%; p-chloromercuribenzoate (0.25 mM), 33%; and p-chloromercuribenzene sulfonate (0.1 mM), 44%. When tested at 2 mM (without preincubation), Fe“ stimulated the activity up to 150%, Mg“ and Ca“ had no effect, and Mn“, Co“, Ni“, and Cu“ reduced the initial reaction rates to 53, 50, 20, and 2%, respectively. Zn“ inhibited the reaction very strongly: concentrations of 0.01, 0.02, and 0.04 mM resulted in initial reaction rates of 85, 70, and 35%, respectively, of the control; exposure of the enzyme to 0.015 mM Zn“ for 1 min was sufﬁcient for a complete inhibition of the ethanol dehydrogenase activity.

The oxygen lability of the enzyme was most pronounced at pH 9, but even at pH 7, 80% of the activity was lost under air within 2 h (Fig. 3). The residual activity after 2.5 h was lost within 1 day, even under N2. The activity of the enzyme could not be restored by ﬂushing the enzyme solution with N2 or by adding dithionite. Lability in oxidizing environ- ments is also a characteristic of ADH2, the Fe-containing alcohol dehydrogenase from Zymomonas mobilis (14, 16); ADH2 could only be puriﬁed under aerobic conditions when buffers containing ascorbate and Fe“ were used. In the absence of Fe“, it became inactive, and it was far more stable under N2 than under air. Whether the stimulatory eﬁect of Fe“ on the activity of DgADH is due to a role of Fe“ in the enzyme is not known, but it seems unlikely. Fe was present in far smaller amounts than Zn.

N-terminal analysis and comparison with other alcohol dehydrogenases. The N-terminal amino acid sequence was analyzed twice by automated Edman degradation of about 2 nmol of DgADH (27). The analysis was carried out for 42 Edman degradation steps; 38 residues could be unambigu- ously determined (Fig. 4). No signiﬁcant similarity was found between DgADH and classical long-chain alcohol dehydrogenases such as yeast ADH1 (22) or short-chain alcohol dehydrogenases (17). The relatedness to members of a recently recognized third family of alcohol dehydrogenases became evident after sequence alignment around the -Gly- X-Gly- sequence at position -18-19-20-. The similarities found between the N-terminal sequence of DgADH and the sequence of Z. mobilis‘ ADH2 is 26%; with ADH4 of S. cerevisiae, 36%; with the methanol dehydrogenase of B. methanolicus, 38%; with propanediol dehydrogenase of Esch- erichia coli, 21%; with E. coli alcohol dehydrogenase, 21%; and with an NADP-dependent alcohol dehydrogenase from Clostridium acetobutylicum, 17%. The sequence compari- son shows that two residues (5%) of the N-terminal region have been conserved in all seven sequences, and seven residues (17%) have been conserved in six of the seven sequences. If conservative amino acid substitutions are also considered, the similarity increases to 14 and 33%, respec- tively. Recently, four additional members of this family of alcohol dehydrogenases were discovered (4a, 28). Despite the rather large N-terminal sequence similarity to DgADH and its similar decameric structure, B. methanolicus metha- nol dehydrogenase is remarkably different in metal compo- sition (Mg) and in some functional properties (substrate speciﬁcity, absence of oxygen lability, effects of Mg“, and an activator protein [27]).
