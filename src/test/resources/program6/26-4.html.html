The substrate specificity of the T roseum ADH towards several compounds is shown in Table 2. A number of primary, secondary and tertiary alco- hols were used to determine the substrate specific- ity of the enzyme. A gradual decrease in rate was observed by increasing the length and branching of the carbon chain of the alcohol. The enzyme oxidized mainly primary aliphatic alcohols and exhibited high substrate specificity towards etha- nol, n-propanol and crotyl alcohol. On the other hand, the enzyme did not show activity towards long chain primary alcohols, short chain secondary alcohols and tertiary alcohols. This substrate spec- ificity of the enzyme was moderately similar to those of ADHs from Moraxella (Tsigos et al., 1998) and Thermoanaerobacter ethanolicus (Bur- dette and Zeikus, 1994). Green et al. (1993) suggested that the lower activity of ADH on large alcohols could be due to the smaller size of the

substrate binding pocket. The effect of ethanol concentration on ADH activity was investigated at 50 °C. The Km value of the T roseum ADH for ethanol was 24.2 mM. This value is similar to those of ADHs from Sulfolobus solfataricus (17.2 mM) (Cannio et al., 1996) and Thermoanaerobac- ter ethanolicus 39E (25 mM) (Burdette and Zei- kus, 1994). However, the Km values of ADHs from Bacillus stearothermophilus (Sheehan et al., 1988), Desulfovibrio gigas (Hensgens et al., 1993) and Moraxella (Tsigos et al., 1998) were 0.45 mM, 0.15 mM and 0.58 mM, respectively. There- fore, T roseum ADH has a weaker affinity toward ethanol than ADHs from Bacillus stearothermo- philus, Desulfovibrio gigas and Moraxella. ADHs from various sources are also known to differ in coenzyme specificity (Branden et al., 1975). Gen- erally, ADHs from eukaryotic organisms are main- ly NAD+ enzymes, while those from prokaryotic organisms are either NAD+ or NADP+ -dependent. The T roseum ADH required NAD+ as a coen- zyme, whereas no activity was found with NADPJ“. ADHs isolated from Bacillus stearoth- ermophilus (Sheehan et al., 1988) and Sulfolobus solfataricus (Rella et al., 1987) also required NAD+ as a coenzyme.

Several compounds were tested for their inhibi- tory action on the T roseum ADH (Table 3). Three compounds (imidazole, pyrazole and pyridine) that had a similar structure of adenine of NAD inhibited the enzyme. Particularly, pyrazole notably inhibit- ed the enzymatic activity and was the most potent inhibitor of those used. Its I50 value for the enzyme was 0.02 mM. Trivi et a1. (1997) reported that pyrazol was the strongest competitive inhibitor of ADH activity.
