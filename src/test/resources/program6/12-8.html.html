The parameters (rate constants) that are deter- mined from the ﬁtting of the metabolic data include the dissociation constant of the dead-end inhibitor (the ratio of k_5/k+5, or k_5 if k+5 is ﬁxed at a typical value) and the rate constant for the elimination of the inhibitor (k+6). The dis- sociation constant for the inhibitor can be initially estimated from the in vitro data, but we ﬁnd that the dissociation constant observed in vivo is often ten times or more higher due to bioavailability of the compound [49,53]. However, because the inhibitor is also being eliminated, determination of the in vivo dissociation constant by an initial velocity approximation of the data in Fig. 5 is subject to error. Therefore, the simulation uses all of the data from the metabolic experiments and ﬁts the two parameters independently. For the data in Fig. 5, k_5 was estimated to be 6.7 min_1 with an error of < 10%, yielding an apparent dissociation constant (k_ 5lk+ 5) of 6.7 umol kg- l. Since the fraction of the body mass that is water in these animals was 0.73 ml g-l, the apparent dissociation constant is equivalent to 9.2 uM compared to the value of 1.1 uM determined in vitro [49]. The rate constant for elimination of inhibitor was estimated to be 0.013 min- l, with a S.E. < 10%. The estimated and ﬁtted values of the parameters that are listed in Fig. 4(B) were used to calculate the lines in Fig. 5 and the agreement with the observed points is excellent (R2 value = 0.991). The simulation illustrates the utility of KIN- SIM/FITSIM for extracting rate constants for complex reaction mechanisms, for which explicit equations cannot be derived. KINSIM was used, for instance, to describe quantitatively the negative cooperativity observed for the oxidation of etha- nol by human ADH1C*2 [40]. The simulation of results in Fig. 5 also demonstrates that data from kinetic studies of carbonyl metabolizing enzymes in vitro can be applied to metabolic data. An important goal of enzymology is to be able to explain in vivo results. The simulations offer a robust method for this purpose and present a challenge to enzymologists to collect data that can correlate in vitro and in vivo results. Metabolic

data for steroid, retinoid and various other alco- hols and carbonyl compounds, including the transient changes that occur during ethanol meta- bolism, should be useful in the search to discover connections between alcoholism and other dis- eases.
