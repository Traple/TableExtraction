The ribose of the NMN-part of NAD is hydrogen bonded to two side-chains, Ser-48 and His-51 and one main-chain carbonyl oxygen atom. The carboxyamide group of the nicotinamide is hydrogen bonded to main-chain atoms, one bond from its oxygen atom to an NH group and two from its amino group to carbonyl oxygen atoms.

The nicotinamide ring is in contact with a Val and a Thr side-chain on one side and the Cys-zinc ligands on the other side. On one edge of the ring it makes several contacts to the first residues in the loop of the coenzyme-binding domain which changes its conformation drastically. One hydrogen bond is formed to the main-chain of the first of these residues from the carboxyamide group. These interactions between the protein and the nicotinamide are probably important for the switch from one conformation of the enzyme to the other.

Substrate binding

The complete substrate-binding cleft is formed by a com- bination of three different components: residues which exist as in the unliganded enzyme, residues which have been brought into the substrate cleft by the conformational change, and by the nicotinamide ring itself. The substrate- binding cleft is thus only partly formed in the apoenzyme. In the holoenzyme the nicotinamide ring forms one side of the substrate cleft and thereby a tight binding pocket. The nicotinamide ring helps to position the substrate in the most favourable position for hydride transfer during the transition state (Fig. 4).

The zinc ion at the active site binds the substrate oxygen atom. For an aldehyde, the zinc ion polarizes the substrate such that its first carbon atom increases its electrophilicity to facilitate the hydride transfer to it. For the reverse reaction, a negatively charged intermediate becomes a necessity. The zinc ion at the active site of LADH is important for stabiliz- ing the charged intermediate of the reaction.

NAD * is essential to produce the charged alcoxide ion for the alcohol oxidation reaction. The positive charge is neces- sary here to shift the pK, of the alcohol in such a way that formation of alcoxide is possible. The water-free environ- ment created by the combination of the conformational change and NAD binding is an important factor in this respect.

Coenzyme binding and the conforrnationai change

The binding site which binds the ADP-ribose part of NAD is to a large extent present in the open form of the enzyme; this has been demonstrated for the ADP-ribose complex which does not induce a conformational change of the enzyme. The main difference between this complex and the complexes with the full coenzyme is that there is a layer of water between the coenzyme and the catalytic domain. Arg-369 which forms a hydrogen bond to one phosphate oxygen atom of NAD does not bind directly to the pyro- phosphate in this complex.

The conformational change of the enzyme increases the number of interactions formed between the coenzyme and the enzyme. The optimal binding interaction is partly due to the rotation of the catalytic domain, which brings Ser-48 and His-Sl to form hydrogen bonds to the ribose of the NMN- half of the molecule. The small conformational change of the coenzyme-binding domain is also important to improve the binding interactions. The coenzyme rotates at the edge of the coenzyme-binding domain further into the binding cleft. Thereby the pyrophosphate comes close enough to form a hydrogen bond also to Arg-369, and the nicotinamide can form hydrogen bonds to main-chain atoms.

The binding of NADH is so tight that its release from the enzyme is the rate-determining step in alcohol oxidation. The consequence of this is that weakening of the interaction between NAD and the enzyme speeds up the reaction. This has been demonstrated by chemical modiﬁcation of co- enzyme-binding residues (Plapp, 1970), from comparative studies of human and yeast isoenzymes differing in one co- enzyme binding residue (Jornvall et al., 1984) and in site- directed mutagenesis on yeast ADH (Plapp et aL, 1987).

Comparisons with other NAD(P)-binding enzymes

The coenzyme-binding domain of LADH is to a varying degree similar to those of other dehydrogenases, to dihydro- folate reductase and to FAD-binding enzymes (for a recent review see Eklund & Branden, 1987b). The conformation of the bound cofactor is inﬂuenced by local structural differ- ences, but is very similar in LADH, malate dehydrogenase (Birktoft et al., 1988) and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate de- hydrogenase (Skarzynski et al., 1987), except that in the latter case the nicotinamide is in syn-conformation. The binding of an acidic residue to the adenosine ribose by two hydrogen bonds is also present in these three enzymes and in the FAD-binding site of glutathione reductase (Schulz et aL, 1982). Main-chain amino groups, mainly from the amino end of helices, are especially important for interactions with the phosphate oxygen atoms.

Conformational changes are different in the dehydro- genases. The conformational changes in LADH and glycer- aldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase are essentially domain rotations, whereas loop movements are most important for lactate and malate dehydrogenase.
