The use of isolated ADHs for organic synthesis offers signiﬁcant advantages over the use of whole-cell catalysts, including reduced side reactions caused by the microbial metabolism and higher volumetric productivities (Goldberg et al. 2007). Such an approach, however, requires efficient regeneration of the nicotinamide cofactors to allow for an economically feasible process. These cofactors can either be regenerated (electro)chemically or by using an enzyme- coupled or substrate-coupled approach (Chenault and Whitesides 1987; van der Donk and Zhao 2003). Even though cofactor regeneration is still one of the major challenges of ADH application, several processes based on isolated ADHs have been established on the preparative scale (Schmid et al. 2002; Straathof et al. 2002).

Furthermore, the stability of the enzyme under process conditions, which often differ significantly from the native conditions within the ADHs host organism, is of major importance for determining its practical usefulness. Partic- ularly, the presence of organic solvents, non-native pH values, and elevated reaction temperatures, which are often needed for organic transformations, are frequently reported to impair a given biocatalyst’s activity and stability (Schoemaker et al. 2003). Enzymes originating from extremophiles often exhibit higher stability under such denaturizing conditions (Haki and Rakshit 2003; Taylor et al. 2004). Especially, enzymes from (hyper)thermophilic organisms (growing at temperatures above 50°C or 80°C) have been recognized for their outstanding stability (Vieille and Zeikus 2001), which was attributed to an increased rigidity imposed by a larger amount of ionic interactions and disulfide bonds in the protein structure (Vieille and Zeikus 2001).

Therefore, it is not astonishing that a number of ADHs originating from (hyper)thermophilic organisms have gained considerable interest with respect to their practica- bility (Radianingtyas and Wright 2003), the most studied being the NADP-dependent ADH from T hermoanaerobium brockii (TbADH). This enzyme shows a very broad substrate spectrum in addition to an impressive stability in the presence of organic solvents (Keinan et al. 1986; Miroliaei and Nemat-Gorgani 2002). However, one major drawback of TbADH is its dependence on the costly cofactor NADP(H), which is approximately five times more expensive than the unphosphorylated form (Wichmann and Vasic-Racki 2005). For a preparative application, it would thus be favorable to use ADHs that require NAD(H).

Thus, in the present report, we describe the biochemical characterization of TADH with respect to its applicability for the production of chiral alcohols and carboxylic acids from their corresponding aldehydes.

Materials and methods General

Most chemicals were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (Buchs, Switzerland) and used without further purification. Other compounds were purchased from the following companies: Nickel chloride was purchased from Acros (Germany), cobalt chloride from Alfa Aesar (Germany), lithium chloride from Calbiochem (USA), and NADH from AppliChem (Germany). Yeast extract and trypton were purchased from Difco (Germany), methanol from Fisher Scientific (Germany), and ethanol, ethyl acetate, and methyl tert-butyl ether from Merck (Germany). Urea, ampicillin, and isopropyl-B-D-thiogalactopyranoside were purchased from Roth (Germany) and 2-propanol from VWR (Germany). Aqueous solutions were prepared using deion- ized water, purified with a Seralpur Pro 90 CN (Seral, Ransbach-Baumbach, Germany) water purification system.

Preparation of TADH

Cultivation of recombinant E. coli BL21 pLysS pASZ2 (Hollmann et al. 2005) was performed on 200-mL scale in 1-L bafﬂed Erlenmeyer ﬂasks in an orbital shaker (Multitron, Infors AG Bottningen, Switzerland) using terriﬁc broth (TB) medium (Sambrook and Russell 2001), supplemented with 100 ug mL-l ampicillin and 23 ug mL-l chloramphenicol at 37°C and 250 rpm. Puriﬁcation of TADH was performed according to Hollrigl et al. (2007). In brief, E. coli cells were directly heat treated at 75°C for 20 min, and precipitated proteins were separated by ultracentrifugation. The resulting supernatant was subjected to an anioic exchange chromatog- raphy (AEX) using Sepabeads® EB-QA405 (Resindion S.R.L., Binasco, Italy). Active fractions were pooled, con- centrated using Amicon Ultra 50-kDa centrifugal filters (Milipore, Germany), and stored at —20°C until further use.
