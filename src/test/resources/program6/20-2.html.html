On the contrary, Cu-ADH has a higher optimum temperature (about 40 °C rather than 35 °C) and shows a certain thermal resistance, i.e. it maintains the catalytic efﬁ- ciency even at high temperature (as reported in Table l). The thermal resistance may be correlated to the rigidity of the active site, and both cobalt and copper probably stiffen the active site, because of the geometry of the complexes they form. As a matter of fact, zinc may give either tetra- or penta-coordinated complexes in a very ﬂexible manner, whereas cobalt and copper give tetra-co-ordinated complexes and force the active site in a deﬁnite and ﬁxed geometry. Moreover, espe- cially copper generally gives more stable complexes than zinc with ligands and, as a consequence, it bestows a great resistance to denaturation on the active site [l8].

As far as substrate speciﬁcity is concerned, Cu-ADH shows an afﬁnity decrease when the chain length in- creases (ethanol > l-propanol g l-butanol) and is even more marked when a branched chain is considered (2-propanol), which allows no catalytic activity to be observed. As can be seen in Table 2, Kw decreases quickly from ethanol to l-propanol, then a further decrease is observed with l-butanol. Native ADH dis- plays the same behaviour regarding substrate speciﬁcity [20]. Nevertheless, in the case of native ADH, kM show a larger increase in comparison with Cu-ADH when butanol is considered, while K6,, decreases more slightly than in the case of Cu-ADH.
