2.4. Anti-ADH antisera and immunoblot analyses

Antisera against ADHs were raised using bacterial inclusion bodies for immunisation, as previously described for SULTs (Meinl et al., 2006). ADHs were subcloned from the pKK233-2 into a high-expression vector, pET (Novagen, Madison, USA). The recombinant vector was transfected into E. coli BL21 (DE3) (Novagen). High expression was induced with isopropylthio- [3-D-1-galactopyranoside. Under these conditions all ADHs formed inclusion bodies, which were isolated as described previously (Meinl et al., 2006). When inclusion bodies were subjected to sodium dodecylsulphate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and staining with Coomassie blue, a single band (representing ADH) was detected. For raising antisera, inclusion bodies were adjusted to a protein concentration of 1.25 mg/ml in medium A [140 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl, 5.4mM Na2HPO4, 1.8mM KH2PO4 and 1% (w/v) SDS]. For the ﬁrst immunisation 200 ptl of this solution was mixed with an equal volume of complete Freund’s adjuvant, for the subsequent burst immunisations incom- plete Freund’s adjuvant was used. Rabbits were immunised subcutaneously at three to four sites of the back. Three to six burst immunisations were performed with intervals of four weeks. Immunoreactivity of the serum against human ADHs was regularly examined. Ten days after the last immunisation rabbits were killed and bleeded. Blood was centrifuged with 2500 >< g for 30 min at 4 OC in order to obtain the serum. The sera were used as the primary anti- body for immunoblot analyses at a dilution of 1:10,000 in medium B [2% (w/v) skimmed milk powder, 20 mM Tris/HCl (pH 9.0), 140 mM NaCl and 0.1% (w/v) Tween 20]. Peroxidase-conjugated anti-rabbit IgG (Sigma-Aldrich, Taufkirchen, Germany) diluted 112500 in medium B, was used as secondary antibody.

2.5. Partial purification of human ADHs

S. typhimurium contains endogenous ADH activity towards HMPs (Kollock et al., 2008). Therefore, it was necessary for kinetic analyses to separate the cDNA-expressed human ADHs from the endogenous activity. For this separa- tion we exploited the observation that various mammalian ADHs show unusually weak interactions with anion exchange materials (Stromberg et al., 2002). The separations were conducted by fast-performance liquid chromatography essen- tially as previously described for ADH2 (Kollock et al., 2008). Fractions were concentrated using AmiconUltra Centrifugal Filter Devises 30,000 MWCO (Millipore, Carrigtwohill, Ireland) and assayed for ADH activity spectropho- tometrically at 340 nm using 33 mM ethanol (ADH1C, ADH2 and ADH4) or 1mM 1-octanol (ADH3) as the substrate. All human ADHs eluted in the ﬂow-through front, whereas elution of the endogenous activity and the bulk of the other proteins required much higher ionic strength. Protein concentra- tions were determined using the BCA Protein Assay Kit (Pierce, Rockford, USA). The purity of the preparations was checked by SDS-PAGE and subse- quent Coomassie-staining. Immunoblot analyses were performed using virtually homogenous ADH inclusion bodies as standards for quantiﬁcation.

2.6.1. Continuous spectrometric assay

Continuous reaction monitoring has obvious advantages. For example, a deviation from linearity with time is immediately recognised. Likewise lev- els of enzyme, substrate or modulator can quickly be optimised, since the result is immediately available. Biochemical reactions involving the co-substrate NAD(P)(H) are commonly monitored using the fact that only the reduced form absorbs light in the 340-370 nm range. However, pyrene derivatives exhibit sub- stantial absorbance in the same wavelength range. Thus, for calculating reaction rates, one has to take into account the sum of the absorption changes due to the formation or utilisation of NADH, the formation or disappearance of the alcohol and the disappearance or formation of the aldehyde. 2.6.2. Endpoint assay with determination of 1-FP by HPLC

The continuous assay presented in the preceding section is only useful when a single substrate is used, but not when competition between different substrates is the object of the investigation. In that case, the product of interest has to be analysed directly. Enzymatic reactions using 1-HMP as the substrate were carried out and the amount of l-FP formed was detected by HPLC as described previously (Kollock et al., 2008).

2.7. Kinetic and inhibition studies

For determining the apparent Vmax and Km values in the presence or absence of an inhibitor, the reaction rate (v) was measured using various concentrations of the substrate S. Reaction rates were expressed per mg ADH protein taking into account the degree of puriﬁcation of the preparation used (Section 2.5). Inhibition kinetics are presented as Lineweaver-Burk or Dixon plots, which ever is more ostensive in the individual case. However, the kinetic constants were not determined from the plots. Vmax and Km values (:1: standard error) were calculated by ﬁtting the data points to the Michaelis-Menten equation v = (Vmax [S])/(Km + [S]) using non-linear regression analysis (SigmaPlot 8.02, SPSS Science Software).
