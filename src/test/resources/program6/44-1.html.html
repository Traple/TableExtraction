ABSTRACTI The protozoan parasite Entamoeba histolytica is an ancient eukaryotic cell that shows morphologically atypical organelles and differs metabolically from higher eukaryotic cells. The aim of this study was to determine the subcellular local- ization of ameba NADi-dependent alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH2). The enzyme activity was present in soluble and mainly in particulate material whose density was 1.105 in a sucrose gradient. By differential centrifugation, most of the ADH activity sedimented at 160,000 g (160,000-g pellet), similar to the Escherichia c0li polymeric ADHE. In the Coomassie staining of the 160,000-g pellet analyzed by electrophoresis, a 96-kDa protein was more prominent than in other fractions; this band was recognized by antibodies against Lactococcus lactis ADHE. By gold labeling, the antibodies recognized the granular material that mainly constitutes the 160,000-g pellet and a material that sedimented along with the internal membrane vesicles. By negative staining, the 160,000-g fraction showed helical rodlike structures with an average length of 103 nm; almost no membrane vesicles were observed in this pellet. In internal membrane fractions, no rodlike structures were found, but protomerlike round structures were observed. These results indicate that the main amebic NADi-dependent ADH2 activity is naturally organized as rodlike helical particles, similar to bacterial ADHE. Detection of ADH2 in membrane fractions might be explained by cosedimentation

of the multimeric ADH during membrane puriﬁcation.

The human protozoan parasite Entamoeba histolytica is an ancient eukaryotic cell that lacks typical mitochondria and shows morphologically atypical organelles. Its metabolism dif- fers from that of higher eukaryotic cells; thus, some of the enzymes implicated in the glucose fermentation pathway re- quire unusual cosubstrates (Reeves, 1984); moreover, they are more similar to bacterial than to eukaryotic enzymes (Kumar et al., 1992; Bruchhaus and Tannich, 1.994; Yang et al., 1994; Rosenthal et al., 1997). Therefore, these enzymes are interesting targets to search for new antiamebic drugs (Yong et al., 1996) because of the differences from their human counterparts and their presumably indispensable role for ameba survival in the human large bowel.

In ameba, 1 NADi- and 3 NADPi-dependent alcohol dehy- drogenases have been described and studied at the biochemical and molecular levels (Kumar et al., 1992; Bruchhaus and Tan- nich, 1994; Yang et al., 1994; Kimura et al., 1996; Rodriguez et al., 1996). The gene of the NAD-dependent ADH (adhZ) was cloned and the enzyme was puriﬁed from the supernatant of a sample centrifuged at 150,000 g (Bruchhaus and Tannich, 1994); the puriﬁed enzyme presented a rod-forming association of more than 20 protomers. This enzyme is encoded by a gene that shares about 45-48% identity with Escherichia coli and Lactococcus lactis adhE (Bruchhaus and Tannich, 1994; Yang et al., 1994; Arnau et al., 1998). Moreover, ADH2 from E. histolytica is present in internal membrane preparations, is as- sociated with the ameba surface, and is secreted or shed to the culture medium (Flores et al., 1996). To determine if ADH2 is a membrane enzyme, its subcellular distribution was deﬁned by its activity, by western blot, and by electron microscopy. It was observed that E. histolytica ADH2 had a similar structure to the rodlike multimeric ADHE from E. coli and does not seem to be a real membrane enzyme.

amond et al., 1978). Trophozoites were harvested by centrifugation at the end of the logarithmic growth phase, at 72 hr of culture, then cells were resuspended with PBS-A (15 mM phosphate buffer, pH 7.0, 175 mM NaCl) and centrifuged at 250 g for 5 min at 4 C.

Ameba fractions

Entamoeba histolytica trophozoites were lysed by homogenization with a glass tissue grinder in 10 mM Tris-HCl buffer, pH 7.5, containing 10 mM iodoacetamide or 100 uM E-64. Samples were centrifuged at 4 C for 3 hr at 112,000 g. In some experiments, the pellet was washed twice by centrifugation at 112,000 g for 1 hr, using Tris-HCl buffer containing 0.5 mM iodoacetamide or 5 uM E-64.

Plasma and internal membrane fractions were puriﬁed by a previously modiﬁed (Calderon and Avila, 1986) method (Aley et al., 1980). Tro- phozoites (108) were harvested and washed twice then were treated with 100 ug/ml Concanavalin A to stabilize plasma membrane. After 5 min at 4 C, trophozoites were centrifuged and homogenized by 18-20 strokes in the presence of yodoacetamide (10 mM) or E-64 (100 uM) as cysteine protease inhibitors. Plasma membrane and internal mem- brane preparations were obtained in sucrose/mannitol and sucrose gra- dients. The modiﬁcations to the original procedure (Aley et al., 1980) were as follows: Concanavalin A was assayed at 100 ug/ml instead of 500 ug/ml to reduce cell damage, and cysteine protease inhibitors were used instead of phenylmethyl sulfonyl ﬂuoride because most proteolytic activities in amebas are thiol-dependent. Otherwise, the Aleys procedure was carefully followed in all details, including the ratio of ameba num- ber to solution volumes.

To obtain ameba fractions by differential centrifugation, the method used by Matayoshi et al. (1989) to pellet spirosomes from E. coli was modiﬁed. The ﬁnal protocol was as follows: trophozoites were lysed with a glass tissue grinder at 4 >< 106 cells/ml in an isotonic buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl; pH 7.4; 0.2 M mannitol; 50 mM sucrose; 10 mM KCl; 1 mM EDTA, and 10 mM iodoacetamide or 100 uM E-64). Cell ex- tracts were centrifuged successively at 10,000 (10 min), and 17,500 g (90 min), to pellet nuclear, heavy, and light microsomal fractions (17,500-g fractions). The 17,500-g supernatant was centrifuged 1 hr at 160,000 g. All pellets were resuspended in 500 ul of isotonic buffer. The reactivity with the antibody against L. lactis ADHE, the acid phos- phatase activity, and the NADi- and NADPi-dependent ADH activities were determined in all fractions. The ADH activities were determined the same day that fractions were obtained.
