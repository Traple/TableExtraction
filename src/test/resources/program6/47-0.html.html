High NAD-dependent alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) activity was found in the cytoplasm when a membrane- bound, quinoprotein, ADH-deﬁcient mutant strain of Acetobacter pasteurianus SKU1108 was grown on ethanol. Two NAD-dependent ADHs were separated and puriﬁed from the supernatant fraction of the cells. One (ADH I) is a trimer, consisting of an identical subu- nit of 42 kDa, while the other (ADH II) is a homodimer, having a subunit of 31 kDa. One of the two ADHs, ADH II, easily lost the activity during the column chro- matographies, which could be stabilized by the addition of DTT and MgClz in the column buffer. ADH I but not ADH II contained approximately one zinc atom per subunit. The N-terminal amino acid analysis indicated that ADH I and ADH II have homology to the long- chain and short-chain ADH families, respectively. ADH I showed a preference for primary alcohols, while ADH II had a preference for secondary alcohols. The two ADHs showed clear difference in their kinetics on ethanol, acetaldehyde, NAD, and NADH. The physio- logical function of both ADH I and ADH II are also discussed.

Key words: acetic acid bacteria; Acetobacter pasteu- rianus; quinoprotein alcohol de- hydrogenase; NAD-dependent alcohol de- hydrogenase

Acetobacter species are well known as vinegar producers, which have been shown to produce acetic acid from ethanol by two sequential reactions of membrane-bound alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH), localized on the periplasmic side of the membrane.” The membrane- bound ADH is a typical quinoprotein, having pyr-

roloquinoline quinone (PQQ) as the prosthetic group, and classiﬁed into type III quinoprotein ADH, which is unique in acetic acid bacteria. Recent- ly, a thermotolerant strain, Acetobacter pasteurian us SKU1108 (re-identiﬁed based on 16S rDNA se- quences), was isolated from pineapple in Thailand based on its ability to produce a large amount of acetic acid and to tolerate high concentrations of acetic acid and ethanol even at higher temperatures.” In order to elucidate the role of quinoprotein ADH in such a thermotolerant strain, we obtained a mutant designated as CN6-2 that has complete loss in ethanol-oxidizing ability and acetic acid resistance, by using N-methyl-N’-nitro-N-nitrosoguanidine (NTG) treatment. During the analysis of the mutant, we found that the mutant strain grows very well in medium containing ethanol, despite having no ability to oxidize ethanol, and that two NAD-dependent ADHs, present in a small amount in the wild-type strain, are increased dramatically in the cytoplasm when cultured in the medium containing ethanol. Thus, the mutant strain seemed to induce two differ- ent NAD-dependent ADHs to survive in a medium containing ethanol when the quinoprotein ADH is defective (unpublished data).

NAD(P)-dependent ADHs are widely distributed in microorganisms, plants, and animals. They can be divided into three groups according to their struc- tures.” Group I is a zinc-dependent, long-chain ADH family, containing approximately 350 amino acid residues per subunit, and consisting of dimeric or tetrameric structures. Group II is a zinc-independent, short-chain ADH containing approximately 250 residues per subunit. There is no evidence for a metal requirement in any of this group so far characterized.
