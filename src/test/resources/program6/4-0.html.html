Molecular Enzymology Group Colloquium Organized and Edited by P. C. Engel (University of Sheffield)

Coenzyme binding in alcohol dehydrogenase

HANS EKLUND

Department of Molecular Biology, Swedish University of Agricultural Sciences, Biomedical Center, S- 751 24 Uppsala, Sweden

Summary

Crystallographic investigations of horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase have demonstrated that NAD is not a passive participant in the redox reactions catalysed by the enzyme. On the molecular level NAD acts as an activator which induces an active form of the enzyme. This is mediated by a large conformational change, making the active site de- hydrated and by providing one part of the substrate-binding cleft. The catalytic events, substrate binding, inhibitor bind- ing and the role of the catalytic zinc ion are discussed in rela- tion to the role of NAD. Human alcohol dehydrogenase isoenzymes which have very different substrate specificities are discussed in relation to sequence differences.

Introduction

Alcohol dehydrogenase (EC. 1.1.1.1) is present in large quantities in mammalian livers (LADH). It catalyses the redox equilibrium between a large number of alcohols and aldehydes/ketones (for a review see Branden et al., 1975). isoenzymes are present (Jornvall et al., 1987). In human livers five different subunits have been characterized, which can be present in different combinations as a number of iso- enzymes. In horse liver three isoenzyme subunits have been found.

The most studied of these is isoenzyme E (‘ethanol active’) of horse LADH. which has been a favourite forenzymic studies (Bréindén et al., 1975). The crystallographic investi- gations described in this paper have been made exclusively on this isoenzyme.

Crystallographic‘ investigations

The crystallographic investigations of horse LADH have been made on five different crystal forms of the enzyme, (Eklund et al., 1976, 1981; Plapp et al., 1983; Horjales et al., 1987). The apoenzyme crystallizes in an orthorhombic form. Several ternary complexes of the enzyme with NAD* or NADH crystallize in triclinic and/or monoclinic forms. LADH with modified lysine side-chains crystallizes in a dif- ferent monoclinic form. The three-dimensional structure of LADH

LADH is a dimer where each subunit is divided by a long cleft into two domains, termed coenzyme binding and cata- lytic domains. The coenzyme-binding domain is built up by six consecutive (1)6 units. The only deviation from this symmetry is that the last of the helices is short and has partly been replaced by an extended chain forming one of the sub- unit interactions. The catalytic domain is built up by a com- plicated pleated sheet structure and three a-helices. The pleated sheet is mainly antiparallel, partly orthogonal. Each catalytic domain binds two zinc ions. One is bound close to the subunit border and stabilizes one part of the structure. The other zinc ion is bound in the centre of the active site by two Cys and one His ligand, and is important for catalysis.

Conformational changes

The crystallographic investigations have shown that NAD transforms the enzyme from an inactive to an active species. The apoenzyme has two long deep clefts dividing the dimer into three parts, where the central part is formed by the two coenzyme-binding domains with the catalytic domains on each side of the elongated molecule (Fig. 1). When NAD binds in this cleft, the main effect is that the domains close up around NAD. The major feature is a rotation of the large catalytic domain by 10° (Fig. 2). This is essentially a rigid body rotation and the internal structure of the domain is pre- served. The rotation axis passes close to the two covalent connections between the domains. One of these connections is a buried 3m-helix two turns long, the other contains one turn of a left-handed helix with two Gly residues.

There is also a small, but significant, rotation of the smaller coenzyme-binding domain in the opposite direction of the catalytic domain. This rotation is about 1.5°, but the two coenzyme-binding domains, which are homologously interacting with each other, rotate in opposite directions. The rearrangement of the co-enzyme-binding domains relative each other is 3°. This rotation changes the position of a few NAD-binding residues, especially Asp-223.
