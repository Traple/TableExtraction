RESULTS

Purification of sec-Alcohol Dehydrogenase “A"

Cells grown on a complex medium (peptone, glucose, yeast extract) (Kroutil et al., 1997) were disrupted using a bead mill and the cell-free extract was applied to DEAE- cellulose in a batch procedure to remove interfering carotenoids and mucous substances. After an initial HIC- chromatography step (Phenyl Sepharose), no activity was detected in the absence of cofactor. All fractions were tested for their ability to oxidize 2-octanol and l-phenyl ethanol in presence of different cofactors (NAD+, NADP+, FMN, FAD and PQQ). Three fractions showed activity upon addition of NADP+, two of them showed enhanced activity in the presence of NAD+. To investigate the number of alcohol dehydrogenases present in the organism, native gel electrophoresis using an activity staining method (Grell et al., 1965; Barret et al., 1980) employing 2-octanol as substrate was performed, revealing the presence of (at least) seven different NADVNADH-dependent sec-alcohol dehydrogenases. In order to identify the desired organic solvent tolerant ADH(s) among competing dehydrogenases at an early stage of the purification protocol, the oxidation- assay was performed in the presence and absence of 10% v/v acetone. An analogous assay was performed for the reduction mode using 2-octanone and acetophenone as

substrate and NADH as cofactor in presence and absence of 2-propanol. For further purification, only the fractions showing enhanced activity in the presence of acetone and 2-propanol were used. After HIC, the fraction of interest was tested for its activity at different acetone and 2-propanol concentrations. To our delight, it showed enhanced initial activity with increasing 2-propanol concentration up to 70% v/v (see Fig. 1). Even at 80% v/v the enzyme still showed an activity comparable to 70% v/v i-PrOH. Similar properties were found in the presence of acetone at a concentration up to 50% v/v.

Purification of the organic solvent tolerant protein fraction was continued after HIC employing ion exchange chromatography (UNO Q6) and affinity chromatography on Blue Sepharose (Table I), a special stationary phase for NAD(H)-dependent enzymes. However, the organic-sol- vent tolerant alcohol dehydrogenase did not bind and was eluted within the void volume. Nevertheless, all other competing ADHs did bind, which facilitated separation.

After a final purification step using size exclusion chromatography (Superdex 200), the pure enzyme was obtained in 8% yield (Fig. 2). Since the protein is stable towards elevated acetone concentration it was termed alcohol dehydrogenase “A” (standing for Acetone-stable).
