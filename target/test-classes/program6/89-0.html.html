Gene Cloning and Biochemical Characterization of an Alcohol Dehydrogenase from Euglena gracilisl

HELGA N. PALMA-GUTIERREZ, JOSE SALUD RODRIGUEZ-ZAVALA, RICARDO JASSO-CHAVEZ,

RAFAEL MORENO-SANCHEZ and EMMA SAAVEDRA Departamento de Bioquzinica, Instituto Nacional de Cardiologziz, Tlalpan, México D.F. 14080, México

ABSTRACT. Euglena gracilis is a freshwater free-living organism able to grow with ethanol as carbon source; to facilitate this me- tabolism several alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) activities have been detected. We report the gene cloning, over-expression, and bio- chemical characterization of a medium-chain NAD+-dependent ADH from E. gracilis (EgADH). The enzyme’s amino acid sequence displayed the highest percentages of similarity and identity with ADHs of bacteria and fungi. In the predicted three-dimensional model, all the residues involved in Zn”, cofactor, and substrate binding were conserved. A conventional signal peptide for import into mitochondria could not be clearly identiﬁed. The protein of 37 kDa was over-expressed, puriﬁed to homogeneity, and kinetically characterized. The enzyme’s optimal pH was 7.0 for ethanol oxidation displaying a Vm of 11.7 i 3.6 U/mg protein and a Km of 3.2 i 0.7 mM for this substrate. Isopropanol and isopentanol were also utilized, although with less efﬁciency. It showed speciﬁcity for NAD+ with a Km value of 0.39 i 0.1 mM and Mg2+ or Zn2+ were essential for activity. The recombinant EgADH reported here may help to elucidate the roles that different ADHs have on the metabolism of short- and long-chain alcohols in this microorganism. Key Words. ADH medium-chain dehydrogenase/reductase, Euglenozoa, microbial alcohol metabolism.

HE protist Euglena gracilis is a freshwater free-living organ-

ism of potential biotechnological interest because of its high capability to resist and to accumulate heavy metals, such as cad- mium, zinc, mercury, and chromium (for reviews, see Cervantes et al. 2006; Rodriguez-Zavala et al. 2007). This organism has also often been used in evolutionary studies because of the history of multiple events of lateral gene transfer and gene fusion in its ge- nome (Ahmadinej ad, Dagan, and Martin 2007).

Euglena gracilis can live under auto- or heterotrophic condi- tions; under the latter, its metabolic plasticity enables it to grow on a diverse variety of oxidable substrates: DL-lactate, gluta- mateimalate, and ethanol are the most utilized substrates for its growth under laboratory conditions (Cook 1968). Euglena gracilis constitutively expresses specific enzymes for the oxidation of D- and L-lactate that are mainly involved in energy metabolism during the late stages of growth (J asso-Chavez et al. 2005; J asso- Chavez and Moreno-Sanchez 2003; lasso-Chavez, Torres- Marquez, and Moreno-Sanchez 2001). On the other hand, an NADP+-glutamate decarboxylase (Tokunaga, Nakano, and Kitaoka 1979) and an NAD+-malate dehydrogenase (Davis and Merret 1973) have roles in the y-aminobutyrate shunt and Krebs cycle involved in glutamate metabolism, respectively. For alcohol utilization, several alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) isoenzymes have been reported in this microorganism, although their physi- ological roles have not yet been well established.

Early reports have documented NAD+ (Hurlbert and Ritten- berg 1962; Mego and Farb 1974) and NADP+—ADH activities (Danforth and Hunter 1966; Eshleman and Danforth 1964; Wilson and Danforth 1958) in E. gracilis grown on ethanol as carbon source. In addition, a microsomal ethanol-oxidizing system (cyto- chrome P-450) was also found in E. gracilis grown on lactate (Julistiono and Briand 1992). Ono et al. (1995) showed that eth- anol induced a signiﬁcant increase in the activities of a mi- tochondrial NAD+—ADH and the glyoxylate cycle enzymes isocitrate lyase and malate synthase. Eug- lena gracilis can also grow on l-hexanol as carbon source, a con- dition under which the cells show two ADH isoenzymes: an NAD+—ADH speciﬁc for short-length alcohols and an inducible NADP+—ADH speciﬁc for long-chain alcohols, which might play an important role in the anaerobic synthesis of wax esters or in the central metabolism (Munir et al. 2002).

Although ethanol is not a natural substrate for Euglena, it is clear that some ADH isoforms reported so far seem to be consti- tutive whereas others are inducible under many different growth conditions. An NAD+—ADH shows a signiﬁcant activity increase during the early stage of growth (24 h) when the cells are cultured on ethanol, glutamateimalate, or in combination (Rodriguez- Zavala, Ortiz-Cruz, and Moreno-Sanchez 2006). Intriguingly, af- ter 48h of growth and paramylon accumulation, another 4-fold increase in the ADH-activity was found only in the gluta- mateimalate condition (Rodriguez-Zavala et al. 2006). These re- sults suggested that several ADHs are involved in the utilization of the different carbon sources used by this protist. In this work, a recombinant E. gracilis NAD+—ADH is characterized.
