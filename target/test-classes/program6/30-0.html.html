ABSTRACT The ionization properties of the ac- tive site residues in Drosophila lebanonensis alcohol dehydrogenase (DADH) were investigated theoreti- cally by using an approach developed to account for multiple locations of the hydrogen atoms of the titrat- able and polar groups. The electrostatic calculations show that (a) the protonationldeprotonation transi- tion of the binary complex of DADH is related to the coupled ionization of Tyr151 and Lys155 in the active site and (b) the pH dependence of the proton abstrac- tion is correlated with a reorganization of the hydro- gen bond network in the active site. On this basis, a proton relay mechanism for substrate dehydrogena- tion is proposed in which the O2’ ribose hydroxyl group from the coenzyme has a key role and acts as a switch. The proton relay chain includes the active site catalytic residues, as well as a chain of eight water molecules that connects the active site with the bulk solvent. INTRODUCTION

Enzymes belonging to the short-chain dehydrogenase/ reductase (SDR) superfamily have been identiﬁed in most organisms, including bacteria, insects, and human. SDR enzymes vary largely in their metabolic function and hence their substrates: alcohols, polyols, steroids, and prostaglandins among others. SDR enzymes display a wide range of hydrogenation/dehydrogenation/epimerase activities, using NAD(P)(H) as coenzyme.

The three-dimensional structure has been solved for a number of SDR enzymes, among them alcohol dehydroge- nase (ADH) from Drosophila leban0nensis.1’2 The typical SDR enzyme has a single-domain subunit structure of ~27 kDa, which is folded into a central seven- to eight-stranded B-sheet ﬂanked on each side by three ot-helices. The N-terminal part constitutes the coenzyme-binding region, showing the largest homology between SDR enzymes. As expected from the various functions of the individual SDR enzymes, the homology of their C-terminal regions is low.

The enzyme Drosophila alcohol dehydrogenase (DADH; EC 1.1.1.1) catalyzes the oxidation of primary and second- ary alcohols to aldehydes and ketones, using NAD+ as coenzyme. DADH is one of the best studied SDR members and can be used as a model enzyme in this family. Detailed kinetic studies of the reaction mechanism of DADH?’ are consistent with a compulsory ordered pathway, where the coenzyme binds prior to the substrate? Studies on the pH dependence of the different reaction steps have shown that a group in the active site is ionized (pK 7.1, in D. lebanonensis ADH). This group also regulates the kon velocity of alcohols and alcohol competitive inhibitors, such as pyrazole, whereas aldehyde reduction is pH inde- pendent.5

Despite the low sequence identity in the substrate- binding region of SDR enzymes, there are three highly conserved amino acid residues: Tyr151 (strictly con- served), Lys155, and Ser138 (both conserved in most of the SDR enzymes). Crystallographic studies have revealed that these residues constitute a catalytic triad in the active site of SDR enzymes. The hydroxyl groups of Tyr151 and Ser138 interact via hydrogen bonds with the hydroxyl/ carbonyl group of the Ca carbon of the substrate, and the Lys155 amino group interacts with the hydroxyl groups of the NAD+ ribose moiety. In the apo form of DADH, the substrate and coenzyme hydroxyl positions are occupied by water molecules that form hydrogen bonds to each of the residues of the catalytic triad. Site-directed mutagen- esis studies in several SDR enzymes have also shown that all three catalytic triad residues are essential for activity.
