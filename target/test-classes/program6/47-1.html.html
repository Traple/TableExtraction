NAD-Dependent Alcohol Dehydrogenases from Acetobacter Strain 959

Group III is an “iron activated” ADHs of which the subunit consists of about 385 amino acid residues. Many organisms have been reported to contain multiple NAD-dependent ADHs, the physiological roles of which have sometimes proven diﬁicult to disentangle.“ Candida guilliermondii has been shown to have two NAD-dependent ADHs, one of which appears to be involved in the production of ethanol from acetaldehyde while the other plays a main role in the oxidation of ethanol to acetaldehyde.” In acetic acid bacteria, ethanol is oxidized extensively by the membrane-bound quinoprotein ADH and only low NAD-dependent ADH activity is detected in the cytoplasm.” There has been no report on NAD- dependent ADHs in acetic acid bacteria so far. Therefore, it is important to know the characteristics of two NAD-dependent ADHs in the mutant strain of Acetobacter species.

In this work, we describe the puriﬁcation and characterization of two NAD-dependent ADHs from the soluble fraction of A. pasteurianus CN6-2. Characterization of the two ADHs indicated that one (ADH I) is of the long-chain family and shows a preference for primary alcohols and another (ADH II) of the short-chain family shows a preference for secondary alcohols.

Materials and Methods

Chemicals. NAD and NADH were obtained from Sigma. All other chemicals were of reagent grade and obtained from commercial sources.

Bacterial strains and growth conditions. A. pasteu- rianus CN6-2, which lacks quinoprotein ADH activi- ty, was used in this study. The strain was maintained on potato medium“) containing 1.5% agar. For culti- vation, bacteria were inoculated into 5 ml of the potato medium and incubated for 18 h at 30°C with shaking at 200 rpm. The seed culture was transferred to 100 ml of the same medium in a 500-ml Erlenmay- er ﬂask and cultivated for 24 h. Then, the culture was transferred to 1 liter of a main culture medium (5 g of yeast extract, 5 g of Polypeptone, 5 g of glycerol, and 5 g of glucose in tap water), in which 4% ethanol (v /v) was added aseptically, in a 3-liter Erlenmayer ﬂask, and cultivated for 48 h.

Preparation of cell-free extract. Bacterial cells were harvested by centrifugation at 9,000><g for 10 min and washed twice with ice-cold 50 mM potas- sium phosphate buffer (KPB), pH 7.5, containing 1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT) and 1 mM MgClz. The cell paste was suspended in the same buffer (1 g of wet weight /4 ml of buffer), and passed twice through a French pressure cell press (American Instruments Co., Silver Spring, MD., U.S.A.) at 16,000 psi. Cell debris was removed by centrifugation at 9,000 >< g for

10 min and the supernatant was ultracentrifuged at 150,000><g for 90 min. The resulting supernatant was used for further puriﬁcation of enzymes.

Enzyme assay. Activities of the two NAD-depen- dent ADHs were measured, at 25°C, spec- trophotometrically at 340 nm by following the for- mation or disappearance of NADH. The formation of NADH follows the oxidation of ethanol to acetal- dehyde (forward reaction), while the disappearance of NADH does the reduction of acetaldehyde to ethanol (reverse reaction). For NAD-dependent ADH I, the reaction mixture (total 1 ml) for the for- ward reaction contained 100 mM glycine-NaOH (pH 8.5), 100 mM ethanol, and 0.1 mM NAD, while the assay mixture for the reverse reaction (1 ml) did 100 mM KPB (pH 5.5), 10 mM acetaldehyde, and 0.1mM NADH. For NAD-dependent ADH II, the forward reaction mixture (1 ml) contained 100 mM glycine-NaOH (pH 8.5), 100 mM ethanol, and 0.1mM NAD, while that for the reverse reaction (1 ml) did 100 mM 2-morpholinoethanesulfonic acid (MES) buffer (pH 6.0), 100 mM acetaldehyde, and 0.1mM NADH. The reaction was started by the addition of enzyme solution. One unit of activity is deﬁned as the amount of enzyme that reduces or oxidizes 1 amol of NAD or NADH, respectively, per min at 25 °C, where a millimolar extinction coeﬁicient of 6.22 was used for the calculation.

Puriﬁcation of two NAD-dependent ADHs. All steps were done at 4°C. KPB (pH 7.5) of various con- centrations containing 1 mM DTT and 1 mM MgClz was used. The resulting supernatant after ultracen- trifugation was put directly on a DEAE-Toyopearl column (about lml of bed volume per 3mg of protein), which had been equilibrated with 50 mM KPB (pH 7.5). Under these conditions, most of the enzyme activity was not adsorbed onto the column. The combined pass-through fractions having the enzyme activity was dialyzed with 5 mM KPB (pH 7.5), and put on an alternative column of DEAE-Toyopearl, which had been equilibrated with the same buffer. The column was washed with the column buffer and then eluted by a linear gradient of 5 mM to 50 mM KPB (pH 7.5). NAD-dependent ADH activity was separated into two fractions. One, with an activity preference for primary alcohols (ADH I), was eluted at 35 mM KPB (pH 7.5), while another enzyme activity having a preference for secondary alcohols (ADH II) was not adsorbed to the column. The two NAD-dependent ADH activity fractions were combined separately.
