Such a feature, with frequent substitutions at the active site in species variants, is uncom- mon and relatively unique for alcohol dehydrogenases.

Variable segment V2 corresponds to the loop around the second zinc atom and an adjacent part. This segment is highly variable in all members of the alcohol dehydrogenase protein family. It also includes a part that is missing in some of the enzymes, e.g. yeast alcohol dehydrogenase, sorbitol dehydrogenase, and quinone reductase/f-crystallin (cf. fig- ures in [20, 21]). The present data show that this variability is also a feature within a single class (Fig. 4, top).

Variable segment V3 corresponds to the segment which participates in major subunit interactions. This segment has been noted in comparisons of classes [18] and found to ac- count for the failure of dimeric hybrids to form with subunits of different classes. As evidenced by the present data, this segment is also variable within a class. This suggests that the subunit inter-face variability is not limited to the function of class distinction. Thus, all variability patterns extend into a single class, complementing interpretations deduced from the class patterns.

In conclusion, the identities in patterns of variability be- tween the major vertebrate forms of one class and the dif- ferent forms of all classes reveal hitherto unknown correla- tions between class and species variability. The conservation of functionally and structurally critical residues delineate dis- tinctive features of the molecular architecture of alcohol de- hydrogenases in general. The comparisons highlight the rela- tionships between classes I and III (Table 4), confirming their ancestral connection [7, 8], and suggest that the other classes may also be derived from the class III line. Hence, the pre- sent results define the overall architecture for the enzyme, the basic relationships for class I, and point to similar but separate duplicatory origins for the other classes.
