DISCUSSION

The deactivation studies of free and immobilized crude LADH indicate the presence of two types of enzyme sub- units, present in relative amounts of 70 and 30%, with vastly different stabilities against exposure to 60°C. The same quantities of two subunits are evidenced by the active-site titration experiments, which demonstrate that 30% of LADH loses its activity upon immobilization to CNBr-Sepharose. Similarly, the EPR spectra of each spin-labeled enzyme preparation can be resolved into two components whose ratio is ca. 75 :25. The consistency of these values strongly suggests that each experiment is discerning the same two types of enzyme subunits.

The above evidence for enzyme heterogeneity is also in line with the conclusion from isoelectric focusing that the enzyme exists as several dimeric isozymes. Of these, the bands labeled in Figure 2 as E1 E1, E l E2, and E2 E2 comprise 96% of the total LADH sample and will be considered sepa- rately from the remaining 4%. As explained earlier, the rela- tive amounts of each band (54, 32, and 9.8%) are consistent with the pairings shown in the sixth column and the follow- ing percentages of each subunit: E, and 73%, and E2 and 27%. Thus, even considering the potential error introduced by not taking into account any ethanol-active enzyme con-

tributed by the remaining 4% LADH, these values are in close agreement with the other results.

Further definition of the enzyme subunits has been pro- vided by isoelectric focusing in the presence of 8.5M urea. Under these conditions, the isozymes separate into their constituent subunits. As shown in Figure 3, four bands were observed rather than the two which might be expected for E, and E2. These results may explain the subbands in the isoelectric focusing gel of Figure 2. Moreover, the top two bands in Figure 3 constitute 75% E-type protein while the bottom two bands make up 25%. Furthermore, as evi- denced by the results obtained with purified isozyme, the two enzyme subunits labeled E,’ and E f’ deactivate at the same rate [Fig. 4(b)] and produce the same EPR spec- trum when spin labeled (Fig. 7). Thus, with regard to their thermostability at 60°C, these two subunits are indistin- guishable and can be treated as a single entity. Similar com- ments apply for E2’ and E2".

The existence of multiple subunits which differ in their electrophoretic mobilities but exhibit similar thermostabili- ties could be explained in several ways. One possible source of subunit heterogeneity is varying degrees of deamida- tion, as has been observed with Neurospora cytochrome c .32 Also, removal of an amino acid from the carboxyl end of the protein has been reported to cause changes in the mi- gration of adolases from chicken,” but loss of the carboxyl amino acid had no effect on the electrophoretic migration of different lactate dehydrogenases.” Another possibility, suggested by Lutstorf and von Wartborg,” is the presence of conformationally different subunits which are sufficiently stable thermodynamically to form catalytically active dimers.
