DISCUSSION

A gene encoding a Zn-containing ADH from the protist E. gracilis was identiﬁed and its biochemical properties were established by over-expressing and characterizing the recombi- nant protein. Although the genome sequence of the chloroplast of E. gracilis was the ﬁrst to be completed from a unicellular organism (Hallick et al. 1993), less than 15 recombinant proteins encoded by nuclear genes have been characterized for this species. The recent release of an EST database for E. gracilis very probably will advance our understanding, at the genomic and biochemical levels, the metabolic and biotechnological potential of this microorganism.

The ADH enzymes are widely distributed throughout all taxo- nomic groups and three major categories have been identiﬁed: (1) oxidases that catalyze the irreversible oxidation of alcohols, such as methanol oxidase of Hansenula polymorpha; (2) the NAD(P)+- independent ADHs that use pyrroloquinoline quinone, heme or F420 as cofactor; and (3) the NAD(P)+-dependent ADHs (Reid and Fewson 1994). The latter category contains three major su- perfamilies: (1) the Fe-activated ADHs; (2) the short-chain (250 residues) ADHs, which do not require a metallic ion as cofactor; and (3) the commonly represented medium chain (350-375 amino acid residues) Zn-containing ADHs (MDR) (Riveros-Rosas et al. 2003). The amino acid length displayed and its NAD+ cofactor dependency suggest that the EgADH can be grouped within the MDR category.

Although the analysis of the 5’-UTR did not show any positive match with reported organelle importing sequences of other or- ganisms, the SWISS-MODEL software found a good alignment of the last ﬁve amino acids of this region (15 nucleotides before the ATG codon at nt position 76 considered for the expression of the protein) with the template sequence and included them in the model. This observation may suggest that part of the protein pri- mary sequence might be missing (e. g. an import peptide signal) or that the mitochondrial protein importing sequence in Euglena is different from those reported in other organisms.

Analysis of the model showed that all the essential cysteine residues for the stabilization of the catalytic and the structural Zn2+ were located at adequate positions and distances to exert their function. Although the model was generated with the cata- lytic Zn2+ near NADJ", EgADH showed a clear dependence for catalysis on Mg”; thus, it may be possible that in this enzyme, Zn2+ is replaced by Mg2+ as the catalytic metal. Another scenario may be the presence of both metals at the catalytic site in this enzyme; in that case, Mg2+ might be stabilized by the phosphate residues located at the middle portion of NAD+ as occurs in alde- hyde dehydrogenase 2 structure (Perez-Miller and Hurley 2003) and not by cysteines, which preferentially bind Zn2+. The ADHs from Amycolatopsis methanolica and Mycobacterium gastri con- tain Zn2+ and Mg2+ in the subunit, and Mg2+ ions are essential for NAD(P)+ binding and stabilization (Bystrykh et a1, 1993).

An aspartate residue is presumably located at 2.64A from the 2’-OH of the NAD+ adenosine ribose of the NAD+; the presence of the Asp negative charge at this distance may promote a repul- sion effect against the 2’-phosphate found in the adenosine ribose ring of the NADP+ molecule. This negatively charged residue is proposed to be responsible for determining the coenzyme speci- ﬁcity in other ADHs (Sun and Plapp 1992). The residue Thr40 that establishes a hydrogen bond with the substrate rests at 2.62 A from the substrate analogue ethanediol. In theory, this distance does not sufﬁce to form a highly stable hydrogen bond; however, a similar distance is also found between these residues in the crystal struc- ture of the ADH from P. aeruginosa.

The enzyme exhibited the highest speciﬁc activity in the buffer mix despite the presence of several salt components including imidazole, an inhibitor of other ADHs. The K m values of Eg ADH were similar to those of most reported Zn-containing ADHs found in the BRENDA database (http://www.brenda-enzymes.info/) (Etoh, 0.15—6 mM; NADJ", 7-200 uM). However, an unusual ﬁnd- ing was its dependence on Mg2+ ions for activity. De Bolle et al. (1997) noted that Mg2+ is a non-essential activator of the ADH1 of S. cerevisiae by preventing the dissociation of its tetrameric structure. Moreover, it has been reported that Mg2+ is necessary for the NAD+—ADH activity from Bacillus methanolicus (Hektor, Kloosterman, and Dijkhuizen 2002); the R-speciﬁc ADH from Lactobacillus brevis is an NADP+-dependent enzyme belonging to the short-chain dehydrogenase/reductase superfamily whose activity is completely dependent on Mg2+ (where activation is structural more than catalytic), and this effect is a unique feature for this enzyme (Nieﬁnd et al. 2003). The decreased EgADH ac- tivity observed in 100 mM PPi buffer might also be caused by Mg2+ chelation. These data indicate that EgADH is peculiar with respect to the essential Mg2+ requirement. Unfortunately, due to the high instability of EgADH, its oligomeric structure could not be determined, and the effect of Mg2+on stabilization remains to be elucidated.
