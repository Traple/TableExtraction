It is generally accepted that the Class I enzymes in the liver are responsible for ethanol metabolism, but the Class II and IV enzymes are also active on ethanol, and all of these forms have broad substrate specificities, suggesting that the enzymes may have multiple functions [12-18].

The Class I enzymes have different speciﬁcities. Human and monkey ADHlA (oc)* are most active on secondary alcohols [20,2l], and only human ADHlC (y) has good activity on steroids [22]. There are two forms of the horse enzyme, which differ at ten amino acid residues; ADHlS (SS) is very active on steroids [23,24], whereas ADHlE (EE) has very low activity [25]. The rat and rabbit enzymes are also active on steroids [26,27]. Muta- genesis studies of these enzymes show that speci- ficity for steroids is not based on a common determinant in the active site, but rather is due to the constellation of several amino acid residues [24,25,28]. For instance, changing Serl l7 to Gly in horse ADHlE, in order to match the residue found in human ADHlC, does not increase activity on typical steroid substrates [25]. Nevertheless, metabolism of ethanol can affect the metabolism of steroids. Chronic drinking alters the balance of reproductive hormones [30]. Tes- tosterone levels in alcoholic men tend to be lower, resulting in feminization [3l—33]. In rats, there is an increased production of estradiol during etha- nol metabolism [33]. The levels of allopregnano- lone (5oc-pregnan-3oc-ol-20-one), a neuroactive steroid, increase significantly in rats given an intoxicating dose of ethanol [34]. These effects of ethanol may be indirect, due to a change in the redox state of the liver [30,35], since the ratio of NADHlNAD+ increases during ethanol oxida- tion. Alternatively, human ADHIC could directly catalyze the kinetically efficient reaction where the oxidation of ethanol is coupled to the reduction of a steroid, thereby bypassing the slow step of coenzyme dissociation. Such a reaction has been demonstrated for ADH4 (o), where all-trans- retinal is reduced with ethanol [36]. In cells, the hydrogens from ethanol are readily incorporated by a rapid exchange of reducing equivalents into acetaldehyde and other metabolites [37,38]. The reactivity of human ADHlC with various steroids needs to be evaluated in order to assess the potential roles of the enzyme in alcoholism.

Previous studies showed that Soc-dihydrotestos- terone and testosterone are potent inhibitors of human ADHlC, noncompetitive against varied concentrations of ethanol or NAD+ and thus considered to be ‘allosteric’ inhibitors [22,39]. The mechanism of this inhibition needs further study since the form of enzyme to which the steroid binds was not established. Recent studies have shown that the ‘negative’ cooperativity of human ADHlC*2 observed in the kinetics for ethanol saturation is due to the formation of an abortive enzyme-NADH-ethanol complex that dissociates more rapidly than the enzyme—NADH complex and thus produces activation of the enzyme with higher concentrations of ethanol [40]. The inhibi- tory steroids also could bind to the enzyme- NADH complex.
