RESULTS

Intracellular Localization. The subcellular distribution of ADH and its inactivator was investigated by sucrose density gradient centrifugation of extracts made with osmotic protection. In co- leoptiles from aerobically germinated 7-d-old rice seedlings, al- most all of the ADH activity was detected in the supernatant fractions (Fig. 1A). When aerobic 5-d-old rice seedlings were subjected to anaerobic treatment for 2 d, the ADH activity of the coleoptile extract was considerably larger than that of the aerobic coleoptile extract, but again no signiﬁcant activity was present in the particulate fractions of the gradient (Fig. IA).

The marker enzymes of mitochondria, fumarase (Fig. lB), and antimycin A-sensitive NADH Cyt c reductase (Fig. lE) were present in a fraction at peak density l. 16 g/cma, and the peak shifted slightly to 1.17 g/cma due to the anaerobic treatment. This shift may coincide with the fact that the mitochondrial ultrastruc- ture, its shape and size, change on transfer of aerobically grown rice seedlings to conditions of anoxia (18).

Catalase, the marker enzyme of microbodies, is found in a fraction at peak density 1.23 g/cma as well as in the supernatant fraction and near the density 1.17 g/cma (Fig. l, C and F).

The ADH inactivator was detected mainly in a fraction at peak density 1.13 g/cm” and to a lesser degree near the top of the gradient (Fig. lD). The inactivator activity of the coleoptile from seedlings exposed to anoxia is smaller than that of the aerobic coleoptile (15) but it behaved similarly during centrifugation (Fig. ID). Nevertheless, when Mg“ was included in the gradient (9, l3), both components were recovered together at peak density 1.16 g/ cm3 (Fig. 2). No loss of inactivator occurred during centrifugation on gradients.

The inactivator activity in peak fractions from the sucrose gradient was not enhanced after disruption of the membranes either by osmotic shock or by addition of detergent (Table I). When the rice coleoptiles were homogenized in grinding buffer without sucrose and the homogenate was centrifuged at 27,000g, about 20% of inhibitor activity was recovered in the supernatant fraction. This activity, however, declines on prolonged centrifu- gation and, furthermore, when (NH4)2SO4 was added to bring the concentration to 35% of saturation, all of the inactivator was salted out. The inactivator is solubilized by addition of 0.1% Triton X- 100 with 80% recovery, but high concentrations of Triton X-100 lead to inactivation. Deoxycholate (1%) solubilizes the inactivator but cholate inactivates.

Properties of the ADH Inactivator. The inactivator shows op- timum activity at acidic pH (Fig. 3). It is unstable at 30°C and the addition of l0 mM MgClg, 30% glycerol, 10% acetone or 0.2 M KCl further decrease the stability. However, addition of EDTA or dialysis signiﬁcantly increase the stability. Ninety-eight % of the inactivator was lost after boiling for 3 min. The inactivator at- tacked ADH from oats, wheat, corn, soybean, pea, and water- melon seeds (Table II) as well as yeast ADH (Table III).
