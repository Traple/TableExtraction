A Proton Relay Model for Substrate Dehydrogenation by DADH

At pH > 8, Tyr151 is half protonated and may serve as a base to attract a proton from the alcohol hydroxyl group. At the same time, the preferred protonated state of this residue acts as proton donor to the O2â ribose hydroxyl group, which can have its hydrogen with equal probability at the Tyr151 and at the Lys 155 sides. Thus, after proton abstraction from the alcohol substrate and protonation of Tyr151, the hydrogen can be transported form the Tyr151 hydroxyl group via the ribose hydroxyl group to Lys155. Lys155 is also half protonated, so that a proton donated by the NAD+ ribose OH can be accepted and released of the active site with equal probability. In this way, the hydroxyl group of Tyr151, the O2â ribose hydroxyl, and the Lys155 e-amino group form a proton relay chain [Fig. 1(C)].

With decreasing pH, the population of the ribose hy- droxyl hydrogen position pointing to Lys 155 decreases and tends to zero; furthermore, the population of the rotamer that does not share its proton neither with Tyr151 nor with Lys155 increases, resulting in a discontinued proton relay chain. Thus, the proton abstraction process from the substrate hydroxyl group does not take place although Tyr151 O1] is proton acceptor in the hydrogen bond with W144 [Fig. 1(B)]. It follows that a key participant of the mechanism of DADH is the NAD+ ribose hydroxyl group. This hydroxyl group functions as a switch in the proton relay chain and is regulated by the protonation/deprotona- tion equilibrium of the coupled Tyr15 1/Lys 155 pair.
