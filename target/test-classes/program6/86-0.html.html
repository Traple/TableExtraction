On the basis of the different substrate speciﬁcities of LADH and yeast alcohol dehydrogenase (YADH), a procedure has been developed to evaluate the enantiomeric product composition of ketone reductions. Z-Butanone and Z-pentanone reductions revealed (S)-2-butanoi (86%) and (5')-2-pentanol (95%) as the major products.

4. The observed enantioselectivity implies the existence of two productive ternary complexes; E - NADH - (pro-S) 2-butanone and E - NADH - (pro-R) Z-butanone. All rate constants describing the kinetic pathways of the system (S)-2-butanol, (R)-2-butanol/2-butanone have been determined. These data have been used to estimate the expected enantiomer product composition of 2-butanone reductions using apparent few/Km values for the two different ternary-complex configurations of 2-butanone. Additionally, these data have been used for computer simulations of the corresponding reaction cycles. Calculated, simulated and experimental data were found to be in good agreement. Thus, the system (S)-2-butanol, (R)-2-butanol/2-butanone is the ﬁrst example of a LADH- catalyzed reaction for which the stereochemical course could be described in terms of rate constants of the underlying mechanism.

5. The effects of Co(II) substitution on the different steps of the kinetic pathway have been investigated. The free energy of activation is higher for alcohol oxidation and lower for ketone reduction when catalyzed by Co(II)LADH in comparison to Zn(II)LADH. However, the free energies of binding are affected by metal substitution in such a way that the enantioselectivity of ketone reduction is not signiﬁcantly changed by the substitution of Co(II) for Zn(ll).

6. Evaluation of the data shows that substrate specificity and stereoselectivity result from combination of the free energies of binding and activation, with differences in binding energies as the dominating factors. In this regard, the interactions of substrate molecules with the protein moiety are dominant over the interactions with

the catalytic metal ion.

Horse-liver alcohol dehydrogenase (LADH), which cata- lyzes the reversible oxidation of primary and secondary alcohols [1, 2], exhibits very broad substrate speciﬁcity. Nu- merous investigations have characterized the substrate-bind- ing capacity of LADH (for review see [3]).

In contrast to alcohol dehydrogenase from yeast (YADH) which exclusively accepts (S)-(+)-2-butanol, (S)-(+)-2- pentanol and (.S’)-(+)-2-octanol, LADH also accepts the (R)- (—)-enantiomers of these alcohols and 3-pentanol which it is not a substrate for the yeast enzyme [4, 5]. These and other secondary alcohols have been the subject of several investi- gations including alcohol dehydrogenases from Thermoanae- robacter ethanolicus [6] and human liver [7].

Systematic variations in the substrate structure (e. g. sub- stituted cyclohexanones, decalones [8—~11] and monocyclic mesodiols [12]) have lead to a topological description of the substrate-binding pocket of LADH with increasing degrees of reﬁnement [8 ~14]. Based on the well-known X-ray structure of the major ethanol-active isoenzyme [15], the substrate-bind- ing site has been the subject of detailed studies using complexes of the enzyme with coenzyme, coenzyme analogs and sub- strates [16] or inhibitors [17, 18]. Proceeding from these stud- ies, several attempts have been undertaken to correlate the substrate speciﬁcity and the three-dimensional structure of the enzyme [16, 17, 19].

At present, computer modelling studies are extensively used to investigate the three-dimensional interactions of en- zymes with substrates or inhibitors. Until now it has been very difﬁcult to obtain reliable free energies of binding and activation for enzyme-catalyzed reactions using energy- minimization studies. The kinetic approach is still the most appropriate method for obtaining these data.
