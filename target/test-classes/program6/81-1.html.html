The preparation of transition-metal- substituted horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase is com- plicated by the fact that each subunit of the dimeric enzyme contains two zinc ions in different environ- ments: one zinc is bound to the active site and plays an essential role in catalysis, the other one is bound 2.06 nm away from the catalytic site [18] and is thought to add to the structural stability of the polypeptide chain folding [19]. These two metal ions exhibit differ- ent exchange rates towards “Zn” or Co“ (with respect to other bivalent cations) which has led to con- ﬂicting interpretations summarized as follows.

a) The catalytic zinc ions participate most readily in ligand exchange reactions but they exchange slowly with other bivalent metal ions [1 —— 3].

b) The catalytic zinc ions are the fast-exchanging ones, the noncatalytic ions being more inert with respect to ligand and metal-metal exchange [12].

c) The properties of the metal ions are different in the subunits, i.e. one of each pair of differently coordinated metal ions exchanges rapidly, the other one slowly, according to a half-of-the-sites-reactivity model [7,8].

A general and deﬁnitive assignment with respect to localization and properties of differently bound metal ions in horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase would therefore be of great value for studies of the catalytic and structural role played by the metal ions in this enzyme. Hitherto, the preparation of cobalt horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase has been done exclusively by stepwise replacement via exhaustive dialysis against excess of C02 T at low pH under anaerobic conditions [5].

In this paper, novel techniques for metal exchange in soluble, immobilised and crystalline horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase are reported. Working in the crystalline state [20] permits metal extraction and insertion speciﬁcally at the active site and opens new aspects concerning the role of metal coordination in structure-function relationship in this class of enzymes.

Metal Analyses

The metal analyses were run in triplicate with two independent methods. Aliquots of enzyme were di- gested with 6 M HCl for zinc or a mixture of 6 M H2SO4/HClO4/HNO3 (1/1/1, v/v/v) for cobalt analyses at 110 “C for 24 h. A Perkin-Elmer 400 atomic absorp- tion spectrophotometer with graphite furnace HGA-74 and automatic sampling device AS-1 was employed for atomic absorption measurements. Photometric zinc and cobalt analyses were done as described [28,29].
