However, in the analysis of the translated 5’- UTR sequence of the isolated gene, we could not clearly identify a conventional signal peptide for import into mitochondria. Unfor- tunately, nothing is known about the mechanisms of protein im- port into mitochondria in this microorganism. Alternatively, E. gracilis might have other genes encoding cytosolic and mi- tochondrial isoforms of NAD+-ADH as suggested by the variety of EST sequences for ADHs found in the database.

The fact that high NAD+-ADH activity was also detected in cells grown on glutamate+malate (Rodriguez-Zavala et al. 2006) or glucose (Munir et al. 2002) as carbon sources suggest that ADH activities might be involved in the utilization of intermediaries from the catabolism of other substrates different from ethanol. The NADP+-ADH speciﬁc for long-chain alcohols (Munir et al. 2002) could play an important role when cells are grown under strictly anaerobic conditions. For anaerobic wax ester fermenta- tion in E. gracilis, mitochondrial fatty acid synthesis takes place; a fraction of the synthesized fatty acids are reduced to odd-num- bered long-chain alcohols (Schneider and Betz 1985), which are esteriﬁed with another fatty acid to synthesize waxes (Buetow 1989; Inui et al. 1984) or are re-oxidized by one of several ADHs present in Euglena. Hence, the E gADH reported here might play a role in the utilization of ethanol. Thus, the metabolic versatility of E. gracilis allows this microorganism to easily adapt to artiﬁcial (laboratory) or natural environmental conditions.
