Other analytical procedures. The purity and molecular mass of the puriﬁed enzymes were analyzed by sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) as described by Laemmli.” Before application, samples were mixed with 0.33 volume of a sample buffer and heated at 60°C for 30 min. Then, the treated samples were put on a 10% SDS-PAGE slab gel. Standard pre-stained proteins (BioRad) were used as markers. For N- terminal sequencing, the separated proteins in SDS- PAGE were transferred electrophoretically onto a polyvinylidene diﬂuoride microporous membrane. Then, the membrane was stained with 0.1% Coomasie brilliant blue R250 for 1 min, and destained with 50% methanol. The visible band was excised and put into a peptide sequence analyzer PSQ-2 (Shimadzu). The protein content was meas- ured by the modiﬁed Lowry method.” The relative molecular mass of the native enzyme were measured by gel ﬁltration by using a Superdex S-200 column equilibrated with 50 mM KPB (pH 7.5) containing 1 mM DTT. The standard marker proteins used were obtained from Oriental Yeast, Co., Ltd. The metal content of the puriﬁed enzymes were measured by induced-coupled plasma atomic emission spectromet- ry (ICP-AES) using the internal standard method with Zn”, Mg“, and Fe“.

Results

Purification of two NAD-dependent ADHs All of the NAD-dependent ADH activity was

found in the supernatant after ultracentrifugation of the crude extract from A. pasteurianus CN6-2 mutant strain. During the puriﬁcation, enzyme activ- ity against acetaldehyde was measured. NAD-depen- dent ADH activity was separated into two fractions on a DEAE Toyopearl column (Fig. 1(A)). One, with an activity preference for primary alcohols (ADH I), was eluted at 35 mM KPB (pH 7.5), while another enzyme activity (ADH II) was passed through the column. ADH II activity was easily lost during the column chromatographies, which could be stabilized by the addition of DTT and MgClz in the column buffer. The puriﬁcation of two NAD-dependent ADHs are summarized in Table 1. ADH I was enriched around 35-fold with a 15% yield, while ADH II was enriched around 21-fold with a 7% yield.

Purity and molecular mass determination

The puriﬁed enzyme fractions of ADH I and ADH II obtained from the ﬁnal step, had a single protein band in SDS-PAGE (Fig. 1(B) and (C)). The relative molecular masses of ADH I and ADH II were esti- mated by SDS-PAGE to be 42 kDa and 31 kDa, respectively. The native molecular sizes measured by a Superdex S-200 gel ﬁltration were 130 kDa and 70 kDa for ADH I and ADH II, respectively (data not shown), suggesting that the native ADH I is probably a trimer composed of identical subunits, while ADH II consists of two identical subunits.

N-terminal amino acid sequence analysis

The N-terminal amino acid sequence of ADH I (20 residues) and ADH II (30 residues) were SGKMKAAVVHEFGKPLTIEE and ARVAG- KVAIVSGAANGIGKATAQLLAKEGA, respec- tively. In a database search using the BLAST algorithm, the ADH I sequence showed the best matching scores at the amino terminus of prokaryot- ic long-chain ADHs, especially from Sinorhizobium meliloti (NC003037), Ralstonia solanacearum (AL646073), and Pseudomonas aeruginosa (AE004955) (Fig. 2(A)). By using the same proce- dures, ADH II sequence was shown to have identity with short-chain ADHs, from Agrobacterium tumefaciens (AE008318), Mycobacterium tuberculo- sis (AE006970), and Caenorhabitis elegans (AC006677) (Fig. 2(B)). The data indicated that ADH I and ADH II belong to a long-chain and a short-chain ADH family, respectively.
