Carboxylate ester-forming activity

The reaction mixture for carboxylate ester biosynthesis was composed of 5O mM Tris buffer (pH 8.3), 3 mM NAD+, 1 M alcohol, and 0.1 M aldehyde. Alcohols included ethanol and 1-butanol, and aldehydes included formalde- hyde, acetaldehyde, butyraldehyde, and valeraldehyde. After the addition of a crude enzyme solution, a 3-ml amber bottle was ﬁlled with the reaction mixture and capped with a silicon stopper and an aluminum seal. The vial was incubated at 25°C for 18 h. Results

Cloning and sequence alignment of N. crassa ADH1 and ADH3 genes

Genome sequence analysis of N crassa FGSC2489 showed that 67 and 74 bp introns were present in the ADHI and ADH3 genes, respectively (Galagan et al. 2003). cDNA synthesis from N. crassa mRNA allowed the correct amplification of the ADHI and ADH3 gene-coding regions. DNA sequencing showed that the ADHl and ADH3 genes in each plasmid exhibited 100% identity to the coding regions published in the public N crassa database. Both coding regions have the same size of 1,062 nucleotides. DNA homology analysis between the N crassa ADHI and ADH3 genes using the Biology Workbench showed 65.4% identity. The deduced amino acid sequences of N crassa ADHl and ADH3 contained a zinc-binding consensus motif of GHEGAGVVVA(/Q)KGELVT and a NAD(P)+-binding motif of GAGGGLG (Fig. 2). Glutamine (Q) in parenthesis refers to the ADH3 sequence. Theoretical isoelectric points and molecular weights of N crassa ADHl and ADH3 were calculated to be pH 6.32 and 37,443 and pH 8.26 and 37,983, respectively. The peptide sequence analysis of the two N. crassa ADHs using the BLASTp program showed 54-75% identity and 69-87% similarity to fungi ADHs. A comparison between ADHl and ADH3 from N crassa showed 65% identity and 79% similarity. Filamentous Aspergillus oryzae and A. fumigatus presented higher scores of identity (61—75%) than budding yeasts such as P. stipitis and S. cerevisiae (54—66%).
