AVILA ET AL.—SUBCELLULAR LOCALIZATION OF ADH IN AIVIEBA 221

The amount of E. histolytica ADH2 detected by the L. lactis ADH antibody in soluble fractions obtained by Aley’s method (1980) and by differential centrifugation does not agree with the activity detected in similar fractions. The soluble NADf- dependent activity was very low, suggesting that aggregation could be important for the activity, as has been suggested for this enzyme and for ADHE from E. coli (Goodlove et al., 1989; Kessler et al., 1991; Flores et al., 1996).

Binding of antibody against ADHE from L. lactis to ameba ADH2 (96 kDa) was expected because both enzymes share about 48% identity with E. c0li ADHE and complement an E. c0li adhE mutant (Bruchhaus and Tannich, 1994; Yang et al., 1994; Yong et al., 1996; Arnau et al., 1998). Moreover, lower molecular mass bands faintly recognized by the antibody were detected in some amebic fractions. These bands could be other amebic ADHs, such as the ADH3 and a putative amebic ADH, whose homology with ADH2 has been previously reported (Ki- mura et al., 1996; Rodriguez et al., 1996). Another possible explanation for the lower molecular mass bands observed is that some proteolytic degradation of the 96-kDa protein could have occurred during the handling of samples, in spite of a speciﬁc protease inhibitor that was present.

After this work was submitted, evidence was published about the importance of NADf-dependent ADH activity for E. his- tolytica growth; in addition, rodlike structures of EhADH2 ex- pressed in an E. coli strain were observed (Espinosa et al., 2001).

The main NADf-dependent ADH2 activity from E. histoly- tica has a rodlike structure that sedimented in a similar fashion to the multimeric ADHE from E. coli. Therefore, this amebic enzyme does not seem to be a genuine membrane protein.
