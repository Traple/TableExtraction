Alcohol and its metabolites (acetaldehyde and FAEEs) can alter metabolic pathways involved in the inﬂammatory response and carcinogenesis? In our study, we found that the activity of human ADH and ALDH did not signiﬁcantly differ between cancer and healthy tissues. It is important to note that the activity of ADH is much higher than that of ALDH. The activity of ALDH in pancreas cancer cells and healthy acinar cells was barely detectable. This would suggest that the pancreas have a very small capability to remove acetalde- hyde. Estival et al16 reported that the pancreas have a limited capacity for oxidation of ethanol, whereas Guynn et a1” concluded that ethanol is not oxidized by the pancreas. Haber et alls showed that the class III isoenzymes of ADH is responsible for the oxidative ethanol metabolism in the pancreas. Our study demonstrates that class III exhibits the highest activity among all tested ADH isoenzymes. More- over, we found also that the activity of class III ADH in the pancreas cancer is about 30% higher [l.23 nmol/(min mg“ of protein)] than that in normal pancreatic tissues [087 nmol/ (min mg“ of protein)]. However, the kinetic properties of human class III ADH isoenzyme indicate that this isoenzyme cannot be saturated by ethanol even at high concentrations. Therefore, in our opinion, the human class III ADH isoen- zyme cannot oxidize ethanol in pancreas cancer cells or pan- creatic acinar cells. This isoenzyme participates mainly in the metabolism of endogenous long-chain alcohols and alde- hydes and in the oxidation of S-hydroxymethylglutathione, which is responsible for maintenance of redox state in the cells.“ Probably, it leads to depletion of glutathione, which has been reported to increase the leakage of electrons due to an incomplete oxidation of substrates in the mitochondria, thus increasing the production of superoxide radicals. An enhanced generation of superoxide radical may lead to oxidative stress. Oxidative stress has been implicated in the development of cancer. But oxidative stress may be both a cause and an effect of tissue damage. The statement that class III ADH effects oxidative stress needs conﬁrmation in further researches.

The other tested classes of ADH isoenzymes have a very low activity in pancreatic cancer cells and are slightly higher than in the healthy pancreas tissue. But it is not a generalizable ﬁnding. In other cancers, we have found a different activity of ADH isoenzymes. For example, in colorectal cancer, the activity of class I ADH is signiﬁcantly higher in cancer cells than in healthy tissuess; in gastric cancer, the activity of class IV ADH is signiﬁcantly higher in cancer tissues”; in breast cancer, the activity of class I ADH is signiﬁcantly lower in cancer cells than in healthy tissues.”

We claimed that the role of class I, II, and IV ADH in the ethanol metabolism in the pancreas seems to be negligible. In this situation, a very small capability to remove acetaldehyde by the reason of vestigial activities of ALDH in pancreas probably does not have great importance in pancreatic carcinogenesis because acetaldehyde is generated in small amounts. acetaldehyde may be also metabolized through 2 other metabolic pathways (aldehyde oxidase and microsomal aldehyde oxidizing system).21 Thus, it is not possible to say that metabolism of ethanol by ADH might be responsible for carcinogenesis in the pancreas. It is known that alcohol is also metabolized by the cytochrome P450 system through cytochrome P450 2E1. As a result, reactive oxygen species are generated, which initiates tissue injuries through activa- tion of nuclear factor KB and heightened transcription of proinﬂammatory cytokines.”

Our results allow to take nonoxidative pathway of ethanol metabolism under consideration. This occurs pre- dominantly in the pancreas.4 In nonoxidative pathway, the FAEEs are generated in the reaction catalyzed by FAEE synthase. It is evident that accumulation of FAEEs occurred in the pancreas after exposure to ethanol.” Fatty acid ethyl esters were shown to appear in serum after ethanol ingestion, and FAEE serum concentration and tissue levels correlated with pancreatic injury.24 Werner et al25 shown that infusion of FAEE reconstituted in low-density lipoprotein produced morphological and biochemical changes in the pancreas. The mechanism by which FAEEs or their metabolites, such as free fatty acids, produce their toxic effects is not known. However, there is now evidence that FAEEs are toxic mediators in isolated organelles and intact cells. Lange and Sobel26 speculated that FAEEs induce mitochondrial dysfunction. A direct toxic and carcinogenic effect of FAEEs may be caused by the ability of FAEEs to alter the ﬂuidity of membranes, as described by Hungund et al.” In addition, nonoxidative ethanol metabolites, FAEEs, inhibit proteases that degrade extracellular matrix proteins, thus increasing ﬁbrosis.”

The mechanism by which alcohol accelerates the pancreatic carcinogenesis processes still remains unknown. Heavy alcohol consumption has been known to be a major cause of chronic pancreatitis and risk factor for type 2 diabetes mellitus, both of which are linked to pancreatic cancer inducers? Our present results suggest that neither ADH nor ALDH activity plays a key role in pancreatic carcinogenesis. Moreover, in our study, we showed that the differences of total ADH and all tested ADH isoenzyme activities between drinker and nondrinker samples from pancreatic cancer tissues are not statistically signiﬁcant. Also, ALDH activity is not signiﬁcantly higher in the cancer tissues from drinkers in comparison to nondrinkers.

In conclusion, we can state that pancreatic cancer exhibited ADH and ALDH activities. Class III ADH activity was signiﬁcantly higher in cancerous tissue than in normal tissue. But in view of insigniﬁcant participation of this class ADH in metabolism of ethanol, we considered that the oxidative pathway of ethanol metabolism via ADH and ALDH does not play role in pancreatic carcinogenesis.
