From the data given above, we may already derive the following con- clusions concerning the relationship between metal binding sites and protein structure.

First, the high selectivity of the metal exchange at both sites permits their deﬁnitive assignment. The column chromatography method allows for the ex- change of zinc for cobalt and the simultaneous separa- tion of the released zinc from the cobalt enzyme which conﬁrms that the exchange of half of the zinc ions is fast and proceeds without change in turnover in the product. These results parallel those obtained by the dialysis procedure of Sytkowski and Vallee [1--3]. The advantage of the column technique lies in its speed and the possibility of adjusting various parameters in an optimal manner, e. g. the exchange was performed not only at low pH, but also at high ionic strength, since it has been shown that high ionic strength seems to labilize the tertiary structure [42]. After passage through the exchange column, the enzyme is trans- ferred to a medium of low ionic strength and higher pH in order to stabilize the protein during the separa- tion from excess of cobaltous ions. If approximately the same exchange kinetics were valid in the column system as in the dialysis system described by Sytkowski and Vallee [1], then the exchange of the noncatalytic zinc ions could occur much faster than the exchange of the catalytic ones. Taking their data into account we estimate that less than 5 ‘X, of the catalytic metal ions would have been substituted by cobalt in a typical column experiment lasting less than 4 h. This amount is hardly detectable by enzymatic or spectroscopic methods. Only at higher temperature is the velocity of exchange of the second half of the zinc ions for cobalt increased so much that a substitution during the time of passage through the column is made possible. The concomitant decrease in speciﬁc activity clearly demonstrates that the catalytic zinc ions are substituted fairly rapidly under these more drastic conditions. For the preparation of fully substituted enzyme, this procedure is outdated already by the development of the speciﬁc substitution at the active site, since this can be done under milder conditions with higher yields. The active-site-substituted enzyme can subsequently be transformed into the fully sub- stitued species by taking advantage of the rapid exchange at the noncatalytic sites by either the column method or by dialysis. The surprising selectivity of the exchange of the catalytic zinc ions, as documented in Results, seems to depend on different exchange mechanisms, which may explain the diﬁierent kinetic behaviour of both metal binding sites.

At least at the noncatalytic binding sites such a mechanism seems reasonable, since these sites are easily accessible at the surface of the enzyme molecule [18] and would enable the formation of the postulated binuclear complex.

In contrast, the catalytic metal ions are buried fairly deeply at the bottom of the hydrophobic cata- lytic cleft [18], and it is doubtful whether the formation and breakdown of the binuclear intermediate is pos- sible at all in this different environment. On the other hand, there is space for the attack of a new ligand from the face of the liganded water molecule, which can be exchanged. In case the ligand is bidentate, such as 1,10-phenanthroline, an extension of the coor- dination sphere is achieved [45].
