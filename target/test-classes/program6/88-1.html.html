MATERIALS AND METHODS

Bacteria were grown on brucella agar plates (BBL, Cockeysville, MD), supplemented with 5% defibrinated sheep blood in air at 35°C for 24 hr (Table 1). The bacterial growth was harvested and first washed three times with 100 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4). An aliquot of the bacterial suspension was sonicated for 5 X 30 sec in an ice bath and then centrifuged at 100,000 >< g for 65 min to obtain the cytosol.

Cytosolic ADH activities were determined spectrophotometrically by measuring, after addition of ethanol, the reduction of NAD (ﬁnal con- centration in the reaction mixture of 2.5 mM) or NADP (final concentra- tion in the reaction mixture of 2.5 and 5 mM) at 340 nm at 25°C in 0.1 M glycine buffer (pH 9.6), with ethanol concentrations ranging from 1.5 mM to 2.5 M. NAD-linked ADH activities at neutral pH were determined in 0.1 M potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) with an ethanol concentration of 25 mM.

The inhibitory effect of 4-methylpyrazole (Aldrich-Chemie GmbH, Steinheim, Germany) on NAD-linked ADH activity was tested using drug concentrations ranging from 0 to 100 mM at ethanol concentrations of 0.5, 1.5, and 2.5 M. Proteins were assayed according to the method of Lowry et a1.” ADH activity was then calculated as nmoles of reduced NAD(P)H produced per milligram of protein per minute.

Results are expressed as means t SEM of at least three determina- tions. Paired Student’s t test was used for statistical analysis, and differ- ence was considered to be significant with p < 0.05. Values for Km (Michaelis constant) were determined by Lineweaver-Burk plots, with a computerized data-analysis program. Values for K,- (inhibition constant) of 4-methylpyrazole were determined with a computerized data-analysis pro- gram by using Dixon plots.

RESULTS

Marked cytosolic NAD-linked ADH activities were de- tected in all bacteria, even at 1.5 mM ethanol concentration and the activities increased with rising ethanol concentra- tions (Fig. 1). ADH activity of Iﬂebsiella oxytoca and Esch- erichia coli at 2.5 M ethanol increased further, suggesting the presence of the second Km for ethanol (Fig. 2). In contrast, NAD-linked ADH activity of Iﬂebsiella pneu- moniae, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Haﬁzai alvei at this ethanol concentration had a tendency to decrease as a result of the substrate inhibition (Fig. 2).

NADP-linked ADH activity was found in the cytosol of E. coli. However, it was nearly 20 times lower than the corresponding NAD-linked activity [6.7 i 3.6 vs. 159.7 i- 11 nmol NAD(P)H/min/mg protein]. Other bacteria did not possess any NADP-linked ADH activity, and no inﬂu- ence of rising NADP concentration up to 5 mM on the ADH activity was observed.

Marked ADH activities were demonstrated also at neu- tral pH that is close to that in the cell. However, in com- parison with ADH activities determined at pH 9.6, ADH activities at pH 7.4 were significantly lower (p < 0.002) in 4 of 5 bacteria. Only ADH of K oxytoca showed almost equal activities at both pHs (Fig. 3).

The inhibition of ADH activity by 4-methylpyrazole was found to be of the competitive type in 4 of 5 tested bacteria (Figs. 4A to 4D).
