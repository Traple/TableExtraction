Leu116 belongs to the substrate binding pocket, according to a detailed alignment of yeast Adhl with structurally solved horse liver alcohol dehy- drogenase 1 (Jornvall er al., 1978). Furthermore, Serl09 and Tyr294 are adjacent to and in the vicinity of Phe1l0 and Ile290, respectively, which have also been shown to be part of the substrate binding pocket (Jomvall er al., 1978). Finally, using the mutation function of Swiss-PdbViewer (http://WWw.expasy.org/spdbv/) to replace tyro- sine with cysteine (Y294C) in the recently elu- cidated macromolecular structure of yeast Adhl (Plapp er al., 2007), we observed a clear widen- ing of the substrate binding pocket, most probably as a result of the absence of the tyrosine phe- nol side chain. Since acetaldehyde is about three times smaller in mass compared with HMF (44 and 126 g/mol, respectively), even when neglecting the three-dimensional structure of the HMF furan ring, it is tempting to speculate that one or more of the amino acid changes have affected the substrate speciﬁcity of the enzyme by enlarging the binding pocket.

Results from in vitro assays conﬁrmed that no detectable NADH-dependent HMF reductase activ- ity was observed in the native Adhl enzyme. Also, higher in vitro furfural speciﬁc activity for mut- Adhl indicated that the mutated enzyme was active towards furfural. However, lower in vitro speciﬁc activity towards acetaldehyde was observed in mut- Adhl strain as compared to the control strain. Equal or higher speciﬁc activity was expected, since the assays were performed with strains that both carried a genomic copy of native ADH] gene and that only differed by the additional copies of the mutated ADH] gene in mut-Adhl strain. One hypothesis might be that genomic ADH] expres- sion is downregulated in the mut-Adhl strain as a result of the overexpression of the mutated enzyme. Alternatively, changes in kinetic constants for acetaldehyde that would favour the utilization of acetaldehyde by mut-Adhl, but with less efﬁ- ciency, cannot be excluded.

The present study indeed demonstrated that the overexpression of the mut-Adh] gene was cor- related with an increased in vivo HMF reduc- tion rate and with higher glucose consumption and ethanol production rates, in a deﬁned cultiva- tion medium. In the mut-Adhl strain, the NADH- dependent reduction of HMF was also accompa- nied by a signiﬁcant decrease in glycerol produc- tion, which is known to be correlated with the need for NAD+ recycling under anaerobic con- ditions (Nordstrom, 1966). As ethanol was not re-assimilated with the cultivation set-up, the cul- ture may have been oxygen-limited and reduced glycerol production may have occurred as a result of competition for NADH between glycerol 3- phosphate dehydrogenase and HMF reductase. The NADH-dependent reduction of HMF in the mut- Adhl strain also resulted in slightly higher acetate production. The NADPT-dependent oxidation of acetaldehyde to acetate (Wang er al., 1998) may be a secondary effect resulting from a slightly higher intracellular acetaldehyde level, which would arise from the competition between acetaldehyde and HMF for alcohol dehydrogenase. However, further characterization of enzyme kinetics will be needed to validate this hypothesis.

Our in vivo results were in agreement with those obtained when overexpressing the NADPH- dependent HMF reductase Adh6 in S. cerevisiae (Petersson er al., 2006), for which increased Adh6 level led to a three- to four-fold increase in spe- ciﬁc HMF uptake rate under both anaerobic and aerobic conditions. However, one main difference between Adhl and Adh6 relies in the co-factor usage of the enzyme for HMF reduction, NADH vs. NADPH, respectively, which may inﬂuence prod- uct distribution. NADH-dependent HMF conver- sion may affect NAD(H)-dependent reactions, such the NADH-dependent reduction of dihydroxyace- tone phosphate to glycerol phosphate by glycerol 3- phosphate dehydrogenase, or the NADH-dependent reduction of acetaldehyde to ethanol by alcohol dehydrogenase, whereas NADPH-dependent HMF reduction may rather compete for NADPH with, among others, biomass synthesis. Future work will aim at a thorough comparison of the effect of both enzymes on product distribution under strict anaer- obic conditions.
