Effects attributable to ionization of zinc-bound water

NADH and NAD+ are bound in extended conformation to liver alcohol dehydrogenase with the AMP portion positioned at the protein surface and the NMN portion accomodated in the deep hydrophobic pocket which constitutes the active site of the enzyme subunit [15]. The catalytic zinc ion is bound by three protein ligands at the bottom of this pocket and coordinates a water molecule as a fourth ligand. Ionization of the latter group results in de- stabilizing charge interactions with the coenzyme pyrophosphate group and decreases the affinity of the enzyme for NADH by a factor of about 100 [3, 4]. The affinity for NADi is increased by a factor of 40, however, due to the stronger stabilizing interaction of the zinc-bound hydroxide ion with the coenzyme nicotinamide ring charge [3, 4]. Results in Fig. 6 indicate that the latter stabilization derives entirely from effects on the coenzyme off-velocity constant, which decreases by a factor of 200 following deprotonation of zinc- bound water (Table 2). It seems obvious that the charge sepa- ration associated with desorption of the nicotinamide ring from the active-site pocket limits the rate of NADl dissocia- tion at high pH.

Results in Fig. 3 confirm the expectation that effects of zinc-bound water on the NADH dissociation rate should be controlled by a true pK, of 11.2 [11] and indicate that NADH dissociates only three times more rapidly when zinc-bound water is deprotonated (Table 2). This means that the NADH off-velocity constant remains essentially unaffected by the strong destabilizing interaction of the zinc-bound hydroxide ion with the coenzyme pyrophosphate group [4], which pre- cludes that desorption of the latter group may represent a rate-limiting event in the NADH dissociation process. The observation that ADP-ribose dissociates about 50 times more rapidly than NADH leads to the same conclusion and pro- vides clear evidence that the dissociation of NADH is rate- limited mainly by desorption of the nicotinamide ring from the active-site pocket. This is consistent with early kinetic results indicating that the NADH dissociation rate is strongly affected by substitutions in the nicotinamide ring but essentially unaffected by substitutions in the adenine ring [17].

Shore proposed from the latter results that coenzyme bind- ing takes place in two rapid steps, the nicotinamide ring being bound subsequently to the adenine ring [17]. That idea has received some support by crystallographic results which show that coenzyme analogues may be bound in a non-productive mode with the nicotinamide ring positioned at the surface of the protein [20, 21]. Kinetic data obtained by pressure-jump relaxation techniques provide direct evidence for a two-step binding mechanism [18] _and can be rationalized in terms of a rapid pre-equilibration between enzyme and the ADP portion of the coenzyme, followed by a slower accomodation of the nicotinamide ring in the active-site pocket [3, 19]; this would

render the on-velocity constant for coenzyme binding directly dependent on the affinity of the enzyme for the ADP portion of the coenzyme. The present results lend support to such a pre-equilibration mechanism by showing that the destabiliza- tion of NADH binding induced by ionization of zinc-bound water represents mainly an on-velocity effect. The 30-fold lower rate of NADH association to enzyme containing deprotonated bound zinc (Table 2) corresponds well to the affinity decrease calculated for the destabilizing electrostatic interaction of a zinc-bound hydroxide ion with the pyrophos- phate group of bound coenzyme [4].

NADH binding, but not the binding of ADP-ribose, in- duces a change in enzyme conformation from an open to a more closed state [1, 15]. The present observation that on- velocity constants for NADH and ADP-ribose binding at pH 7 agree closely provides the inference that neither the coenzyme-induced conformational change, nor accomoda- tion of the coenzyme nicotinamide ring, limits the rate of NADH association. Conversely, it may by concluded from the about tenfold-lower magnitude of the NAD” on-velocity constant that binding of the nicotinamide ring provides a main rate-limiting contribution to the association of oxidized coenzyme at pH 7. The latter rate limitation is likely to reflect the energetic disadvantage of bringing the positively charged nicotinamide ring of NAD J’ from solution to the hydrophobic active-site pocket, where no compensating negative charge is provided by the enzyme at low pH. Ionization of zinc-bound water creates such a charge and largely eliminates the difference between the NADH and NAD+ association rates (Table 2). The difference in rate-limiting events for the associ- ation of reduced and oxidized coenzyme explains why the NADH and NAD+ association rate constants are affected to different extents following ionization of zinc-bound water (Fig. 7) or the ligation of anions such as cyanide to catalytic zinc [22].

Previously reported kinetic data for NADH and NADl binding to liver alcohol dehydrogenase have been taken to indicate that coenzyme association to or dissociation from the enzyme becomes insigniﬁcantly slow [12, 23, 24] or forbidden [25] after ionization of zinc-bound water. The present results indicate that the kinetic effects of the ionizing group are actually ﬁnite and of a magnitude which provides some additional insight into the events and energetic factors which control the coenzyme binding process.
