From the work of Dalziel and Dickinson [4, 5] until the present day [7], it has been hard to understand why the R- enantiomers of secondary alcohols [(R)-2-butanol and (R)-2- pentanol] are oxidized at higher rates in comparison to the S- enantiomers, although their kcm/Km values are much smaller. With the data shown here, it is now possible to explain this observation in terms of systematic variations in the kinetic behaviour of LADH, caused by systematic substrate-structure variations.

The substitution of an rat-hydrogen of a primary alcohol by a methyl group (e.g. ethanol to isopropanol) results in a drastic increase in Km. Simultaneously, the free energy of activation for the hydride-transfer step increases considerably. For acetone in comparison to an aldehyde, this effect is even larger. For secondary alcohols, enlargement of the alkyl side chain in the hydrophobic substrate channel of LADH (R1 in Fig. 2) results in reduced dissociation constants and an acceleration in hydride transfer. This agrees with the results obtained for primary alcohols [41, 43, 44]. The binding of 2- butanone in the pro-S conﬁguration in comparison to acetone is stabilized even better than found for the corresponding alcohols. At the same time, the free energy of activation is 2.5 kJ ' mol _ 1 higher for (pro-S) 2-butanone compared to acetone, while it is 3.3 kJ - mol” 1 lower for (S)-2-butanol compared to isopropanol. The most probable reason for this is the better ﬁxing of residue R1 in the hydrophobic channel with increas- ing chain length (Fig. 2), at the expence of optimal positioning of the carbonyl group with respect to the metal ion. The effect on the free energy of activation for alcohols and ketones is opposite, possibly due to the weaker binding of the carbonyl oxygen to the catalytic Zn“ in comparison to the binding of the alkoxide ion, thus withdrawing the ketones from a more favourable position for hydride transfer.

Does the intrinsic reactivity of ketones explain our data? Lemiere et al. [39] have claimed that the intrinsic reactivities of ketones, obtained from NaBH4 reductions, have to be taken into account when comparing the reactivities of differ- ent ketones in LADH-catalyzed reactions. This seems to hold for acetone, which has a free energy of activation for NaBH4 reduction which is 2 kJ - mol‘ 1 lower than that of 2-butanone. For 3-pentanone this effect should be even larger (6 k] - mol ‘ l in comparison to acetone) [45]. Contrary to this expectation, the data for Zn(II)LADH show a slightly decreased free en- ergy of activation for 3-pentanone in comparison to acetone. Thus, these effects have to be correlated with a change of the enzyme-substrate interaction, depending on the substrate structure. The concept of intrinsic reactivities does not explain the observed energetic changes which depend on substrate structure.

The enlargement of R2 in Fig. 2 results in a decrease in the free energy of activation for alcohols and ketones [4.2 kJ - mol“ for 3-pentanol compared to (S)-2-butanol and 3 kJ - mol‘ 1 for 3-pentanone compared to (pro-S)-2-butanone]. At the same time, the Kd values increase.

For (R)-2- octanol, we obtained similar ternary-complex configurations in modelling experiments based on the diamond-lattice ap- proach (unpublished results). Thus, the very low oxidation rate could be limited by isomerization of a nonproductive to a productive ternary complex. This would imply that the observed rates of oxidation are not related to the hydride- transfer step.

A further increase of the chain length of R, (isopropanol < (S)-2-butanol < (S)-2-pentanol < (S)-2-octanol) results in decreased K2 and Km values for alcohols and ketones with hydride-transfer rate constants increasing for alcohols and decreasing for ketones. The enlargement of R2 results in more complicated effects. Km for (R)-2-pentanol is higher compared to (R)-2-butanol and the hydride-transfer rate constant is decreased but still higher than that found for (S)-2-pentanol. The best explanation for the effects on the hydride-transfer step seems to be that the distance and/or orientation of the reactive centers of coenzyme and substrate are strongly depen- dent on the substrate structure. A change in the distance of substrate and coenzyme may explain the unidirectional changes in free energy of activation for both directions of the reactions.

Out data show that binding and activation can be esti- mated separately. Depending on the substrate structure, decreasing binding energies can be accompanied by decreasing free energies of activation and vice versa. Since binding domi- nates the catalytic efficiency of LADH for the substrates inves- tigated, the poorer substrates can show higher k0,, values. Our results show to what extent binding and activation con- tribute to the substrate specificity of LADH.

S tereoselecri vity of Zn (H) LADH-catalyzed ketone reductions

The stereospeciﬁcity of LADH for primary alcohols and aldehydes was found to be (pro-R) for all substrates investi- gated [3] excepting 1-octanol, for which Shapiro et al. [46] showed that the (pro-S) hydrogen can be exchanged in a LADH-NAD/NADH-diaphorase recycling system in D20 at a rate of 2 ~ 4% that found for (pro-R) exchange. With YADH they observed pure (pro-R) specificity. They presented three possible explanations for these results. Our data for the long- chain secondary alcohols seem to support their first expla- nation which postulates binding of 1-octanol to LADH in a configuration which is impossible with YADH and enables the transfer of the (pro-S) hydrogen.

In some cases, both enantiomers of chiral reaction prod- ucts could be detected using prochiral cyclic ketones as sub- strates [3, 8, 47]. The stereoselectivity for the reduction of acyclic ketones has been investigated using alcohol dehydro- genase from Thermoanaerobium brockii [37] and alcohol de- hydrogenase from T. ethanomus [6]. In both cases, the stereoselectivity was found to depend on the ketone structure.
