The Activity 0f Class I, II, III, and IV Alcohol Dehydrogenase (ADH) Isoenzymes and Aldehyde Dehydrogenase (ALDH)

in Liver Cancer

Wojciech Jelski - Bogdan Zalewski - Maciej Szmitkowski

Received: 28 August 2007/ Accepted: 26 November 2007/ Published online: 26 January 2008

© Springer Science+Business Media, LLC 2007

Abstract Background Alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH), which are most abundant in the liver, are the main enzymes involved in ethanol and acetaldehyde metabolism. Aims The purpose of this study was to compare the activity of ADH isoenzymes and ALDH between liver carcinoma cells and healthy hepatocytes. Patients Samples were taken from 44 liver cancer patients (19 drinkers, 25 nondrinkers). Seventeen patients had primary liver tumors and 27 had metastatic liver tumors. Methods Fluorimetric methods were used for measurement of the activity of class I and II ADH isoen- zymes and ALDH activity. Total ADH activity and activity of class III and IV isoenzymes were measured by a pho- tometric method. Results The activities of total ADH, ALDH, and class I ADH were signiﬁcantly higher in cancer cells than in healthy tissues. The other tested classes of ADH showed a tendency toward higher activity in cancer than in normal cells. The differences between enzymes of drinkers and nondrinkers in both cancer and healthy tissue, were not signiﬁcant. Conclusion Differences in the activities of total ADH, ALDH and class I ADH isoenzyme between cancer liver tissues and healthy hepa- tocytes might be a factor in ethanol metabolism disorders, which can intensify carcinogenesis.

Introduction

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is one of the most com- mon malignant tumors worldwide. Tumors of the liver are either primary (arising from native hepatic cells) or sec- ondary (owing to metastasis or the direct spread of neoplasia from adjacent organs) in nature [l]. In developed countries, alcohol drinking has been recognized as one of the major risk factors for hepatocellular carcinoma, toge- ther with hepatitis B virus and hepatitis C virus infection. The association between long-term alcohol intake and the development of cirrhosis, and between cirrhosis and the development of HCC is well documented [2]. However, direct correlations between ethanol consumption and risk of liver cancer remain unknown. It is accepted that the metabolism of ethanol in liver is catalyzed by alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH). Human ADH exists in multiple molecular forms that have been grouped into several classes [3]. Class I is the classical liver ADH but also detected in the gastroin- testinal tract. Class II in humans is found only in the liver whereas class III is present in all examined tissues. Class IV of ADH exists in the digestive tract organs [4]. The metabolism of ethanol by ADH leads to generation of acetaldehyde, a toxic and carcinogenic compound, which is removed by the action of ALDH. ALDH plays major roles in the oxidation of acetaldehyde to acetate in the liver [5]. The goal of the present study was to compare the capacity for ethanol metabolism measured by ADH iso- enzymes (class I, II, III and IV) and ALDH activity between hepatocellular carcinoma and healthy liver cells.
