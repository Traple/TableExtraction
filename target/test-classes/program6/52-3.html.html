We speculate that the elevation of cyanide-resistance and restoration of ADH II activity could be explained by competition between NADH and cyanide for binding at the active center of ADH II. The fact that NADH more eﬂiciently competed with cyanide than did NADT was in good agreement with the reported Km values for both ADH II cofactors (Kinoshita et al. 1985): l2 uM for NADH and ll0 uM for NADT. In general, this ﬁnding means that elevated intracellular NADH concentrations (increased NADH/NAD+ ratio) could, in principle, in- crease the apparent cyanide-resistance of ADH II.

Cyanide—sensitivity of ADH II depends on the redox state of culture

In order to verify the putative importance of intracel- lular NADH concentration for ADH II cyanide-resis- tance in growing cells, bacteria were cultivated under different conditions of aeration and cyanide, and then: (l) cyanide-sensitivity of both alcohol dehydrogenases was assayed in permeabilized cell suspensions, and (2) intracellular NAD(P)H concentrations were determined. The percentage of enzymatic activity remaining after 45 min of incubation of the permeabilized cells with 200 uM cyanide at 0°C (relative to that of the control sample, incubated for the same period of time without cyanide addition), is presented in Fig. 3a. In agreement with our previous observations (Kalnenieks et al. 2003), ADH I was almost insensitive to cyanide under all experimental conditions. However, culture aeration strongly affected cyanide-sensitivity of ADH II. It was particularly sensitive to cyanide in aerobically cultivated samples. In permeabilized cells, taken from a vigorously aerated chemostat or batch culture, activity of ADH II after 45 min had decreased to below 20% of the corre- sponding control value. At the same time, ADH II in the cells from an anaerobic batch, or from an aerobic cya- nide-fed chemostat, retained 40-45% of its activity. Notably, cyanide-resistance of ADH II did not correlate with its absolute activity (Fig. 3b). Elevation of ADH II cyanide-resistance in cells grown aerobically with cya- nide has been demonstrated in our previous paper (Kalnenieks et al. 2003). The present results show that cyanide per se is not essential for the increase of ADH II cyanide-resistance. Anaerobic growth conditions led to an equally cyanide-resistant ADH II (Fig. 3), for which the presence of cyanide in the culture medium had no extra elTect (not shown).
