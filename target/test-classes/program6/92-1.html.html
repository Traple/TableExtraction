RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Structural properties. DgADH was electrophoretically pure after two chromatographic steps (Table 1; Fig. 1). Some puriﬁcations yielded maximal dehydrogenase activities of more than 100 U mg of protein-l. This suggests that DgADH makes up <1% of the total protein in the cell extract. The enzyme was not very stable. When stored under nitrogen at 4 or —20°C, DgADH (phenyl-Sepharose pool fractions) lost over 90% of its activity within 6 days.

The molecular weight of the DgADH subunit was esti- mated to be 43,000 by sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacryl- amide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) (Fig. 1). A native molecular weight of 320,000 was found with gel ﬁltration on a Superose 6 HR 10/30 column (Pharmacia); a molecular weight marker set (Bio-Rad Laboratories) was used in ac- cordance with the instructions of the manufacturer. This suggests that DgADH is an octameric protein. Electron microscopy, however, showed a different structure. In elec- tron micrographs of DgADH (Fig. 2), most molecules show a ﬁvefold symmetrical view with a diameter of 16 nm. Other views are relatively rare; about 10% of the molecules are in a different orientation. Some typical views are indicated by arrowheads in Fig. 2. The molecules have a striking resem- blance to methanol dehydrogenase from Bacillus methanolicus (1, 27) and methanolzN,N'-dirnethyl-4-nitrosaniline oxidore- ductases from Amycolatopsis methanolica and Illycobac- terium gastri MB19 (4a, 26). Extensive image analysis led to the conclusion that these enzymes are decamers, consisting of two rings of ﬁve subunits stacked face to face.
