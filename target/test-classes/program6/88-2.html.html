DISCUSSION

Most of the ingested ethanol is absorbed during the first hour after its intake in the stomach and small intestine. Alcohol is evenly distributed into the water phase of the human body, so ethanol concentrations in the ileum and colon are comparable with that found in the blood.26'27

Acetaldehyde of the microbial origin has been recently incriminated as a possible factor behind the alcohol-asso- ciated gastrointestinal morbidityfgls Carcinogenity of ac- etaldehyde has been widely demonstrated in experimental animals.” In addition, acetaldehyde has been shown to accelerate the chemically induced carcinogenesis in the rectal mucosa.” A significant positive correlation between crypt cell production rate, hyperregeneration, and mucosal acetaldehyde concentrations has been demonstrated in the colonic mucosa of rats receiving ethanol for 1 month. In that study, acetaldehyde levels in rat colonic mucosa varied from 50 to 350 nmol/g of tissue.” On the other hand, in colonic contents in piglets after diﬁerent ethanol doses, acetaldehyde levels ranged from to 36 i 9 to 271 i 20 uM/liter.“

ADH activity has also been found in the colonic mucosa of humans and rodents.31'33 However, the ADH activity of colonic mucosa has been reported to be considerably low (i.e., about six times lower than in the liver and about half of the gastric activity).33’34

The results of our study show that ADHs of the tested bacteria have rather low Km’s for ethanol (0.06 to 29.9 mM). Accordingly, these values are comparable with the ethanol concentrations that may be observed in the colon after moderate drinking.” Under these circumstances bac- terial ADHs are able to metabolize ethanol to acetaldehyde with a velocity close to the maximal (Fig. 1), and, accord- ingly, to produce marked amounts of acetaldehyde. Although both NAD and NADP are widely distributed in the living material, most ADHs use NAD as a cofactor, combining first with NAD and then with the substrate to form a reactive ternary complex. In contrast to the human ADH, some ADHs of bacteria and yeast can prefer NADP as a cofactong Different isoenzymes of the same microbial ADH may use different cofactors.” Moreover, the prefer- ence of microbial ADH to NAD or NADP depends on the presence of residues—asparagine or glutamine-—in posi- tion 223 of their primary structure.3°'37

In our study, all microbial ADH preferred NAD as a cofactor. Because NADP has a higher molecular weight than NAD, it is plausible that the size of cofactor-binding cleft of the ADHs of these bacteria is large enough to pass only NAD molecule and does not possess additional site for the binding of the phosphate group of NADP. Both NAD- and NADP-linked ADH activities were found in the cytosol of E. coli, although NADP-linked ADH activity was signif- icantly lower than NAD-linked activity. Consequently, it is likely that the ADH of E. coli consists of two isoenzymes that use different cofactors.

A strong epidemiological association between the pres- ence of H. alvei in stool specimens and the development of diarrhea has recently been found. However, the mechanism behind this association has remained unknown.” The very low Km of ADH of H. alvei found in our study may offer a new explanation for the mechanism of this gastrointestinal symptom. The Km for ethanol (0.06 mM) of the cytosolic ADH of H. alvei is ~7 times lower than the endogenous ethanol level (0.4 i 0.2 mM) determined in the large intestine.” Endogenous ethanol produced by normal mi- crobial fermentation may thus represent an excessive level of substrate for the ADH of H. alvei, and this enzyme may produce toxic acetaldehyde in a reverse reaction from en- dogenous ethanol. Accordingly, the increased colonic acet- aldehyde levels might explain, at least in part, the patho- genesis of H. alvei-associated diarrhea. The large variation in the levels of endogenous acetaldehyde found earlier in the colon of piglets“ further supports the hypothesis of this type of microbial acetaldehyde production.
