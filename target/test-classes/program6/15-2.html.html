In our study we found that the activity of class I ADH in the wall of abdominal aortic aneurysm is about 48% lower than that in normal aorta. The signiﬁcant decrease of class I isoenzymes of alcohol dehydrogenase activity in aneurysm, diminishes some additional function of this isoenzyme in the wall of vessels. Class I isoenzymes of alcohol dehydrogenase are able to reduce aldehyde products generated from lipid peroxidation (e.g. 4-hydroxynonenal) (Sellin et al. 1991). These lipid-derives aldehydes can react with cysteine or lysine residues of proteins to form a type of advanced glycation end products (AGEs). It has been accepted that AGEs in low concentrations contribute to the regulation of normal tissue remodeling, but when found in excess are pathogenic (Baynes and Thorpe 2000; Mizutani et al. 2002). The ADH isoenzymes perform a protective function by the reduction of the highly electrophilic products of lipid peroxidation to their less toxic alcohols. Moreover, ADH I can catalyze reactions many substrates include products of catecholamines and serotonin meta- bolism (Consalvi et al. 1986). ADH I also takes part in the oxidation of retinol to retinal, the ﬁrst step in biosynthesis of retinoic acid. It is a principal mediator of retinoids actions required for maintaining epithelia in a differentiated state (De Luca 1991; Kurlandsky et al. 1995). The requirement of different extrahepatic tissues for retinoic acid is thought to be met largely by retinoic acid generated in situ from retinol. It is obvious that disruption of elements of retinoid homeo- static system has been identiﬁed in disturbances of proliferation and differentiation processes (Napoli 1999).

The wall of human aorta also contain the class III ADH isoenzyme. This isoenzyme class participates in the metabolism of endogenous long-chain alcohols and aldehydes and in the oxidation of S- hydroxymethylglutathione (Koivusalo et al. 1989). ADH III is the enzyme responsible for formaldehyde metabolism, and also elimina- tion of major product of lipid peroxidation. In our study we found that

the activity of ADH III isoenzyme did not exhibit differences between wall of aortic aneurysm and wall of normal aorta.

The activity of class IV ADH is about 5 times lower than that of class I. Both classes I and IV are highly active in transformation of alcohols and aldehydes of importance for the maintenance of the epithelia. ADH IV can oxidize retinol to retinal, an important reaction for the produc- tion of retinoic acid, an essential modulator of epithelial development (Ang et al. 1996). Moreover class IV similar to class I plays a signiﬁcant role a in the elimination of aldehydes resulting from lipid peroxidation (Boleda et al. 1993). Therefore it is reasonable that set of ADH classes in blood vessels constitutes a defence mechanism against cytotoxic alde- hydes generated by lipid peroxidation in a highly oxygenated tissue. The present study demonstrates that the activity of class IV ADH was insigniﬁcantly lower in the aneurysm than in wall of unchanged aorta.

Blood vessels contribute signiﬁcantly to extrahepatic ethanol metabolism. ADH of blood vessels may also have a role in the ﬁrst- pass metabolism of ethanol (Allali-Hassani et al. 1997). The presence of some level of ethanol metabolism in blood vessels has been interpreted as a positive mechanism of preventing cardiovascular diseases through minimalization of lipoprotein oxidation (Bello et al. 1994). In our study we have found that the total activity of ADH was insigniﬁcantly lower in the wall of aortic aneurysm than in unchanged aortic wall. The wall of aneurysm and healthy aorta also contain a little amount of aldehyde dehydrogenase. It is important that alcohol dehydrogenase activity is much higher than aldehyde dehydrogenase. The ratio of ADH to ALDH activity is about 48 : 1 in aneurysm tissue (46 : 1 in wall of healthy aorta). This would suggest that the blood vessels, especially with aneurysm has a higher capability to oxidation of exogenous ethanol than to remove the acetaldehyde. Moreover, ethanol metabolism by vascular ADH could interfere with proposed function of this enzyme in the elimination of lipid peroxidation products, therefore it contributes to vascular damage.
