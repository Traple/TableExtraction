Activity of Yeast Alcohol Dehydrogenases on Benzyl Alcohols and Benzaldehydes. Characterization of ADH1 from Saccharomyces carlsbergensis and Transition State Analysis

Suresh Pal, Doo-Hong Park, and Bryce V. Plapp* Department oi Biochemistry, The University of Iowa, Iowa City, Iowa 52242-1109 USA

Abstract

The substrate specificities of yeast alcohol dehydrogenases I and II from Saccharomyces cerevisiae (SceADHl and SceADH2) and Saccharomyces carlsbergensis (ScbADHl) were studied. For this work, the gene for the S. carlsbergensis ADH1 was cloned, sequenced and expressed. The amino acid sequence of ScbADHl differs at four positions as compared to SceADHl, including substitutions of two glutamine residues with glutamic acid residues, and has the same sequence as the commercial yeast enzyme, which apparently is prepared from S. carlsbergensis. The electrophoretic mobilities of ScbADHl, SceADHZ and commercial ADH are similar. The kinetics and specificities of ScbADHl and SceADHl acting on branched, long-chain and benzyl alcohols are very similar, but the catalytic efficiency of SceADHZ is about 1O to lOO-fold higher on these substrates. A three dimensional structure of SceADHl shows that the substrate binding pocket has Met-270, whereas SceADHZ has Leu-270, which allows larger substrates to bind. The reduction of a series of p-substituted benzaldehydes catalyzed by SceADHZ is significantly enhanced by electron- withdrawing groups, whereas the oxidation of p-substituted aromatic alcohols may be only slightly affected by the substituents. The substituent effects on catalysis generally reﬂect the effects on the equilibrium constant for the reaction, where electron-withdrawing substituents favor alcohol. The results are consistent with a transition state that is electronically similar to the alcohol, supporting previous results obtained with commercial yeast ADH.

Alcohol dehydrogenase; Substrate specificity; Enzyme kinetics; Quantitative structure-activity relationships; Transition state; X-ray crystallography

1. Introduction

The zinc-containing, medium-chain alcohol dehydrogenases catalyze the interconversion of ethanol and NAD+ to acetaldehyde and NADH, and they are commonly found in bacteria, yeasts, plants and animals [l—3]. Mammalian ADHs react efficiently with a great variety of alcohols, aldehydes, and ketones, but yeast ADHs have more restricted specificities and are most active on ethanol and acetaldehyde [4,5].
