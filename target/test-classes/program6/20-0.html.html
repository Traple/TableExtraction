Abstract

Yeast alcohol dehydrogenase (Y-ADH) is widely studied for its biotechnological importance and various attempts to improve its catalytic properties have been made. In this paper, a catalytically active metal-substituted Y-ADH was prepared in vitro by substituting one zinc atom with copper. EPR and Raman spectroscopy suggest that copper maintains the same co-ordination geometry as zinc in native Y-ADH. The active Cu-ADH shows lower substrate afﬁnity and lower speciﬁc activity (SA) than native ADH, but greater than a previously obtained Co-ADH. Furthermore, Cu-ADH maintains its catalytic efﬁciency in a wider pH range than native enzyme. © 2002 Elsevier Science B.V. All rights reserved.

1. Introduction

Yeast alcohol dehydrogenase (Y-ADH, E.C. 1.1.1.1) catalyses the last and rate-limiting step of the alcoholic fermentation [1,2]. This fermentation has long been studied for its implications in wine and beer production [3,4] and recently, an increasing interest has been shown in its application for biotechnological processes of bio- conversion of different organic wastes into ethanol to be used as solvent or fuel [5-7]. For these reasons, many attempts to modify Y-ADH to improve its cata- lytic properties have been made by using metal [8-10] or aminoacid [11,12] substitution.

Y-ADH is a tetramer (150 kDa), containing zinc with catalytic and structural functions [13,14]. The real num- ber of both catalytic and structural zinc atoms is still in question [10,13—16].

Atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) and activity studies on the native and the cobalt-substituted ADH, performed in controlled conditions to avoid zinc contamination, showed a zinc content of 4 g-atoms per molecule of protein (i.e. 1 g-atom per monomer) and demonstrated the complete loss of catalytic activity, when 1 g-atom of zinc was removed. Contemporarily, total reactivation was observed by adding 1 g-atom of zinc to the inactivated enzyme. The addition of 1 g-atom of cobalt produced a modiﬁed ADH, which showed lower SA and substrate afﬁnity in comparison with native ADH, but greater thermal resistance and pH stability in a wider range than the native enzyme.

Until now, only manganese [8] and cobalt [9,16,17] have been employed as possible zinc replacements due to their similarity in ion charge, dimension and co-ordi- nation geometry. Since copper(II) also shows many common features with zinc(II) [18], we considered the possibility of obtaining a copper-substituted ADH.
