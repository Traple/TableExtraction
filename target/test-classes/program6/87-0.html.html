The enzymatic and structural properties of alligator liver alcohol dehydrogenase have been determined. Aliphatic and alicyclic alcohols serve as substrates for this first reptilian form of the enzyme characterized, with Km values decreasing rapidly from methanol to hexanol, as for the human class I enzymes, and a Km of 1.2 mM for ethanol at pH 9.9. The N-terminus of the 374- residue protein chain is acetyl-blocked.

The enzyme is related in descending order to class I > III > V > II of the structurally charac- terized mammalian alcohol dehydrogenases. This observation is compatible with the presence of a I/III ancestral line. Differences of the enzyme classes exceed those of the species, suggesting an early origin of the classes. Within its enzyme class, the reptilian protein is most closely related to the avian form (82% residue identities), and is closer to the human than to the amphibian form (76%, versus 69%, respectively). This establishes class I alcohol dehydrogenase as an enzyme having fairly constant rate of change during much of vertebrate evolution, ==10% residue differ- ences/100 million years of separation between pairs compared.

Residues interacting with the substrate and coenzyme are largely conserved. In the alligator enzyme, there are only four replacements in the substrate pocket compared with the human class I y subunit, and those are not known to have functional roles. These properties account for the kinetic parameters, and suggest distinct metabolic functions for the class I enzyme in vertebrates.

Comparisons of the enzymes of the different vertebrate lines reveal that segment patterns are characteristic features of the class I enzymes. Three segments are ‘variable’, while two are ’con- stant’, and both these types of segment are identical with those of the classes. There is extensive variability in close proximity to the active site of the enzyme and this appears to constitute a

fundamental property of class I liver alcohol dehydrogenases in general.

Medium-chain alcohol dehydrogenases are members of a large enzyme family, encompassing many different enzyme activities: alcohol, sorbitol, xylitol, and threonine dehydroge- nases, as well as quinone reductase [1]. Thus far, they corre- spond to minimally nine genes in the human, seven of which encode alcohol dehydrogenases of five different classes [1- 4]. Comparisons of alcohol dehydrogenases and their charac- teristics in vertebrate lines are essential to establish the origin and metabolic functions of this complex system. The purification of the major form of alcohol dehydroge- nase from alligator liver and the characterization of its enzy- matic and structural properties suggest a conserved function but variable structure of all the vertebrate class I enzyme forms. This adds insight regarding the nature of the enzyme classes, suggesting that they have distinct metabolic func- tions. In addition, the results establish basic patterns for the class I enzyme. They define variable and constant segments that constitute basic characteristics of the enzyme.
