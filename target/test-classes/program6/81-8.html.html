By comparison, studies on carbonic anhydrase have shown that dissociation of the intrinsic zinc ion proceeds faster by chelation with the tridentate dipi- colinic acid than with bidentate 1,10-phenanthroline [43]. A kinetic study [44] has clearly ascertained that the dissociation of the catalytic metal ion in cobalt carbonic anhydrase proceeds via formation of a binary complex between the enzyme and 1,10-phenanthro- line. Although no exact comparison has been made so far, the same appears to hold for horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase: complexes with monodentate and bidentate ligands like imidazole and 1,10-phenanthro- line have been crystallized and studied by X-ray crystal- lography [45] which requires storage of the crystals for days or weeks. The extraction of the catalytic zinc ion with dipicolinic acid (this work) can be done within a few hours, whereas the treatment with 2,3- dimercaptopropanol or sodium diethyldithiocarba- mate takes several days, cf. Vallee et al. [46].

Thus the low exchange rate at the catalytic site could be explained by assuming the dissociation of the metal ion to be the rate-limiting step, according to Eqn (2a). The successful preparation of the crucial horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase intermediate, H4 Zn(n)2-enzyme, is, of course, not a proof per se in favour of this mechanism. However, it opens the way also to studies of the kinetics and energetics of the recombination of the metal with the empty catalytic site. The ligand-mediated dissociation of the non- catalytic zinc ion seems to be less favored, since the metal ion is tetrahedrally surrounded by four sulfur ligands. Although this latter binding site is expected to be thermodynamically more stable, it is the faster- exchanging site for kinetic reasons. We believe, that the interpretation of the properties of both metal binding sites will ultimately depend on a thorough knowledge of the protein structure and its energetics. The dissociation rates of the catalytic and the non- catalytic metal ions (Zn and Co) seem to be sufﬁciently low to prevent completely any exchange of metal ions between both kinds of binding sites, since neither the ‘blue hybrid’ nor the ‘green hybrid’ showed any ten- dency of metal exchange, either in crystals or in solu- tion. This is in accordance with data on the zinc enzyme labeled in both positions with “Zn [1]. We wish to stress that no crystallographic data are avail- able concerning the packing of the enzyme molecules in the crystal lattice of horse liver alcohol dehydro- genase. Therefore we do not yet know, whether the noncatalytic zinc ions are unreactive towards chelatin g agents due to their inaccessibility in the crystal lattice or due to other reasons.

The third conclusion is that catalytic metal ions play an important structural role. In the three-dimen- sional structure of horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase

it can be seen that the ligands of the catalytic zinc ions stem from rather distant parts of the polypeptide chain (Cys-46, His-67 and Cys-174), whereas the four ligands of the noncatalytic zinc ions are derived from amino acids situated in a limited part of the sequence (cysteines 9'7, 100, 103 and 111). Moreover, each active-site pocket contains residues from the other subunit [47]. Therefore it seems plausible that removal of the metal ions from the catalytic sites may lead to a considerable distortion of the tertiary structure around these sites which also might affect the quater- nary structure of the enzyme. In the crystal, the three- dimensional structure of the H4 Zn(n)2-enzyme seems to be preserved, since divalent metal ions such as zinc or cobalt are incorporated in the empty sites at rela- tively high metal concentrations. In solution, a partial unfolding is likely to occur. Since the metal concen- trations which were used for reconstitution of the crystalline enzyme lead to irreversible losses of the dis- solved H4 Zn(n)2-enzyme, we may conclude that the pathway of folding of the polypeptide chain is inﬂu- enced by divalent metal ions in a complex manner. At high concentrations, metal ions may bind at sites created by the unfolding of the polypeptide chain and thus prevent correct refolding. Probably the afﬁnity for metal ions will be lower in these random sites; at low metal concentrations the catalytic sites, which are high-afﬁnity sites, will be preferentially saturated. Since there is an equilibrium between the folded and unfolded forms of the H4Zn(n);-enzyme, the concen- tration of the latter will gradually decrease as the process of reconstitution goes to completion.

The reversibility of the unfolding of H4 Zn(n)2- enzyme is obvious from the ease of crystallizing this species and from the observation that recrystallized H4 Zn(n)2-enzyme can be reconstituted at high metal concentrations, as expected. The participation of zinc ions in the reactivation and reconstitution of totally denatured, metal-free horse liver alcohol dehydro- genase was shown by Rudolph ct al. [49]. In this system too, both the rate and extent of reactivation was dependent on the concentration of zinc ions, although no distinction could be made between recon- stitution of catalytic and noncatalytic sites. The critical role of the metals for the maintenance of an intact tertiary structure for correct insertion of the catalytic metal ions could explain in part why extrac- tion of the catalytic zinc ion in solution, which was achieved earlier [46], is followed by irreversible loss of activity, especially when rigorous anaerobic condi- tions are not maintained and the procedure is extended over a long time period. Thus we expect the critical step in the preparation of any active-site-substituted metallo-alcohol dehydrogenase to be the removal of the catalytic zinc ion, which is easily achieved in the conformationally stabilized crystalline state.
