Although in vivo substitution cannot be performed, because of the toxicity of high copper concentrations in culture media, we reported, nevertheless, in this paper, the preparation of an in vitro copper-reactivated form of Y-ADH and its characterisation as far as its catalytic properties and metal complexation geometry are con- cerned. These various hypotheses are considered in the paper.

2. Materials and methods

To avoid zinc and copper contamination, all materi- als have been previously puriﬁed as described [16].

2.1. Chemicals

Native enzyme, in lyophilised form, and Chelex 100 was obtained from Fluka. The Y-ADH purity was veriﬁed by the sodium dodecyl sulphate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE; 7.5% bis-acrylamide) method. Phosphate buffer 0.15 M, pH 7.6 (PBS) was prepared using suprapur reagents (disodium hydrogen phosphate-sodium dihydrogen phosphate) obtained from Merck. Merck also supplied ethanol, l-propanol, l-butanol and 2-propanol.

2.2. Activity measurements

Kinetic assays to determine the SA were carried out at 340 nm, by means of a Hitachi U-3200 spectrophoto- meter, using PVC cuvettes and continuously stirring, according to the procedure and conditions reported in the literature [17]. The evaluation of kinetic constants was performed according to the Lineweaver—Burk methodology [19].

2.3. Inactivation ana’ reactivation of the native enzyme

Inactivation of the native enzyme was carried out by incubating the Y-ADH with Chelex 100, as previously described [16]. The pseudo-apoenzyme reactivation curves were obtained after the ﬁltration of the Chelex 100 with 0.45 um ﬁlters and by incubating the sample with an increasing concentration of Copper(II) sulphate

(Merck) by stirring for 1 h at room temperature. The same reactivation was carried out with increasing con- centrations of Zinc chloride (Merck).

2.4. Cu-substitutea’ ADH stability

The behaviour of the Cu-substituted enzyme as a function of temperature and pH was evaluated in the range between 15 and 50 °C, and pH 6-9. Moreover, substrate speciﬁcity of the Cu-substituted ADH was tested using ethanol, l-propanol, l-butanol and 2- propanol as substrates.

2.5. Copper analysis

The amount of copper in the modiﬁed enzyme was evaluated by electrothermal AAS, using a Perkin- Elmer 5000 spectrophotometer, as reported for cobalt determination in a previous paper [16].

The metal co-ordination of the Cu-modiﬁed ADH was investigated by means of EPR and Raman tech- niques. In both cases, to obtain well-resolved spectra, a lyophilised sample was required. The sample solution was then concentrated by ultraﬁltration on centriplus ﬁlters (Amicon) with a 10 kDa molecular cut-off (3000 rpm, 2 h). The concentrate solution was lyophilised in a Uniequip (Stepbio) lyophiliser and the dry enzyme recovered.

EPR spectra were recorded on a Bruker ESP300E (band X), equipped with a liquid nitrogen cryostat, using a receiver gain of 2.00 >< 104, a modulation fre- quency of 100.0000 kHz and a modulation amplitude of 2.016 G. The centre ﬁeld was at 3100.00 G and fre- quency and power were, respectively, 9.4184140 GHz and 1.00 >< 10 mW.

Raman spectra were recorded on a FT Raman Bruker RFS 100 equipped with an Nd, YAG laser (1064 nm) with a resolution of 4 cm‘ 1. Each spectrum was recorded by performing 300 scans (laser power = 120 mW) in the range between 50 and 4000 cm_1.

3. Results and discussion

The zinc removal was performed by competition between the enzyme and a chelating resin (Chelex 100) as previously described [16]. This procedure allows us to obtain a partially apoenzymatic form, which shows a residual SA of 10—20% of that of the native enzyme and can be reactivated by adding zinc or cobalt [16]. As the achievement of complete inactivation increases the probability of irreversible denaturing effects, as a conse- quence, the zinc removal is stopped before reaching total inactivation.
