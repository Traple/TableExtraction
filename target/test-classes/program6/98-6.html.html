Furthermore, as shown in Figure 4, acetyl-CoA synthetase (8802059, 8802863, and 8803203), which was annotated to degrade acetate to acetyl CoA, was found to be at least 2-fold more abundant (in protein level) in cultures grown in ethanol only, compared to the control (see Table 1). The increased expression of acetyl-CoA synthetase at the protein level suggests that acetyl-CoA intermediates are formed from ethanol deg- radation. Acetolactate synthase large subunit homolog (8802962), involved in converting two pyruvate molecules to carbon dioxide and acetolactate, was also increased in expression by 2-fold in ethanol cultures. Similarly, the pyruvate dehydroge- nase enzymes (8801525 and 8801526), believed to catalyze the conversion of pyruvate from acetyl CoA, were more highly expressed (>1.9-fold) in ethanol cultures. These preliminary

results support the postulated pathway in ethanol metabolism, suggesting that ethanol is catabolised into the central metabolic pathway via acetyl-CoA and pyruvate intermediates. However, further studies, including the detailed characterization of the enzymes involved along this annotated pathway, are required to verify the ethanol degradation pathway, as was done in the pentose oxidation pathway recently?
