recognizing changes in the coordination sphere due to conformational changes of the protein. The cobalt(II) ion (d?) can replace the metal in many zinc enzymes [21, 22]. These modiﬁed enzymes are catalytically active and the electronic spectra provide information about the coordination number and the geometry of the chromophoric metal. This technique has also led to deeper insight into the structure and function of horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase [23]. In the latter case a successful exchange of the active-site zinc is only possible in the crystalline state [7]. The horse liver EE isozyme can easily by crystallized and it is available in large quantities. In con- trast, the human class I isozymes are available only in small quantities, and crystallization is difficult. They easily denature during concentration so it is necessary to work with rather dilute solutions. In spite of these difficulties we were able to obtain an active-site-speciﬁc cobalt(II)-substituted B1B1 human liver alcohol dehydrogenase in solution. There are distinct differences in the rates of metal extraction between the isozymes containing the B1 or v1 subunits. With the aid of dipicolinic acid the active-site zinc ion can be removed within a few days from the B1 B1 isozyme, whereas it proved to be much more difficult to remove the catalytic zinc from the B1311 or y1y1 isozyme. The higher rate of metal extraction in the case of B 1 B 1 isozyme may be related to the increased lability compared to y1y1 isozyme [8]. The comparison between Co(c)2Zn(n)2 human liver B1B1 and Co(c)2Zn(n)2 horse liver enzyme allows the following conclusions.

a) According to the circular dichroic and electronic ab-

sorption spectra of the chromophoric cobalt(II) ion, the coor-

dination number and geometry of the active-site metal in B1 B 1 human isozyme is very similar to that of horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase.

b) In both enzymes the binding of the coenzyme NADH is accompanied by a conformational change of the protein from an “open” to a “closed” structure. This event is monitored by a 25-nm red shift of the visible absorption band of the active-site cobalt(II) ion. The change of the absorption maximum from 648 nm to 673 nm in the active-site cobalt(II)- substituted human liver B 1 B 1 isozyme opens the possibility of studying the dynamic processes of coenzyme binding.
