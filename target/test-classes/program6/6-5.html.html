Therefore it is not able to suppress the aggregation completely, as it cannot affect the pre-nucleation of the aggregation phase (delay time phase I). Pre-nucleation is a pre-construction stage for the association of biomolecules in the aggregation phenomenon. The results show that this phenomenon is an essential phase for the prevention and, ﬁnally, the suppression of the aggregation. It seems that hydrophobic interactions are a major factor in ADH aggregation. To conﬁrm the hydrophobic effect under the conditions of our study, we monitored the appearance of aggregation in the presence of oe-CyD with free phenylalanine. The potency of CyDs is often ascribed to the afﬁnity of the hydrophobic cavity for aromatic amino acids (especially phenylalanine), the surface exposure of which is affected by the temperature, duration of storage or chemicals, and can lead to protein association and aggregation [I]. In this way, oe-CyD can bind to the hydrophobic parts of the protein surface, leading to a hydrophilic mask, which increases the solubility of the protein. Phenylalanine is a purely hydrophobic amino acid, whereas tyrosine and tryptophan are less hydrophobic because of the hydroxy and NH groups in their aromatic side chain [5 I]. There are 38 phenylalanine residues in the structure of ADH (see Figure 7), but only four tryptophan residues [52], a majorfactor for aggregation by inter-subunit hydrophobic interactions under thermal pressures on the native conformation. This means that phenylalanine interacts with oz-CyD with high afﬁnity, obliterating the effect of the oz-CyD hydrophobic moiety in the presence of ADH. The phenylalanine competitive effect on oe-CyD as an anti-aggregation agent is in agreement with the other previous studies on the CyD-assisted refolding process [I6]. The change in the slope of the aggregation curve at the growth phase in the presence of phenylalanine supports the idea that the growth phase is the primary time during which oz-CyD affects and reduces the aggregation (the size and population of nuclei). As a result, this interaction (oe-CyD—ADH) is inherently hydrophobic, and it would be expected that the high ionic strength (adding 400 mM NaCl) could cause a stronger interaction and lead to the aggregation decrease. Figures 5 and 6 illustrate and conﬁrm the mentioned supposition for the oz-CyD/NaCl sample. For a more detailed examination of this process, we used DLS as a powerful technique for measuring the hydrodynamic size of the aggregates. This technique has been widely applied to the study of the aggregation behaviour of proteins in aqueous solutions. It provides information on the particle size distribution through the measurement of the ACF (autocorrelation function) of the scattered light, which is associated with the diffusion coefﬁcient of the particles. The mean hydrodynamic radius of the protein aggregates was calculated from the average diffusion coefﬁcient, using the Stokes-Einstein relation [53].
