The total turnover number (TTN), also called the cycle number, is deﬁned as mole product formed per mole co- factor consumed. There are different methods available to increase this number. The cofactor can be coupled di- rectly to an enzyme by means of a spacer (Mansson et al., 1982). The cofactor can be coupled to a water-soluble polymer like polyethylene glycol (PEG) (Biickmann et al., 1981). Thus, the enlarged cofactor can be retained together with the enzymes involved in a bioreactor by means of an ultraﬁltration membrane. For some enzymes such as glucose-fructose oxidoreductase the cofactor is bound tightly to the enzyme. In such a case no additional methods for its retainment are necessary (Zachariou and Scopes, 1986). Separation of the native cofactor and low- molecular-weight products can also be achieved in some cases with charged ultraﬁltration (Nidetzky et al., 1994; Rothig et al., 1990) or nanoﬁltration membranes. It is also possible to achieve high TTN for the native cofactor by using substrates of especially high or low water solubility. In the ﬁrst case, low cofactor concentrations in the pres- ence of high substrate concentrations can be used in the

feed. In the latter case, the water phase containing the cofactor can be recycled subsequent to the extraction of a hydrophobic product. This will be illustrated by two examples: L-tert-leucine 4 as a product with high solubility and (S )-1-phenyl-2-propanol 9 as a sparingly soluble product (Kruse et al., 1996). Furthermore, results for pro- duction of L-leucine 11 highlight the use of a polymer- enlarged cofactor.
