The structure of the recombinant medium chain alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) from the hyperthermophilic archaeon Aeropyrum pernix has been solved by the multiple anomalous dispersion technique using the signal from the naturally occurring zinc ions. The enzyme is a tetramer with 222 point group symmetry. The ADH monomer is formed from a catalytic and a cofactor-binding domain, wioth the overall fold similar to previously solved ADH structures. The 1.62 A resolution A. pernix ADH structure is that of the holo form, with the cofactor NADH bound into the cleft between the two domains. The electron density found in the active site has been interpreted to be octanoic acid, which has been shown to be an inhibitor of the enzyme. This inhibitor is positioned with its carbonyl oxygen atom forming the fourth ligand of the catalytic zinc ion. The struc- tural zinc ion of each monomer is present at only partial occupancy and in its absence a disulfide bond is formed. The enhanced thermal stability of the A. pernix ADH is thought to arise primarily from increased ionic and hydrophobic interactions on the subunit interfaces.

Introduction

The alcohol dehydrogenases (ADHs; EC 1.1.1.1) belong to the oxidoreductase family, a class of enzymes responsible for the catalysis of all biologi- cal oxidation and reduction reactions. ADHs catalyse the interconversion of alcohols with the corresponding aldehyde or ketone, using the nico- tinamide cofactors NAD(H) or NADP(H). The enzymes are distributed widely, and have been isolated from species throughout the three domains of life, Archaea, Bacteria and Eukarya.1'3

The ADHs may be separated into three distinct classes on the basis of molecular size and cofactor requirement? The type I, or medium-chain, ADHs may be dimeric (in higher eukaryotes) or tetra- meric (commonly seen in bacteria), but all contain approximately 370 residues per chain and are zinc-dependent. Horse liver ADH is an extensively studied example of this classf‘ Type II, or short- chain, ADHs have approximately 250 residues per chain, and rarely contain metals? The domain Archaea encompasses many species classified as extremophiles, microorganisms that inhabit environments at the limits of biological endurance. Extremophiles are not limited to the Archaea but the majority isolated to date belong to this domains In the last 3O years many extremo- philic microorganisms have been isolated from conditions previously thought incapable of sus- taining any life—form. Such environments include acidic hot springs, deep-sea hydrothermal vents and natural salt lakes. The organisms inhabiting them thrive in extreme conditions of salinity, temperature, pressure, and pH.

Hyperthermophiles grow optimally at tempera- tures of greater than 8O °C and are rarely able to grow below 6O °C.9 Thermophilic species of Bacteria and Archaea are diverse, but the hyperthermophiles are comprised almost entirely of Archaea. Enzymes isolated from the thermo- philic Archaea show remarkable stability to, amongst other factors, temperature and organic solvents, rendering them particularly suitable as industrial biocatalysts.
