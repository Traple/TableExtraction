Metal Ions

Pure sec-alcohol dehydrogenase “A” did not show any loss of activity in the presence of ethylenediamine tetra- acetic acid (EDTA) as metal-ion chelator. However, since tightly bound metal ions cannot be detected by this method, the final purification step (SEC) was coupled to an induc- tively coupled plasma-mass spectrometer (ICP-MS) (Hem- mens et al., 2000) in order to identify possible metal ions. From the investigated metals (Mg, Mn, Fe, Co, Zn, Cu, Mo) only zinc with its three major isotopes (64Zn, 66Zn, 68Zn) in their constitutional ratio was found at the UV- signal of the alcohol-dehydrogenase (Fig. 5, highest UV- signal). Moreover, it was shown that zinc was not removed from the enzyme, when the above experiment was per- formed in presence of EDTA, since the counts for Zn remained the same as in the absence of EDTA.

Cofactor Recycling

Using the less volatile cosubstrate methylisobutyl ketone (MIB K) and rac-l-phenylethanol as substrate, the formation of co-product and product was analyzed by gas chromatog- raphy by co-inj ection with reference material when purified ADH “A” was used in presence of NAD+. Analogous experiments were performed for the reduction mode. Ad- ditionally, the recycling of NADH during the reduction of 6-methyl-5-hepten-2-one was spectrophotometrically mo- nitored at AmaXNADH = 340 nm in presence and absence of 2-propanol (10% and 25% v/v). Whereas the concentration of NADH continuously decreased in the absence of cosub- strate (2-propanol), addition of 2-propanol led to an increase of NADH after a short period of equilibration (Fig. 6). Likewise, elevated cosubstrate concentrations resulted in

higher NADH-concentration towards the end of the experi- ment. The formation of the reaction-product (sulcatol) was proven by gas-chromatography, enhanced conversion was observed in presence of 2-propanol.

DISCUSSION

In this study, we identified and characterized an NADH- dependent sec-alcohol dehydrogenase, which is exception- ally stable towards elevated concentrations of 2-propanol and acetone. Thus, the enzyme can efficiently be used for the simultaneous oxidation of a sec-alcohol and cofactor recycling at the expense of acetone as hydrogen-acceptor on a preparative scale. Employing 2-propanol as cosubstrate/ hydrogen donor allows the efficient asymmetric reduction of ketones. Although the organism contains (at least) seven sec- alcohol dehydrogenases—as estimated by native gel electro- phoresis—which are able to accept 2-octanol, only a single enzyme possesses the desired organic solvent tolerance and can recycle the cofactor.

Stability tests in the presence of 2-propanol/acetone using an enzyme preparation after HIC proved that the enzyme does not need the cell wall or cell membrane as protectant but the protein itself tolerates 2-propanol up to 80% v/v (Fig. 1) and acetone up to 50% v/v. To the best of our knowledge, no ADH has been reported to date with com- parable stability. In line with the exceptional chemostabil- ity, the temperature optimum for the enzyme is between 50—60°C.

The native form of the enzyme elutes from size exclusion chromatography as a homodimer composed of subunits of 38 kDa. Alcohol dehydrogenases from Thermomicrobium roseum (Yoon et al., 2002), and Mycobacterium tuber- culosis (De Bruyn et al., 1981) appear as homodimers of larger subunits (80 kDa). ADHs from Clostridium beijer- inckii (Ismaiel et al., 1993), Nocardia fusca (Xie et al., 1999), Alcaligenes eutrophus (Steinbuchel and Schlegel, 1984), Thermoanaerobacter ethanolicus (Burdette and Zei- kus, 1994; Bryant et al., 1988), Rhodococcus erythropolis (Schenkels and Duine, 2000), and Bacillus stearothermo- philus (Guagliardi et al., 1996) display a similar subunit- size (~38 kDa) and were found to be homotetramers (Zelinski et al., 1994).

Pure ADH “A,” which can be stored over l0 months without significant loss of activity, was obtained in 8% yield. The low yield may result from the inaccurate determination of the exact initial specific activity of the crude cell free extract caused by interference by other alcohol dehydro- genases. Another reason might be losses due to adsorption of the enzyme onto glass surfaces or membranes due to the high hydrophobicity of the enzyme.

The stereopreference of the dehydrogenase follows Prelog’s rule (Prelog, 1964) exclusively yielding (S)-1- phenylethanol by reduction of acetophenone. Furthermore, ADH ‘ ‘A’ ’ shows exquisite regioselectivity for sec-alcohols, while primary alcohols and aldehydes remain untouched.
