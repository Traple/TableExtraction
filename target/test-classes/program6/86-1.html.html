In order to obtain separate data for the energy of binding and activation of substrates, at least one step of an enzyme-catalyzed reaction, either binding or activation, has to be determined independently of the ki- netically evaluated steady-state parameters. The most simpli- ﬁed mechanism, which describes all the kinetic experiments of the presented investigation, is the ordered bi-bi mechanism [21]. The central step of this mechanism is the catalytic or hydride-transfer step of an oxidation or reduction reaction, respectively.

Aldehyde or ketone reductions in the presence of pyrazole [22] and alcohol oxidations in the presence of isobutyramide [23] can be studied in stopped-flow experiments under single- turnover conditions. The extrapolation of substrate-concen- tration-dependent measurements of such reactions results in rate constants which can be directly related to the hydride- transfer step, provided the subsequent steps in the mechanism are fast in comparison to the central catalytic steps [24]. Thus, free energies of activation for the catalytic step of LADH- catalyzed reactions can be obtained directly.

As will be shown, a combination of steady-state and single- turnover kinetics for substrate pairs allows the evaluation of the free energies of activation and binding, using the deﬁ- nitions of km and Km in terms of the kinetic rate constants of the ordered bi-bi mechanism ﬁrst shown by Plapp [25].

The system (S)-2-butanol, (R)-2-butanol/2-butanone of- fers particular advantages.

First, the rate constants of hydride transfer and asso- ciation/dissociation processes are of magnitudes which allow their separate determination and also fulﬁll the criteria ad- vanced by Pettersson for the analysis of an ordered bi-bi mechanism [24].

Secondly, the limited enantioselectivity permits analysis of the two parallel pathways. Given a complete set of data, it is then possible to investigate the influence of structural and/or electronic changes in the protein and the substrate on the stereoselectivity of the catalytic pathway.

In the present investigation we have taken advantage of the speciﬁc replacement of the catalytic metal ion by Co(II). Co(II)LADH shows great structural similarity to the native Zn(II)LADH [26]. In recent years, the extensive study of Co(II)LADH has made it possible to observe intermediate complexes and different conformational states of the enzyme, based on the unique spectroscopic properties of the Co(II) ion in this environment [27 -- 29].

By comparing the inﬂuence of both the substrate structure and the metal ion on the individual rate constants, it is possible to discriminate substrate-protein and substrate-metal interac- tions, which together control the kinetic behaviour of the enzyme.

Thus, we have obtained kinetic rate constants, which en- able us to explain the substrate speciﬁcity of LADH for acyclic secondary alcohols and also to determine the stereochemical course of the reduction of the corresponding ketones.

LADH, YADH and the coenzymes (NAD free acid, grade 1, 100%; NADH, disodium salt grade 1, 100%) were pur- chased from Boehringer Mannheim.

LADH was further puriﬁed by recrystallization from 50 mM Tris/HCI, pH 8.0, with t-butanol as the precipitant (up to 15% by vol). The enzyme used migrated as a single band in native PAGE, corresponding to the homogenous major ethanol-active isoenzyme.

From the recrystallized enzyme, the speciﬁcally active-site- substituted Co(II) derivative was prepared as described by Maret et al. [27], except that the catalytic metal-ion-depleted enzyme had also been recrystallized before incubation with Co(II). The degree of metal substitution of the species used in this investigation was found to be 97 i 10/0 by atomic absorption spectroscopy.

For kinetic experiments, the concentrations of active enzyme have been determined by active-site titrations according to Einarsson et al. [32]. Enzyme concentrations are always given in terms of active sites.

In order to reduce nonspeciﬁc side-reactions in quantita- tive experiments, YADH, LADH and the coenzymes NAD and NADH had to be further puriﬁed. The substrates and inhibitors used were of the highest available purity, ketones were further puriﬁed by repeated fractionated distillation and inhibitors (isobutyramide and pyrazole) by recrystallization.
