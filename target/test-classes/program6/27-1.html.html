Stability, and in particular thermostability, is an important functional property of an enzyme. It has been reported that several enzymes are much more thermostable in organic solvents than in water [5-7]. Alcohol dehydrogenases in general are temperature labile and yeast alcohol dehydro- genase (YADH) is somewhat unstable even at 25 °C [8]. In addition to thermostability, the broad substrate speciﬁcity of alcohol dehydroge- nase from T hermoanaerobacter (formerly Ther- moanaerobium) brockii (TBADH) attracted our attention to compare the behaviour of these two related enzymes. Thus a comparative study on the inﬂuence of a number of organic solvents on the activity and thermostability of TBADH and YADH was carried out.

Results suggest that in addition to the general rules outlined in the literature in relation to the theoretical and practical aspects of non-aqueous enzymology, some speciﬁc inter-related details may also be involved. The latter being determined by speciﬁc structural properties of the enzyme of interest and the particular physico-chemical na- ture of the solvent to be utilized in each individual situation.

2. Materials and methods

Yeast and T. brockii alcohol dehydrogenase, NAD+ (99% purity) and NADP+ (95% purity) as powders obtained from Sigma (St. Louis, MO, USA). All organic solvents were of analytical grade and obtained from Merck (Darmstadt, Ger- many) and kept dry over molecular sieves.

2.1. Enzyme assay and kinetic studies

YADH activity was determined in a spec- trophotometer (Shimadzu-l60) at 25 °C using the method described previously [9]. TBADH activity was measured at 65 °C, following the reduction

of 0.5 mM NADP+ (and monitoring the forma- tion of NADPH) at 340 nm in the assay mixture containing 150 mM 2-propanol in 50 mM potas- sium phosphate, pH 7.8. Enzyme concentrations were determined by measuring absorbance at 280 nm and using the absorption coefﬁcients Afffn = 12.6 for YADH [l0] and 2:234 >< 104 M-lcm“ for TBADH [ll].

For kinetic studies, the reaction mixture (l ml) contained various concentrations of NADP+ /2- propanol for TBADH or NAD+/ethanol for YADH in 5O mM potassium phosphate buffer, pH 7.8. Rates of NAD(P+) reduction were fol- lowed at 340 nm in the spectrophotometer and analyzed by bisubstrate rate equation [l2]. Data from inhibition studies were analyzed using the EZ-Fit program [l3].

2.2. Thermal stability studies in organic solvents

In a typical enzyme stability study, l mg of the enzyme powder was dissolved in l ml of 5O mM potassium phosphate, pH 7.8 and divided into 100 ul aliquots in glass tubes and then lyophilized. Lyophilization did not result in any detectable activity loss. One hundred microliter of phosphate buffer (control) or the desired solvents were sepa- rately added to samples which were subsequently heated at various temperatures for 3 h. Upon cooling to room temperature, the tubes were opened and the solvent removed to dryness by evaporation under vaccum. Upon addition of the buffer, activities were determined.

2.3. Preparation of water /organic cosolvent media

Samples were prepared by mixing required amounts of the components (organic cosolvent, aqueous buffer, enzyme and substrate solution) in a spectrophotometer cell. The pH of reaction mixtures were determined using the Henderson- Hasselbach equation and P-nitrophenol as an in- dicator as described elsewhere [l4].
