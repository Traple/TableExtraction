Discussion

Full genomic sequencing of N. crassa led to the establish- ment of the N. crassa database at the Broad Institute (http:// www.broad.mit.edu). Searching of the N crassa database allowed identiﬁcation of two alcohol dehydrogenases, ADH1 (NCU01754) and ADH3 (NCU02476), recently renamed from a hypothetical ADH. Elimination of introns by ampliﬁcation from cDNA enabled the cloning and expression of two active N crassa ADHs in recombinant E. coli. Homology alignment among fungi ADHs, prefer- ence of alcohols and cofactors, and carboxylate ester pro- duction experiments were carried out to characterize ADH] and ADH3 from N crassa. The bli-4 gene of N crassa had already been identiﬁed as encoding a mitochondrial short- chain alcohol dehydrogenase-like protein induced by blue light (Bruchez et al. 1996). Alignment of this protein with N crassa ADHs showed about 20% similarity. Usually homology alignment among ADH isozymes in yeasts such as S. cerevisiae and P. stipitis provided high scores of similarity and identity. N crassa ADH] and ADH3 were deﬁcient in mitochondrial anchoring peptides. Mitochon- drial ADHs such as S. cerevisiae ADH3 (Young and Pilgrim 1985) and C. boidinii ADH2 and ADH3 (Yurimoto et al. 2004) contain up to 25 N-terminal amino acids that act to localize these proteins to the mitochondrial membrane. Amino acid sequence analysis suggested that N crassa ADH] and ADH3 might be cytoplasmic proteins and would be appropriate to include in the ADH superfamily Group I with an NAD(P)+-requirement and Zn-dependent activity and had approximately 350 residues per subunit. In spite of these motifs within the sequence and its accumulation as a soluble protein at about half of the total expression level, N crassa ADH3 has low activity of alcohol consumption for ethanol and other alcohols (data not shown). It was reported that ADH] from Gordonia sp. showed high activities on ethanol and 1-butanol, but any activity of ADH3 on the same alcohols was not detectable (Kotani et al. 2003). A transcriptional analysis of N crassa in response to glucose

starvation proposed that ADH] and ADH3 likely played roles in ethanol consumption and synthesis, respectively (Xie et al. 2004).

N crassa ADH] preferentially acted on primary alcohols without branched chains. Similar to ADHs from yeasts such as C. boidinii (Murdanoto et al. 1997), C. utilis and Hansenula polymorpha (Verduyn et al. 1988), high alcohol dehydrogenase activity on long chain alcohols such as 1- pentanol, l-hexanol, and 1-octanol was observed. Gene knock-out of ADH] and ADH2 in S. cerevisiae reduced methyl formate synthesizing activity (Kusano et al. 1998), proposing the possibility of the action of N crassa ADH] in carboxylate ester synthesis. N crassa ADH] was able to oxidize hemiacetals and produce carboxylate esters. Shorter alcohols and aldehydes were more reactive substrates for carboxylate ester production. Similarly, a commercial ADH of baker’s yeast possessed the highest oxidation activity on the hemiacetal formed from methanol and formaldehyde (Kusano et al. 1998). They supposed that hemiacetal structure resembled a primary three-carbon alcohol struc- ture, and the baker’s yeast ADH preferred hemiacetals formed from short chain alcohols and aldehydes.

To increase the titer of carboxylate esters, reaction conditions such as temperature, acidity, metal requirement, and reaction inhibitors could be optimized, and enzyme reactors could be formed with immobilized ADHs. In addition, it will be of interest to investigate the in vivo formation of carboxylate esters by ADHs in Neurospora species.
