The blue-shifts observed for some enzyme-NADH complexes have been suggested to be a result of either a non-polar environment [41] or a positive charge in the vicinity of the dihydropyridine ring [42]. Drosophila Adh diﬂers from horse liver Adh as it lacks the blue-shift in the binary enzyme-NADH complex and in the ternary enzyme~NAD*—pyrazole complex. With Drosophila Adh, a 10-15 nm blue-shift appears when the ternary enzyme-NAD(H)—acetone complex is irreversibly formed (Table 1). This indicates that the nicotinamide moiety of the coenzyme in the converted enzyme does not share the same environment as the nicotinamide moiety of the coenzyme in the reversibly formed enzyme-NADH and enzyme—NAD"—pyrazole com- plexes. As the absorption coeﬂicient e340. for the NAD(H)—acetone adduct is 4.5 x 103 M‘1-cm'1 [40], the absorbance of 0.043 for the 328 nm absorption peak of the converted enzyme (5 pM) indicates that the enzyme contains 9.3 [LM of bound coenzyme. This was also the case when the calculation was based on the absorbance diﬂerence at 260 nm, which showed that the converted enzyme contained one coenzyme molecule per active site.

Both NADH and the NAD(H)—acetone adduct give rise to ﬂuorescence when the excitation wavelength is around their absorption spectrum maximum of 340 nm [43,44]. When NADH forms a binary complex with Drosophila Adh the wavelength maximum (Amax) of the NADH ﬂuorescence changes from 460 nm to 424 nm and a 20-fold increase in the ﬂuorescence intensity occurs [26,39]. This contrasts with the converted enzyme, where excitation at 325 or 330 nm resulted in a very low ﬂuorescence compared with that of NADH, and no peak could be detected. This lack of ﬂuorescence enhancement also indicates that the nicotinamide moiety of the coenzyme in the converted enzyme is exposed to an environment different from that in the reversibly formed enzyme-coenzyme complexes.
