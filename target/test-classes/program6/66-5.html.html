The oxidation of the substrate by htADH involves proton and hydride equivalent transfers (Chart l). In the studies of horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase, both steps have been considered to be partially or largely facilitated by confor- mational motions within the active site (17, 19). Short-range local motions are often coupled to longer-range conforma- tional motions (44, 45). In htADH, the thermal motion of the cofactor domain shapes the NAD+ conformation, allow- ing it to directly assist both proton and hydride equivalent transfer. The correlated motions between NAD+ and several constituents of the cofactor domain, such as fragments A—D, are larger at higher temperatures (Figure 2). This is particu- larly so for fragment C and NAD+. The distance between the side chain of Val26O and the NAD+ cofactor is shortened by the cofactor-binding domain motions, resulting in move- ment of the Val26O side chain toward the NAD+ and subsequent pushing of the NAD+ cofactor toward the substrate. The cofactor-binding domain motions are frozen at low temperatures, and Val26O moves away from the nicotinamide of NAD+. This change is due to the expansion of the cofactor-binding pocket. Thus, the hydride equivalent transfer distance is enlarged.

From the plots of transfer distance versus approaching angle (Figure 4), it can be seen that the htADH-NAD+'EtO_ complex exists in more than one average conformation (states l—3). At the optimal growth temperature, the cofactor- binding pocket is tightened by the pushing motions of the cofactor-binding domain. As the temperature decreases, these correlated motions are weakened and the cofactor-binding pocket expands. At 348 K, there are only two overlapping states, l and 2, while a nonoverlapping state 3 with a longer transfer distance is present at 323 K. As the temperature continues to decease to 298 K, state 3 predominates.

In our MD simulation of the htADH-NAD+'EtO_ com- plex, the percentage of the ground state present as NAC increases by 7-fold from 298 to 348 K. This is comparable to the ratio (~8-fold) of kw at 298 and 348 K. From the correlation motion calculation, it has been shown that motions of the htADH cofactor domain are highly correlated to NAD+ at high temperatures. This leads to the short- ening of the reaction coordinate for the hydride equivalent transfer. From both long-range and short-range local motions, htADH regulates the hydride equivalent transfer distance by the correlated motions between NAD+ and the cofactor domain.

The proton transfer accompanying ionization of EtOH to EtO_ and H+ in the first step of the alcohol dehydrogenase enzymatic reaction is mediated by the hydroxyl group of Thr4O in htADH (and a serine in horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase). The time-dependent distance has shown that Thr40—OH oscillates in hydrogen bonding between EtO_ and the 2'-hydroxyl group of NAD+ on a nanosecond time scale at 348 K, while this motion is frozen by a tight hydrogen bond with a substrate at 298 K. This observation is consistent with the suggestion by Klinman and co-workers that that some regions within htADH are less dynamic and demonstrate limited cooperative protein motion at reduced temperatures (1, 12, 22). At higher temperatures, oscillation of the Thr4O side chain between the substrate and NAD+ cofactor facilitates the proton transfer. The loss in the

CONCLUSION

The temperature dependence of the structure of the B. stearothermophilus alcohol dehydrogenase E-S complex (htADH-NAD+-EtO_) has been investigated by MD simula- tions at 298, 323, and 348 K. The structure of the E'S complex is affected by both long-range and short-range local motions. The conformation of NAD+ is largely determined by the motions of the cofactor domain, and the motions of NAD+ are well correlated to the cofactor domain at the optimal growth temperature but not at low temperatures. The correlated motions are crucial to a favorable Boltzmann distribution of reactive conformations of the htADH~NAD+ ~EtO_ complex. Multiple states were found from plots of d versus t9. State 3, which corresponds to the larger transfer distance, is not present at the optimal growth temperature. At 348 K, the nicotinamide ring of NAD+ is pushed toward the substrate to form a perfect conformation (NAC) in which the C4 atom of nicotinamide and the HD atom of substrate are brought together within van der Waals distance and the approaching angle is appropriate for the reaction. Further- more, the nicotinamide ring moves away from the substrate with a hydrogen bond between the NAD+ amide group and Val26O residue amide group at lower temperatures. As the temperature is lowered and the enzyme motions are frozen, the side chain of Thr4O sticks to the substrate instead of oscillating between the substrate and 2’-hydroxyl group of the NAD+ ribose, preventing the EtOH —> EtO_ + H+ reaction. Thus, both proton and hydride equivalent transfer in the alcohol dehydrogenase enzymatic reaction are modu- lated by the correlated motions between NAD+ and the cofactor domain.
