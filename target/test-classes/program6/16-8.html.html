The structural lobe surrounding the zinc ion forms one of the major points of contact in the ADH tetra- mer and formation of a disulfide bond may func- tion in maintaining the integrity of this lobe when zinc availability is limited.

During solution of the structure, a single additional zinc-binding site was observed in the asymmetric unit, on the interface between tetra- mers. The zinc ion was bound by Asp332 and His334 of subunit A, in addition to Glu96 of a sub- unit from a separate tetramer. The anomalous signal for this zinc site was comparable to those of the catalytic zinc ions, suggesting full occupancy, and stronger than the signal from either of the structural zinc ions. The additional zinc ion is thought to be merely an artefact of crystallisation, and it may be possible that zinc binding by this site is correlated to the depletion of zinc in the structural lobe.

Thermostability

Enzymes isolated from thermophilic or hyperthermophilic microorganisms display an inherent thermostability, yet often do not differ dramatically in sequence or overall fold when com- pared to their mesophilic counterpartszé-ZS As the number of high-resolution structures of hyper- thermophilic proteins rises, it is becoming increas- ingly apparent that there is no single, universal rule for molecular adaptation to life at high temperatures. Instead, thermostability appears to be achieved by a combination of features, with mechanisms varying significantly between species. Factors commonly reported as contributing towards thermostability include adoption of a higher oligomeric state, increased numbers of ionic interactions, reduction in size of surface loops, helix dipole stabilisation and increased hydrophobic interactions. This topic has been reviewed recently.”

As has been mentioned, the overall fold of the A. pernix ADH is very similar to that of the meso- philic horse liver ADH (Figure 3), though with reductions in the length of many surface loops. These shortened loops are observed also in the S. solfataricus ADH.“ The A. pernix ADH is found predominantly as a tetramer, whilst mammalian ADHs are dimeric. However, while the thermo- philic and hyperthermophilic type I ADHs isolated to date are all tetrameric, so are mesophilic bacterial ADHs. For this reason it is difficult to say that thermostability is dependent on a tetra- meric state, but it does seem that the thermostable ADHs have adapted to maintain the integrity of the tetramer. Extensive comparisons have been made of the T. brockii and C. beijerinckii ADHs, which share 70% sequence identity but have melt- ing points differing by 30 deg. C. It was concluded that enhanced thermal stability of the T. brockii

enzyme is due mainly to increased interactions at the subunit interfaces, reinforcing the quaternary structure.” One of the significant differences observed was the presence of an intersubunit ion- pair cluster on the interface between subunits A and D (and B and C) in T. brockii ADH, involving four residues and including two intersubunit ionic bonds. As discussed previously, each A. pernix ADH monomer makes four ionic bonds to neigh- bouring subunits, plus the symmetry-related equivalent of each, giving a total of 16 intersubunit ionic bonds within the tetramer. Of the four inter- subunit bonds formed by subunit A, three are on the A / B interface, which corresponds to the sub- unit interfaces in dimeric ADHs, while one is on the interface with subunit C. Analysis of the meso- philic horse liver ADH dimer (PDB code 1HEF) revealed no intersubunit ionic interactions. Com- parison of hydrophobic interactions in the two enzymes showed more extensive hydrophobic patches on subunit interfaces in the A. pernix ADH. It appears that these interactions reinforce the tetrameric structure, enhancing thermostability by mechanisms similar to those employed in T. brockii ADH.

The high thermostability of the A. pernix ADH, with a half-life for activity of over two hours at 90 °C, makes it an ideal catalyst for commercial biotransformations. Further characterisation of the thermal properties of the enzyme structure is being carried out using circular dichroism spectroscopy.

Materials and Methods

Crystallisation and data collection

The A. pernix ADH enzyme was overexpressed in E. coli, purified and crystallised as described.” Crystals were grown using the sitting-drop technique. The reservoir solution contained 100 mM Pipes (pH 6.75), 13-16% (w/v) PEG 600 and 0.5 mM NADH. Droplets contained 2 |.Ll of protein at 10 mg ml-l mixed with 2 p.l of reservoir solution.

Crystals were harvested into 35% PEG 600 in 100 mM Pipes (pH 6.75), 0.1 M NaCl, 0.5 mM NADH. To avoid crystals being damaged by osmotic shock due to the sharp increase in the concentration of PEG, large crystals were soaked for one minute in mother liquor as above but with 25% PEG 600, prior to transfer into the cryogenic liquor.
