Deactivation 0f the less stable subunit upon immobiliza- tion to CNBr-Sepharose offers further insights into pos- sible differences between E1 and E2. Immobilization to CNBr-Sepharose occurs via pendent amino groups, i.e., lysine residues, which are typically localized near the sur- face of the protein. Presumably, E2 has a lysine residue near its surface that couples to the support, resulting in im- mobilized enzyme that cannot bind OPSL or NADJ’ (OPSL is a competitive inhibitor of the coenzyrne NAD+)-26 It is possible that E1 does not have such a lysine, and thus remains active upon immobilization to CNBr-Sepharose. However, that lysine modification by chemical reagents did not influence enzyme stability indicates that it is not lysine modification per se that inactivates E2, but rather attachment to CNBr-Sepharose such that the coenzyrne binding site can no longer bind NAD+ (possibly due to steric interference from the support surface).

In all, the data obtained in this work provide several bases for distinguishing two major types of LADH sub- units. Of these bases, the difference in thermostability is the most dramatic. Moreover, the stability results are in some ways consistent with trends observed in previous studies of protein structure and stability. For example, the spectral simulations indicate that the motion of SL-l is much slower in the active site of E1, the more stable type of subunit. Slower motion implies a more restrictive envi- ronment, which can be explained by a more compact pack- ing of amino acid residues in the active-site domain of E1. Interestingly, more densely packed interiors have been pro- posed to contribute to the higher stabilities of proteins from thermophilic bacteriaf and the SL-1 results support the theory that a higher amino-acid density can result in higher protein thermostability.

Possible relationships between EPR data and therrnosta- bility must be viewed with some caution, however, as evi- denced by results obtained for soluble and Octyl-Sepharose LADH. In particular, spectra 5(A) and 5(C) and the lower deactivation trajectory of Figure 4(a) demonstrate that dif- ferences in the EPR spectra of spin-labeled proteins, which can reflect subtle as well as major dissimilarities in protein structure, do not ensure different protein thermostabilities. This finding is not surprising. On the other hand, in both soluble and immobilized form, E1 and E2 exhibit differences in their EPR spectra consistent with a structural trait believed to contribute to greater stability. Unfortunately, neither the generality of this result, nor the general utility of EPR spectroscopy as a sensitive probe of structure-stability rela- tionships, can be ascertained from the limited database currently available.

It must also be bourn in mind that the structural inforrna- tion most readily available from spin-labeling studies, i.e., the gross geometry of the labeling site, is not necessarily relevant to any of the known mechanisms of irreversible pro- tein denaturation.“ As pointed out by Zale and Klibanov,” a correlation between reversible thermal unfolding and irre- versible thermoinactivation exists only at inactivation tem- peratures near or below the melting point of the protein. The transition temperature of LADH at pH 8.1 was found

by Koch—Schmidt and Mosbach to be 82.5°C;36 thus, a higher internal packing density, which has been proposed to stabilize proteins against reversible unfolding, would also be expected to stabilize LADH against irreversible in- activation at 60°C.

Finally, a few comments on the independent deactivation behavior of the individual subunits are appropriate. Since LADH is active in dimeric form only, it is interesting and perhaps surprising that, based on the available data, the in- activation at 60°C of the E1E2 isozyme occurs as if E1 and E2 were completely independent. This result implies that denaturation of the less stable subunit (E2) occurs prior to dissociation of the dimer, and that the residual dimer re- tains partial activity until the E1 subunit unfolds.

In conclusion, although comprehensive studies of protein therrnostability require sophisticated analyses of proteins from thermophilic and mesophilic sources, this work demon- strates that relatively simple procedures, e.g., spin labeling and immobilization, can be very useful in interpreting de- activation data and in probing the mechanisms of enzyme stabilization. This methodology should therefore be con- sidered in future efforts to elucidate the structural charac- terisitics that distinguish stable from less stable proteins.
