Multifunctionality of Liver Alcohol Dehydrogenase: Kinetic and Mechanistic Studies of Esterase Reaction

C. S. TSAI

Department of Chemistry and Institute of Biochemistry, Carleton University, Ottawa K 1S 5B6, Canada.

Received May 19, 1981; and in revised form June 18, 1981

Alcohol dehydrogenase from horse liver is shown to catalyze ester hydrolysis. Nico- tinamide coenzymes do not affect the rate of esterolysis. A kinetic approach to study esterase reaction at 10w substrate to enzyme ratio is described. Kinetic effects of ester structure, temperature, pH, solvent polarity, and ionic strength were investigated. The liver enzyme enhances the rate of esterolysis by lowering activation energy of reaction according to the Uni-Bi kinetic sequence. Two ionizable groups, cysteine and lysine, are tentatively assigned at the esterolytic site of liver alcohol dehydrogenase from pH-rate proﬁles and chemical modiﬁcation studies. A plausible mechanism for the esterase re- action proceeds via the acid-assisted nucleophilic catalysis involving the ammonium ion of lysine and the thiolate of cysteine in the acyl-oxygen cleavage.

Liver alcohol dehydrogenase (alcohol: NAD+ oxidoreductase, EC 1.1.1.1.) (ADH)1 is characterized by a broad substrate spec- iﬁcity catalyzing the reversible oxidation of various primary and secondary alcohols (1). In addition, the enzyme is shown to exhibit multifunctional activities (2) me- diating oxidation of aldehydes (3, 4) and hydrolysis of esters (5). Multifunctional proteins have been the topics of recent reviews (6, 7) and monograph (8). Their studies have improved our understanding of enzyme architectures and functions. However, little attention has been focused on mechanistic basis of multifunctional reactions. The characterization of this as- pect of liver alcohol dehydrogenase-cata- lyzed reactions is particularly important because of its physiological function in ethanol metabolism (9) and its use as a model for investigating pharmacological activities of some metabolites and drugs which are capable of affecting ethanol ox- idation (10, 11).
