The function of the ionizable groups in the catalytic reaction is still unclear and has been explained by three models, known according to the assumed proton-shufﬂing group as Tyrosine-, Lysine-, or Serine mechanisms. To promote hydride transfer from the substrate to the NAD+ molecule, one residue of the catalytic triad (the group showing a pK of 7.1 in the E.NAD+ complex) has to abstract a proton from the Ca hydroxyl group of the substrate to generate an alcoholate anion. On the basis of its position in the active site and the pH dependence of alcohol binding, it has been proposed that this residue is the strictly conserved Tyr151.2’7_9 This hypothesis has been recently challenged by a measurement of the tempera- ture dependence of the pK value of the group that regu- lates the koii velocity for alcohols.” It was found that the ionization enthalpy of this group is rather high (18 kcal/ mol), suggesting that the strong base was more likely Ser138 in the catalytic triad.

In this work, we present an analysis of the ionization behavior of the groups in the active site of Drosophila lebanonensis ADH. Electrostatic interactions were calcu- lated on the basis of a continuum dielectric model and a computational algorithm, which explores the probability of hydrogen atoms to occupy different positions on the titrat- able and polar groups. The calculations showed that the ionization of the pair Tyr151-Lys155 is irregular and that it determines the ionization behavior of the active site. We propose a proton relay mechanism based on the calcula- tions and structural considerations.

METHODS Theoretical Basis

Computation of protonation/deprotonation equilibria of titratable groups of Drosophila lebanonensis ADH is based on the theoretical considerations described earlier.11’12 Here, only some key points relevant for the discussion of the results are presented. The protein molecule is repre- sented by a continuum material with low dielectric con- stant immersed in the high permittivity medium of the solvent. The shape of the low dielectric region and the charge distribution are determined by the coordinates of the protein atoms, their van der Waals radii, and their partial charges. The linearized Poisson-Boltzmann equa- tion is solved for this system by means of the ﬁnite difference method.13_15 The molecular details in this ap- proach are approximated by a few parameters, such as the dielectric boundary and atomic partial charges. The small number of parameters, as well as the conceptual simplicity of the model makes it an attractive, and practically the most used, instrument for analysis of electrostatic interac- tions in proteins. One of the limitations of the approach is that the protein molecule is assumed to have a ﬁxed structure, for instance, that determined by X-ray diffrac- tion experiments. No conformational ﬂexibility or possible pH-induced changes of the protein-structure are taken into account.
