pH Dependence of the Hydrogen Atom Populations in the Active Site Hydrogen Bond Network

The calculations show that the water molecule in the active site (W144) preferably acts as a proton donor in the hydrogen bond with Tyr151 hydroxyl group and a proton acceptor with Ser138 (Fig. 1). The population of this orientation is about 0.8 in both subunits. The orientations at which W144 is proton acceptor or proton donor to both Ser138 and Tyr151 share the rest of the population. At pH < 6, the latter two orientations are almost equally populated, whereas the population of the reference state decreases to zero at pH > 10. This is in quantitative agreement with the pH dependence of the Ser138 rotamer populations, where at high pH values the hydroxyl group of this residue tends to act as a proton acceptor. The difference between the populations of the different states in the two subunits is < 0.1, and in both subunits, the preferred orientations of W144 and the Ser183 hydroxyl group are identical [Fig 1(B) and (C)]. The orientation of the hydrogen atom from the NADT ribose O2’ hydroxyl group shows a strong pH dependence (Fig. 4). At low pH, the orientation at which this group does not serve as proton donor is preferably populated [Fig. 1(B)]. With increasing pH, the occupancy of this orientation decreases and ﬁnally vanishes at pH > 10. At low pH (Lys155 is protonated), the population of the orientation at which the NADT ribose hydroxyl group is a proton donor to Lys155 e-amino group is zero and in- creases with increasing pH (i.e., with increasing degree of deprotonation of Lys155).

The geometry of the active site hydrogen bonds indicates that the hydroxyl oxygen of Tyr151 can be a good hydrogen donor to the NADT ribose O2’ hydroxyl group. The latter readily donates its hydrogen to both On of Tyr151 and NZ, of the deprotonated form of Lys 155. The e-amino group of Lys155, however, serves as donor rather to the NADT ribose O3’ than to the NADT ribose O2’ hydroxyl group, whereas for O3’ there is no appropriate proton acceptor. The computations revealed a strong pH dependence of the orientation of the hydrogen atom from the NADT ribose O2’ hydroxyl group (Fig. 3). At pH < 6, the ribose NADT ribose O2’ hydroxyl group is proton acceptor in the hydro- gen bond to Tyr151. The orientation at which the hydroxyl hydrogen is distant from both hydrogen bond partners is most populated. Accordingly, Tyr151 is preferably proton donor to the ribose hydroxyl (population 0.75) and proton acceptor in the hydrogen bond with W144.
