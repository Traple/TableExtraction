Kinetics of thermal aggregation of yeast alcohol dehydrogenase I (yADH) have been studied using dynamic light scattering at a ﬁxed temperature (56 °C) and under the conditions where the temperature was elevated at a constant rate (1 K/min). The initial parts of the dependences of the hydrodynamic radius on time (or temperature) follow the exponential law. At rather high values of time splitting of the population of aggregates into two components occurs. It is assumed that such peculiarities of the kinetics of thermal aggregation of yADH are due to the presence of a sequence —YSGVCHTDLHAWHGDWPLPVK— in the polypeptide chain possessing chaperone-like activity. Thermodynamic parameters for thermal denaturation of yADH have been calculated from the differential scanning calorimetry data.

1. Introduction

Yeast alcohol dehydrogenase I (yADH; alcohol:NAD* oxidoreduc- tase, EC 1.1.1.1), a tetrameric enzyme with molecular mass of 150 kDa [1,2], consists of identical subunits containing two zinc ions per subunit, one in the active site (catalytic zinc) [1] and one in a loop (conformational zinc) [3,4]. It has been indicated that the subunits of yADH have structure similar to that of horse liver ADH, which is a dimer also containing two zinc atoms per subunit [5-7].

In yADH one zinc atom in the catalytic site is bound to three ligands (Cys-46, His-67 and Cys-174). Sequence similarity around Cys-174 is notable for all members of the alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) family. The presence of conformational zinc helps keep the conformation of the active site in a strained state [8]. The position of conformational Zn” is known for horse liver ADH, where it is bound to four cysteine residues (97, 100, 103 and 111). These four cysteine residues are present in yADH as well, and it is supposed that the zinc atom is also bound to these four cysteines [9,10]. The conformational zinc is suggested to play an important role in stabilization of the conforma- tion of yADH [4,11]. There is evidence that the active site becomes more ﬂexible after the conformational zinc is removed [12,13].

Incubation of the enzyme with dithiothreitol is accompanied by the reduction of four disulphide bridges per enzyme and chelation of the structural zinc atom without any effect on the zinc located in the active site [13,14]. The enzyme activity was not affected, but the enzyme became very sensitive to heat denaturation [4,11]. Analysis of native and chimeric yADHs suggested that unstable yADHs contained an inter-subunit electrostatic repulsion between Glu-101 and Asp-236 [14,15]. It has been suggested that electrostatic repulsion has a strong effect on thermostability.

Changes in the CD spectra in the course of heating at 6O °C indicate that the secondary structure of the enzyme molecule is slightly dependent on temperature [16]. The unfolding courses of yADH during thermal denaturation followed by changes in the UV difference absorbance and intrinsic ﬂuorescence have demonstrated that yADH unfolding during denaturation at 52 and at 56 °C is a biphasic kinetic process, which consists of two ﬁrst-order reactions.

Although yADH is crystallized [17,18], the three-dimensional structure of the enzyme is still not available. Though the general subunit conformation of yADH is largely identical to that of horse liver dimeric enzyme, they are only distantly homologous and with only 25% of all residues conserved [7]. It has been suggested that at least part of subunit contacts observed in horse liver ADH are located at homologous positions in yeast ADHs. In contrast to comparison of yADH with dimeric liver ADH, yADH shows 36% homology to tetrameric Escherichia coli ADH [19].
