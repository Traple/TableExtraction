By comparing Figures 2(A) and 2(B), it is clear that oz-CyD caused an onset temperature increase of ~ I0°C for the aggregation and the loss of oz-helix, which are probably related to the thermal stabilization. For this reason, the midpoint temperatures (Tm) of the unfolding and aggregation were calculated from Figures I and 2 and are summarized in Table I. By comparing the Tm values with respect to fluorescence (due to the three-dimensional native conformational changes) with the Tm values with respect to the dynode voltage (due to the aggregates), it is seen that these Tm values are close to each other in both the absence and presence of oe-CyD, conﬁrming the co- occurrence of the two processes. As a consequence, the irreversible thermal unfolding of ADH is strongly dependent on these two phenomena (eqn 4).

oz-CyD is well known as an artiﬁcial chaperone and has been used for many years for this purpose in vitro protein refolding. However, there is no information on its effect on stability. Here we described such effects, based on a study of the aggregation of ADH. It seems that the oe-CyD interaction with the accessible hydrophobic ADH side chains causes a decline in aggregation and leads to protein stabilization. Figure 3 illustrates that the thermal stress (50°C) causes the aggregation of ADH. In this Figure, there are at least three phases, including the lag time for aggregation (the pre-nucleation, phase I); the nucleation and the acceleration of the aggregation (see the slope of the curves, phase II); and the saturation phase (phase III). The growth phase is a fast step, which probably represents the build-up of aggregates that develop on a pre-nucleus from the ﬁrst phase. Three major factors can affect this process: high propensity to convert from the oz-helix into the ,8-sheet structure, low net charge and high hydrophobicity [45].
