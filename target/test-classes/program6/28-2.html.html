3. Results 3.1. Thermal denaturation of yADH

The ﬁrst stage of thermal aggregation of yADH is unfolding of the protein molecule. Denaturation of yADH at elevated temperatures was studied by DSC. The thermal denaturation of yADH proved to be fully irreversible, and the heat sorption curve was represented by the main thermal transition at 63 °C with a broad low-temperature shoulder at 40-55 °C (Fig. 1, curve 1). The appearance of such a shoulder can be due to the presence of some labile fraction in the yADH preparation. This shoulder may represent, for example, the enzyme that loses the structural zinc atom. To check this assumption, we have studied by DSC the yADH preparation preincubated with 2 mM dithiothreitol for 3O min at 5 °C, the treatment leading to chelation of the structural zinc atom without any effect on the zinc located in the active site [13,14]. After this treatment, a pronounced thermal transition with maximum at 48 °C appears on the thermogram instead of the shoulder (Fig. 1, curve 2), and the appearance of this new transition is accompanied by the decrease in the amplitude of the main transition. This suggests that the new low-temperature transition, as well as the shoulder in the initial yADH preparation, may correspond to the thermal unfolding of either some portion of unstable molecules in the yADP preparation or the presence of unstable domains in the protein molecules. To decide between these two possibilities, we have performed the following experiments. yADH was heated in the calorimeter cell up to 52 °C, then it was cooled down and again heated to 8O °C. (In some respect, this procedure is similar to the so-called “successive annealing”, which was successfully used in earlier studies to reveal separate calorimetric domains in the isolated myosin head [38], i.e. the regions of the protein molecule that unfold cooperatively and independently of the other regions). Upon the second heating, the low-temperature transition fully disappeared and only the high-temperature transition was observed on the thermogram (Fig. 1, curve 3), thus indicating fully irreversible denaturation of the protein presented by the low- temperature peak. This denaturation was accompanied by protein aggregation. In following experiments, yADH was heated to 52 °C and, after cooling, the protein was withdrawn from the calorimeter cell and subjected to centrifugation for 2O min at 20,000 ><g. Aggregated and precipitated protein was removed, while the supernatant was again studied by DSC and showed the same sharp high-temperature transition at 63 °C as in the previous experiment (Fig. 1, curve 3). This means that the low-temperature transition corresponds to the thermal unfolding of yADH molecules with low thermal stability, but not to the separate domain of the protein whose melting should result in aggregation of all yADH molecules and complete disappearance of the thermal transitions on the DSC proﬁle.

The presence of unstable molecules in the yADH preparation made such a protein preparation absolutely unsuitable for following studies on the mechanism of the yADH thermal aggregation, as well as the mechanism of the protective action of the agents possessing chaperone-like activity. Therefore we applied a very simple approach to remove these molecules from the original yADH preparation. The yADH molecules with low thermal stability were removed by heating the yADH preparation at 52 °C for 13 min with subsequent cooling and centrifugation at 20,000 000 ><g for 20 min, as it was described above. After such a treatment, the yADH demonstrated sharp thermal transition at 63 °C on the DSC proﬁle, which was very similar to that obtained by the “successive annealing” procedure (Fig. 1, curve 3) except only a lower amplitude (by ~30%). Determination of the enzyme activity of the preheated yADH showed that preliminary heating of the yADH preparations did not affect the speciﬁc enzyme activity. The speciﬁc activities of the original and preheated prepara- tions were 153 and 160 umol NAD/min-mg of protein, respectively. Therefore, in all further experiments on yADH denaturation and aggregation we used the enzyme preparations preheated at 52 °C for 13 min.

Fig. 2 shows the temperature dependences of Cf?‘ for the preheated yADH preparation obtained at various temperature scanning rates (v=0.5, 1.0 and 2.0 K/min). The position of maximum on the DSC proﬁles was shifted from 61 to 66 °C as the temperature scanning rate was raised from 0.5 to 2.0 K/min. Temperature dependences of excess heat capacity of the preheated yADH preparation obtained at the temperature scanning rate of 1 K/min at various protein concentra- tions, from 0.5 to 4.2 mg/ml, were absolutely identical (data not shown). This means that the mechanism of yADH thermal unfolding does not contain kinetically signiﬁcant stages of reversible dissocia- tion of yADH tetramer into the oligomeric forms of lesser size (see for discussion [39,40]).
