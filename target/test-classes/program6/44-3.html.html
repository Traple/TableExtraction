The 160,000- g supernatant contained little NAD+-dependent ADH activity (Fig. 3, gray bars), despite the detection of ADH2 by the anti- L. lactis ADHE antibody in the soluble fractions (Fig. 1B, lane 2, 1D, lane 4). Otherwise, the NADP+-dependent ADH activity remained as a soluble enzyme in the 160,000-g supernatant (Fig. 3, open bars).

Considering that NADt-dependent ADH activity of E. his- tolytica was found mainly in 160,000-g pellet; this fraction was ﬁxed, and the ADH enzyme was detected by electron micros- copy with the L. lactis ADHE antibody and a gold-labeled sec- ondary antibody. As shown in Figure 2D, the antibody bound to a granular material which mainly constituted this fraction; almost no membrane vesicles were observed in this pellet (Fig. 2C, D).

One possible explanation for the ﬁnding of ADH2 in granular material in membrane fractions and in preparations with very few membrane vesicles is that ameba NAD"“-dependent ADH could have the multimeric structure of bacterial ADHE, with which it shares homology, and similar high-speed sedimentation (as shown in this study). By negative staining, many membrane vesicles (Fig. 4A, stars) were observed in the internal mem- brane fractions. Round structures ranging from 18 to 38 nm diameter were also present (Fig. 4A, thin arrows).

The 160,000-g pellet was also analyzed by negative staining. As shown in Figure 4B, rodlike helical structures (thick short arrows) were clearly observed; their length ranged from 75 to 145 nm (55 = 103 nm) with an average width of 13.7 nm. Also, abundant spherical smaller particles of 13-26 nm diameter were present (Fig. 4B, thin arrows).

DISCUSSION

Most of the alcohol dehydrogenases are soluble enzymes; in addition, mitochondrial ADHs have been reported in some fun- gi, such as Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Kluyveromyces lactis (Young and Pilgrim, 1985; Saliola and Falcone, 1995). In the present study, most NAD+-dependent ADH activity was pellet- ed at 160,000 g. This result and the negative staining analysis of this fraction demonstrate that ameba ADH2 is organized as multimers, in a structure similar to that of the ADHE from Escherichia coli (Kessler et a1., 1991) and puriﬁed E. histolytica ADH2, which however, was puriﬁed from a 150,000-g soluble fraction (Bruchhaus and Tannich, 1994). The present study clearly demonstrates that the main ADH activity sedimented in a pattern similar to ADHE from E. coli and does not behave as a membrane enzyme. Moreover, in fresh preparations, ameba ADH2 has a structure similar to that of the E. coli.
